# Spread 05 — Campaign

**Pages 11–12.**

This spread closes the case. After rarity (S1), buyer (S2), price (S3), and trust (S4), the reader needs to know the operational ladder that turns those advantages into a sold home. One funnel. One headline number. No 11-channel matrix.

The emotion this spread must produce: **trust (in execution).** "This is engineered, not improvised."

V2 showed channel matrices, geographic heat maps, and persona-by-channel cadence tables. V3 collapses all of that into a single funnel and a single closing claim. The reader does not need to see the spreadsheet — they need to feel that the spreadsheet exists.

## Designer notes

- **The funnel is the page.** It must be visually elegant — not a "performance dashboard" feel. Clean typography. Generous spacing between rows. Copper bars on cream ground.
- **Bar widths use a non-linear scale.** A linear scale would make rows 4 and 5 invisibly small. Use square-root or log scaling. Document the scaling decision in the source line if production prefers full transparency, otherwise let the visual carry the truth without explanation.
- **The single copper dot for "1 buyer at the right price" is non-negotiable.** It is the emotional landing of the entire spread. Same diameter as the dots used on the Spread 03 positioning band (visual continuity).
- **No geographic heatmap.** V2 showed a four-city dot map. Cut. The four cities are named in the funnel's first row text — that's enough.
- **No persona-by-channel cadence table.** V2 showed an 11-row table per persona. Cut entirely.
- **No "$5K spend" or budget references.** Implementation detail. Belongs in the campaign brief, not the appraisal.

---

## What this spread must achieve

The seller must finish this page thinking: *"This is engineered."*

The funnel does the work. They see 52,000 impressions narrow to one buyer through five stages. They don't need to understand each stage — they need to feel that someone has already thought about this carefully, and that the same care will be applied to their home.

The final dot — *one buyer* — is the ending. That singularity is the point. Premium prices are paid by a single person, and the campaign exists to find that person. A V2-style "audience size" claim does the opposite — it dilutes by expansion.

V3 closes by inverting V2's emphasis. The headline is not how big the audience is; the headline is how precisely we narrow.

## Data sources for production

- **Funnel numbers** carry from V2's Spread 04 (52,000 impressions / 3,800 click-throughs / 480 editorial views / 95 deep engagements / 45 inspections / 1 buyer). These were derived from FB Custom Audience benchmarks, industry CPMs, and Fields' observed 4.10% CTR.
- Verify each number at production time against the latest 90-day Fields ecosystem performance. Round all numbers to no more than 2 significant figures (e.g. ~52,000 not 51,847).
- The "28-day campaign window" is the standard Fields campaign default. Don't change for this report unless the campaign brief specifies otherwise.
