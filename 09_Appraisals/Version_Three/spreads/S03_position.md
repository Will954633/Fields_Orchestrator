# Spread 03 — Position

**Pages 07–08.**

This spread replaces V2's three pricing-related spreads (S07 Valuation Engine + parts of S02 Scarcity demand-side + parts of S08 Suburb Intel). The pricing argument is reframed: the home is not being valued against the market; it is being positioned against the small set of homes a premium buyer will actually choose between.

The emotion this spread must produce: **confidence (in pricing).** "These people understand pricing better than anyone."

V2's pricing argument felt mathematical (weighted means, 90% confidence intervals, adjustment tables). That kind of complexity invites scrutiny rather than building trust. V3's pricing argument is smaller, calmer, and more defensible — *because* it is simpler.

## Designer notes

- **No adjustment tables.** No $/m² calculations. No 90% confidence interval. No weighted means. The full V2 valuation engine spread (Spread 07) is replaced by these five lines and one band.
- **The numbers are written in full — `$1,850,000 – $2,050,000`** — never `$1.85M`. Per the editorial style guide.
- **The recommended range is "buyer interest range," not "valuation."** This is deliberate. "Valuation" invites scrutiny ("how do you know it's worth that?"). "Buyer interest range" is honest — it is the range at which buyer competition is engineered to occur. It is also the language sellers find easiest to act on.
- **Three competitor cards is non-negotiable.** Not two, not four. Three is the cognitive sweet spot for shortlists. If V2's competitor map produced more or fewer at production, select the three with the strongest feature overlap.
- **No map.** V2's map of competitors was visually busy and did little work. The card-based hero communicates the competitive set more cleanly.
- **The positioning band is the only chart on this page.** It must be elegant, not analytical. Single axis. Two dots. One band. White space around it.

---

## What this spread must achieve

The seller must finish this page thinking: *"These people understand pricing."*

Not: *"That's a sophisticated valuation methodology."*

The difference is critical. Sellers are emotionally terrified of:
- under-pricing
- over-pricing
- sitting stale
- choosing wrong

V2's response to that terror was to show *more math*. V3's response is to show *more clarity*. Three competitors. One band. Five reasons. One range. Done.

The implicit promise: *we know exactly where this home should sit, and we can defend it without complexity.*

That is what calm authority looks like.
