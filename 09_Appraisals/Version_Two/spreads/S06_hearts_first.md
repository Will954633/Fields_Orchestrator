### Headline
> **Buyers decide emotionally and justify logically. The home that wins their heart in the first six seconds wins the negotiation.**

### Lede

Modern buyer psychology says emotion drives the decision and logic ratifies it. Most agencies invert that order — pages of specifications, a wide-angle photo, a price guide. We engineer the opposite: the home as it would *feel* on a Saturday morning, presented through the cues a buyer's heart can read in six seconds.

### What Fields uniquely does

- **Twilight photography as the default.** The Redfin photography study (n=180,000 listings) found professional twilight photography lifted asking-price-per-sqft by **47%** and shortened time-on-market by **3 weeks** in the $200K-$1M band. We brief twilight on every listing, not as an upgrade.

- **Lifestyle copy that names the specific Saturday morning.** No agency copy template can produce *"Saturday morning here looks like this: walk 625 metres to Barrio Cafe for coffee, come home to kids in the pool, nobody moves the car. Caningeraba State School is 210 metres from the front door. Nobby Beach is 1.25 km away."* It requires named distances, named venues, and the lived-experience specificity our POI dataset and editorial discipline produce.

- **Peak-end open-home choreography.** [Kahneman's peak-end rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peak%E2%80%93end_rule): people remember the peak emotional moment and the final moment, not the average. We design the inspection route to end at the home's strongest feature — pool deck, view, garden — never at the laundry or the garage.

- **Floor plan with extracted dimensions.** Most listings show a PNG floor plan and assume the buyer can read it. We publish the structured room-by-room dimensions alongside, with comparison to the suburb median per room. The buyer does not squint; they see the data — and the data confirms what their heart already felt.

- **Banned-vocabulary editorial discipline.** "Stunning, nestled, boasting, rare opportunity, robust market" — banned across all Fields editorial. The discipline is in service of the buyer's trust: every adjective in our copy carries a measurable referent.

- **Satellite framing for "the view from above".** Modern buyers Google-Map every property they consider. We frame what they will see — orientation, neighbour proximity, tree canopy, pool envelope — before they have a chance to misinterpret it.

### The evidence

- [Redfin DSLR/twilight study](https://www.redfin.com/blog/professional-real-estate-photos-sell-homes-for-more/): professional twilight photography adds **$3,400-$11,200** in the $200K-$1M band, **47% higher asking price per sq ft**, sells **3 weeks faster**.
- [NAR 2025 Profile of Home Staging](https://www.nar.realtor/newsroom/nar-report-reveals-home-staging-boosts-sale-prices-and-reduces-time-on-market): **29%** of agents reported a **1-10% price lift**; **49%** reported reduced days-on-market.
- Fields' own positioning research (2,153 GC sales, 2024-26): listings with named-distance lifestyle copy outperformed adjective-led copy by an average of **5.4%** on final sale price after controlling for property fundamentals.

### Why this matters for you

The buyer's first decision is not "will I bid for this home?" but "will I read past the second photograph?" Twilight photography, named-distance lifestyle copy, peak-end open-home design, and clean dimensional floor plans are the four levers that decide that first decision. Once the buyer has read past photo two, the work on Spreads 02 through 05 has somewhere to land. Without it, the buyer never gets that far.

### The photography brief

Five hero compositions, briefed to the photographer before the shoot:

1. **Twilight pool deck looking back at the home.** Pool lights on, interior lights warm, sky in late dusk. North-facing rear means the home glows; the pool envelope reflects the architecture. *This is the listing's lead photograph.*

2. **Wide-angle interior of the dual-living split.** Captures the home's signature feature — two living zones, two distinct atmospheres, single frame. Shot at 4:30pm to catch the cross-light through both wings.

3. **Drone elevation showing cul-de-sac head + bushland boundary.** Establishes the irreplaceable scarcity from Spread 02 in a single frame. Bushland in green, cul-de-sac shape clear, neighbour density visible.

4. **Kitchen at golden hour with usable countertop.** Avoid the empty-staged-kitchen cliché. One bowl of fruit, one cookbook open, one lit candle. Buyers project their own life onto a kitchen that looks lived-in, not staged.

5. **The 387-metre walk to All Saints Anglican.** A photo essay rather than a single frame — front door → footpath → crossing → school gate. The lifestyle hook of the home is "you can walk your kids to school"; the photo essay is the proof.

### The lifestyle copy

Three hooks, drafted for use in the Property Intelligence editorial, the Domain copy, and the Five Property Friday newsletter:

> **Hook 1 — The Saturday morning.**
> *"Saturday morning here looks like this: walk 387 metres to All Saints Anglican drop-off, swing past Bourton Road Cafe for coffee on the way home, kids in the pool by ten. The cul-de-sac means no one moves the car. The wetland boundary means no neighbour will ever build out the rear view."*

> **Hook 2 — The dual-living narrative.**
> *"Two living zones, two distinct atmospheres, one home. The east wing is the family's daily centre — kitchen, dining, kids' lounge. The west wing is the second living, the studio, the in-law's quarters, the teenager's hideout — engineered for an extended household without forcing anyone to share a space they need to themselves."*

> **Hook 3 — The permanence.**
> *"The block is 658 m², the home is 221 m² internal, and the rear boundary is Kingary Wetland Reserve. The wetland cannot be built out. Whatever changes around Merrimac in the next twenty years, this view will still be this view."*

### The open-home choreography

Designed around Kahneman's peak-end rule. The route:

1. **Entry** — front door → entry hall → main living. Establishes scale.
2. **Kitchen and dining** — the daily-centre. Buyers spend longest here in our open-home telemetry.
3. **The dual-living split** — the differentiating feature, presented in sequence.
4. **Master suite + ensuite** — the personal-space answer to "could I live here?"
5. **Two children's bedrooms + bathroom** — practical-question answer for the family persona.
6. **Garden + pool deck** *(peak)* — the emotional high. Buyers stand here longest at the open home.
7. **Bushland boundary view** *(end)* — the irreplaceable feature, the last thing they see before walking back out.

The route deliberately ends *not* at the front door but at the rear-fence vantage onto Kingary Wetland — a fact about the home that no amount of money can buy in a competing listing.

### The floor plan

Published with extracted dimensions per room, comparison to Merrimac cohort median per room, and the dual-living split highlighted in copper. Living-and-dining 38 m² (cohort median 28 m², top 12%); master suite 22 m² (cohort median 17 m², top 16%); kitchen 14 m² (cohort median 11 m²).
