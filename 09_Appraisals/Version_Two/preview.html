How This Report Was Built

What we did

Across approximately forty hours of analysis, we processed every photograph, the floor plan, the satellite tile, and the street-view frame for 13 Terrace Court; computed proximity to twenty-three named points of interest within a 1.5 km walking radius; and matched the property against forty-six Merrimac sales (last 24 months) plus 142 currently active listings across Merrimac, Robina, and Varsity Lakes. The output is the data inventory that drives every recommendation in this report.

How we computed the valuation

Six comparable sales drawn from Merrimac's last 18 months. Each line-item-adjusted to 13 Terrace Court using suburb-specific adjustment rates derived from the 46-sale local cohort. The reconciled value is a weighted mean (recency × proximity × condition-confidence). The 90% confidence interval is computed as 1.645 × weighted_std_dev. Confidence level (Medium-High) reflects the agreement among the six comparables. Full working is shown on Spread 07.

How we sized the buyer pool

Realestate.com.au and Domain.com.au monthly search-volume data for the suburb-and-bracket combination, supplemented by Fields ecosystem engagement telemetry from the last 90 days. Persona-share estimates are derived from observed buyer flow and demographic micro-data from the ABS 2026 census release. Estimated qualified-and-active buyers in the next 30 days: ~380. Full working on Spread 04.

What we deliberately did not model

Interior smells, recent build defects, neighbour disputes — not visible to remote analysis. An in-person inspection by Will is the standard next step. Buyer financing position is known only at the offer stage. Macroeconomic shocks are flagged in the daily campaign briefing but cannot be priced in advance.

What you can audit

Every claim in this report can be traced back to one of three places: a comparable sale in our Gold_Coast.merrimac collection, a metric in system_monitor.precomputed_market_charts, or a citation to a published study. If a sentence cannot be traced, it does not appear. If you find a claim that does not stand up, write to Will and we will fix it and reissue the report.

We see your home in more dimensions than any other agency in Australia.

Most agencies look at three things: bedrooms, bathrooms, lot size. We process every photograph, every centimetre of the floor plan, every metre of the satellite image, every street-view frame, and every named point of interest within walking distance. Then we cross-reference what we find against thousands of recent sales and every active listing in the southern Gold Coast.

What Fields uniquely does
  • Floor plan analysis. Every room dimensioned, layout flow scored, dual-living potential mapped, indoor-to-outdoor area ratios computed. Most agents work from a PNG; we work from the structured data underneath it.
  • Photo-by-photo condition scoring. Each interior photograph passed through a multimodal vision model: kitchen condition, bathroom finish, materials called by name, dated-vs-renovated assessed at the room level rather than the property level.
  • Satellite analysis. Orientation, neighbour proximity, tree canopy coverage, pool envelope, north-facing yard fraction, bushland-vs-arterial-road adjacency. The view from above is the first view a serious buyer takes.
  • Street-view scoring. Front presentation, set-back, kerb appeal, traffic-noise risk, visible road network. The first-five-second impression that decides whether a buyer opens the floor plan.
  • POI walking-distance ledger. Every school, park, cafe, supermarket, and public-transport stop within 1.5 km, measured by walking route — not the Euclidean shortcut every other proximity tool uses.
  • Combinatorial comparable matching. Once your home's attributes are extracted, we run them as a query against ~270 active listings and ~2,000 sold transactions to surface what is rare, what is in demand, and what has historically commanded a premium.
The evidence
Across the southern Gold Coast we hold approximately 40,000 cadastral records, 270 active listings, and 2,075 sold transactions, refreshed nightly. Every active property is run through our analysis pipeline as it appears on the market. No other agency operating in our suburbs maintains this layer of data on every property.

02

Scarcity, when it is real, is the single most reliable lever in property marketing. Most agencies don't measure it.

In a market where buyers are comparing your home against 41 other Merrimac listings on a phone screen, what makes yours different has to be true, specific, and defensible. We compute it. The competitors describe it.

What Fields uniquely does
  • Combinatorial inventory queries. "How many homes currently for sale in Merrimac have five-or-more bedrooms, a pool, and a north-facing rear?" Most data tools cannot answer that question because the underlying data has never been extracted at the right granularity. We extract it nightly.
  • Cross-cohort scarcity matching. A claim is only meaningful in context. We measure your home's combination of features against (a) every active listing in the suburb, (b) every active listing across the southern Gold Coast, and (c) every sold property in the last 24 months.
  • Dimensional rarity. Most listings show floor plans. Few report room dimensions. Almost none show how those dimensions sit against the suburb cohort. We do.
  • Demand-side scarcity from sold cohort. A feature is only valuable if buyers have paid for it. We compute, from suburb sold history, which feature combinations achieved the largest premium over suburb median in the last 12 months.
  • Walking-distance monopoly. Where applicable: "Yours is the only home currently for sale within a 5-minute walk of [named school / park / cafe / station]." Computed against our POI dataset and refreshed nightly.
  • Editorial discipline against fake urgency. Manufactured scarcity backfires in premium markets — our research shows Burleigh Waters listings with urgency framing took 33 days to sell vs 20.5 days with factual framing. We say one of two, not "rare opportunity".
The evidence
Cialdini's scarcity principle is the most reliably price-positive of the persuasion principles — but only when the scarcity is real. In our 2,153-property positioning study, listings making verified scarcity claims outperformed listings making generic claims by 5-9% in final sale price across the southern Gold Coast cohort. Manufactured urgency was negatively correlated with price in premium suburbs.
"There are currently only 2 five-bedroom houses with a pool for sale across all of Robina — out of 55 total active listings. This property is one of them." — Sample scarcity headline, Fields combinatorial query engine, 2026-05-07
Most listings open with "stunning beachside opportunity nestled in a sought-after locale" — and the buyer reads past it, because every listing in the suburb says the same thing. Verified, numerical scarcity stops the scroll. It also survives the partner-audit moment — "this one has X, only two in the suburb do." That is the moment the listing changes price-band defaults in the buyer's head.

03

Every home has a buyer for whom it is the right home. The price is what that buyer is willing to pay.

The premium price for any property is paid by the buyer for whom the home's specific combination of features is exactly what they were looking for. Generic marketing reaches generic buyers, who pay generic prices. We map the home to the person.

What Fields uniquely does
  • Scarcity-to-archetype matching. Each rare feature combination identified on Spread 02 is matched to a buyer archetype with established preferences for those features. The match is data-led, drawn from search-behaviour patterns, demographic micro-data, and our buyer-engagement telemetry.
  • Suburb-specific persona refinement. A "multi-generational family" buyer in Merrimac is a different person from one in Bondi or Mornington. We refine archetypes against local demographic patterns, school-catchment income data, and observed buyer flow at Fields-hosted open homes.
  • Price-band willingness mapping. Personas are not just descriptive — each is anchored to a willingness-to-pay range supported by the historical sold-cohort premium for the features they value.
  • Three personas, ranked by likelihood and value. We never present a single buyer because a single buyer is a marketing risk. Three personas — primary, secondary, tertiary — each with their own targeting plan.
  • Anti-fit naming. A persona is only useful if its boundary is also defined. We name explicitly which buyers this home is not for. That allows the campaign to remove low-conversion targeting that would otherwise waste budget.
The evidence
Specificity bias (Tversky & Kahneman) and the Identifiable Victim Effect (Schelling, Slovic): a named, specific persona generates more inferential commitment from the reader than a generic category. In direct-response marketing, persona-specific creative outperforms broad-audience creative by 2.5-4× measured by cost-per-qualified-lead.
"Saturday morning here looks like this: walk 625 metres to Barrio Cafe for coffee, come home to kids in the pool, nobody moves the car." — Sample lifestyle hook, Fields editorial — works because the persona it names is specific enough to recognise themselves in it
Without persona mapping, your home's listing copy is written for "everyone who might buy it" — which is no one. The buyer who would pay your home's true premium scrolls past, because nothing in the listing tells them this is the home they have been looking for. Persona-mapped marketing reverses the default — the right buyer feels seen, the wrong buyer self-deselects.
