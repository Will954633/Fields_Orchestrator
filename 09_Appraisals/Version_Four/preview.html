Valuation — The single most important factor in sale outcome.

The valuation is the foundation.

If the valuation is wrong, the campaign is built from the wrong starting point.

Price too low, and you risk transferring value from the seller to the buyer. Price too high, and you risk weakening competition before it begins: the listing ages, buyers become sceptical, and momentum is lost.

The valuation is not the ceiling. It is the foundation from which the premium is earned — through positioning, presentation, campaign reach and negotiation.

04

Premium pricing requires reaching the buyer who isn't currently searching.

Most marketing reaches one kind of buyer: the active buyer — those already searching the portals today. That is half the buyer market, at most.

The other half is the passive buyer — the buyer who matches the avatar but isn't searching this week. They are bigger in number. They are also the price mechanism: premium prices come from competition, and competition requires two or more passionate buyers in the room. Reaching only the active half of the market reaches only half the possible competition. The cost of the missing passionate buyer is paid by the seller.

For 13 Terrace Court, Fields targets the buyer avatars from Section 02 across both states — wherever their attention actually sits, whether they are searching today or not.

Premium prices come from competition between two or more passionate buyers. Reaching only the active half of the market reaches only half the possible competition. The passive buyer Fields finds is the second person in the room — and the reason your home sells above the cohort, not at it.
Portals capture demand that already exists. Fields creates and redirects demand.

06

Buyers discount what they cannot verify.

Transparency builds confidence. Confidence protects the premium.

A buyer at a Gold Coast inspection is making one of the largest financial decisions of their life. They have four things to work with: the asking price, the agent's word, what they can see, and what they can verify for themselves.

When buyers cannot verify the price, the comparable sales or the trade-offs, they protect themselves in the only place they can: the offer. That uncertainty becomes a discount.

Fields was built around data, analysis and transparency because confidence is commercial. A buyer who understands the price, the evidence and the trade-offs is less likely to hedge against what they cannot see.
