# Competitive Audit — What We Are Beating

This document is a structured benchmark of the agencies a Gold Coast homeowner is most likely to receive an appraisal from, plus the international best-in-class who define the absolute ceiling. Every weakness identified is a Fields opportunity.

Sources: agent websites, sample reports, vendor packs, marketing materials, industry analyses (cited inline). Compiled 2026-05-06.

---

## TL;DR — the unguarded position

The Australian premium-agency category is mature enough that **all incumbents look the same**:
- Same data source (CoreLogic / Pricefinder)
- Same structure (agent bio → market overview → comps → fee → marketing plan)
- Same deliverable (branded PDF, sometimes printed, ~12–30 pages)
- **Zero original property-level analysis published.** None show their reasoning. None embed video. None run as a living document. None acknowledge the uncertainty in their own numbers.

International leaders win on one of three axes:
- **Data rigour** — Knight Frank / Savills (UK)
- **Story** — The Modern House / Inigo (UK)
- **Platform** — Compass / Compass One (US)

**Nobody combines all three.** Fields can. Doing so creates a category-of-one report.

## 1. Australian incumbents — the field a Gold Coast seller compares us to

### 1.1 Ray White (market-share leader)
**Product 1 — instant digital "Property Report".** Pricefinder data piped into a templated PDF. Comparable sales, suburb median, 10-year price change, average DOM, auction clearance rate. ([Aspley sample](https://raywhiteaspley.com.au/property-report/property-report-digital-appraisal))

**Product 2 — in-person sales appraisal.** Local agent visit, verbal price guide, printed comp sheets.

**Distinguishing feature:** speed, scale, brand ubiquity.
**Weakness #1:** the digital product is a white-labelled CoreLogic export. Every Gold Coast Ray White office sends the same PDF; so does every other franchise that licenses Pricefinder. **The seller cannot distinguish between agencies based on the document.**
**Weakness #2:** the in-person appraisal lives or dies on the individual agent. The brand cannot transfer trust the way the agent must.

**Fields opening:** original analysis the agent themselves cannot produce. We don't compete with Ray White's brand; we compete on the document the seller actually compares against.

### 1.2 McGrath
**Product 1 — the [McGrath Report](https://issuu.com/mcgrathestateagents/docs/mcgrath_report_2024).** 100+ page suburb-by-suburb forecast, published annually. A genuine vendor-attraction asset.
**Product 2 — the appraisal itself.** Conventional in-person + branded PDF leave-behind. Covers location, building condition, fixtures, accessibility.

**Distinguishing feature:** the McGrath Report builds authority before the agent walks in.
**Weakness:** the McGrath Report is *national*, not personal. By the time the conversation reaches the seller's specific property, it falls back to a conventional appraisal. The pre-built asset and the property-specific asset are disconnected.

**Fields opening:** we do at the property level what McGrath does at the national level. Their authority is borrowed (national report); ours is property-specific.

### 1.3 Kollosche (Gold Coast prestige leader)
**Product:** $9bn settled, 28 years, $21M record sale of 159 Hedges Avenue. Vendor pitch is **expertise + network density**. Publishes [Prestige Property Drivers](https://www.kollosche.com.au/media-centre/prestige-property-drivers/) thought-leadership, leans on track record.

**Distinguishing feature:** the agent's reputation IS the report.
**Weakness:** opaque to vendors who haven't met Michael. The digital footprint underdelivers vs the brand. Sellers who don't already have Michael's number don't know how to evaluate Kollosche from the outside.

**Fields opening:** when Kollosche's network can't reach a seller (and most southern GC families never get that conversation), the seller looks at what the agency *publishes*. Fields can publish more about that seller's specific home than Kollosche has ever published about anyone's.

### 1.4 Harcourts Coastal
**Product:** generic franchise template, in-person delivery, marketing-strategy heavy. Free [sales appraisal request](https://propertyhub.harcourts.com.au/sales-appraisal).

**Distinguishing feature:** local market saturation.
**Weakness:** generic franchise template; no published vendor-facing IP.

### 1.5 Belle Property, LJ Hooker, Place, Eview, Amir Prestige
All run variations of the same model: agent visits, runs Pricefinder/CoreLogic comps, hand-delivers a branded PDF with a price range and a marketing schedule.

**LJ Hooker exception:** distributes a [physical pre-listing presentation box](https://trustyboxes.com.au/portfolio-item/lj-hooker-real-estate-pre-listing-presentation-box/) — the *physical object* is the differentiation, not the content. Notable because nobody else does it. **Useful reference point for our print edition packaging — we should match or beat the object quality.**

**Place / Belle:** premium Brisbane / Sydney positioning, polished marketing, but at the property level still rely on Pricefinder comps and an agent's verbal pitch.

### 1.6 The category template
Most Australian agency appraisal reports are produced from one of three sources:
- [Pricefinder CMA exports](https://help.pricefinder.com.au/hc/en-us/articles/360055271813-Create-a-CMA-report)
- [Real Estate Design Studio](https://www.realestatedesign.com.au/pre-listing-kits/) pre-built kits
- [Realtair](https://s3-ap-southeast-2.amazonaws.com/realtair-pitch-uploads/fdfeca5e-5fad-4ebe-a6a2-d68f69a74b57.pdf) digital pitch decks

The category is **unbranded at the report level**. There is no signature look that a homeowner could identify as "this is the McGrath report" or "this is the Belle report". Fields can be the first.

### 1.7 The Australian seller experience — what they actually receive
A typical southern Gold Coast homeowner who interviews three agents currently receives:
- Three printed PDFs of broadly similar 12–30 page length
- Three different agent biographies / career boasts
- Three marginally different comp sets (drawn from the same Pricefinder export with different filters)
- Three different price ranges (often varying by 10–15% with no transparent reason)
- Three different proposed marketing budgets

The seller's decision is being made on **trust signals (chemistry, charisma, who recommended whom)** — not on the document. Because the documents are all roughly equivalent, the document does no work in the decision.

This is the structural failure of the category. Our entire opportunity sits inside it.

## 2. International best-in-class — the ceiling

### 2.1 Compass (US) — the platform leader
[Compass](https://www.compass.com/) pioneered the modern CMA experience with an [AI-powered comparable-selection workflow](https://www.inman.com/2020/08/06/compass-launches-ai-powered-cma-tool-for-agents/). Side-by-side comp comparison, agent overrides AI suggestions, integrated into the [Compass One client dashboard](https://getrealchestercounty.com/blog/compass-one-client-portal-user-overview).

**What they do brilliantly:** the report is a *living URL*, not a PDF. Updates as comps land. Shareable with spouse/lawyer/accountant. Agents report up to **+40% conversion** with integrated digital CMA flows ([source](https://provenreality.com/augmented-reality-and-real-estate/)).

**Where they fall short:** the design feels like agent software exposed to the client — corporate, not editorial. The seller is treated as an end-user of a B2B tool, not as the protagonist of a story.

**Fields lift:** match the platform sophistication, beat the editorial.

### 2.2 Sotheby's International Realty — the brand leader
[Sotheby's](https://www.sothebysrealty.com/eng) leans on the [Luxury Outlook annual report](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/sothebys-international-realty-releases-2026-luxury-outlook-report-shows-luxury-residences-leading-the-years-real-estate-market-302655246.html) (data partners: JPMorgan, PwC, UBS, Henley & Partners) plus bespoke dossiers per listing. Print + digital, magazine-grade photography, editorial copy.

**What they do brilliantly:** the brand is the proof. The dossier looks like an art catalogue.
**Where they fall short:** the dossier is a **marketing brochure**, not a vendor analysis. It describes the home, doesn't analyse the decision. There is no methodology page. There is no honest-assessment page. The seller learns no more about their home from a Sotheby's dossier than from a glossy real estate magazine.

**Fields lift:** match the production quality, beat the analytical depth.

### 2.3 Knight Frank / Savills (UK) — the rigour leaders
[Knight Frank residential valuations](https://www.knightfrank.co.uk/residential/services/property-valuations) are produced by RICS-qualified surveyors with documented methodology (income capitalisation, residual appraisals, DCF) and the [Wealth Report](https://www.thebuyingsolution.co.uk/what-knight-franks-wealth-report-tells-us-about-the-premium-london-property-market/) flagship. [Savills](https://www.savills.co.uk/services/valuation/residential-valuation.aspx) runs the Prime London Index since 1979 with 50+ registered valuers.

**What they do brilliantly:** institutional rigour. This isn't an agent's opinion — it's a chartered surveyor's report. Citations, methodology, named methods.
**Where they fall short:** corporate dryness; no emotional resonance for an owner-occupier. They have turned residential valuation into accountancy. The seller does not feel the human on the other side of the document.

**Fields lift:** match the rigour, beat the warmth.

### 2.4 The Modern House / Inigo (UK) — the editorial leaders
[The Modern House](https://themodernhouse.com/) and its sister brand [Inigo](https://www.inigo.com/) — founded by ex-architecture journalists Albert Hill and Matt Gibberd — sell "Britain's finest modern architecture" via *editorial-quality* listings: long-form prose written by trained writers, large-format photography, hand-drawn floor plans, story-led property pages. [Inigo's sales list](https://www.inigo.com/sales-list) reads like a magazine.

**What they do brilliantly:** every listing is a piece of journalism. Fully edited, trained-writer prose. Architectural photography that doesn't apologise.
**Where they fall short:** they don't publish a separate seller appraisal — the listing page IS the report. It works because the homes already qualify (mid-century modernist, listed, designer-made). Replicating it for ordinary stock is harder.

**Fields lift:** match the editorial discipline, apply it to the *appraisal* (where a seller is making a decision) not just the listing (where a buyer is shopping).

### 2.5 Where each leader falls short — and why Fields can take all three positions

| Axis | Leader | What they own | What they miss |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Data rigour** | Knight Frank / Savills | Method, citations, RICS qualification | Warmth, narrative, owner-occupier emotional resonance |
| **Story** | The Modern House / Inigo | Editorial prose, photography, magazine-feel | Vendor analysis (they don't appraise; they list) |
| **Platform** | Compass | Living URL, AI-CMA, client portal | Editorial quality, narrative, design language |

**Fields' unfair advantage is owning all three.** Original Gold Coast data on every property + editorial discipline that tells each home's story honestly + technical platform to deliver it as a living document. Each axis alone is achievable; the combination is the moat.

## 3. The design canon — what "expensive" actually looks like

The competitor pattern is *real-estate fluency* — the visual codes the seller has learned to associate with "agency". Our break-out has to look unmistakably *not* like that. References:

### 3.1 Information design
- **[Edward Tufte](https://www.edwardtufte.com/)** — *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information* (1983). Graphical excellence = greatest number of ideas in shortest time with least ink.
  - **[Sparklines](https://www.edwardtufte.com/notebook/sparkline-theory-and-practice-edward-tufte/)** — word-sized data graphics inline in prose. Imagine "Median price in Burleigh Waters [↗ sparkline] +14% over 5 years" — eight characters of data, no chart break. We use this.
  - **Small multiples** — grid of identical mini-charts comparing variants. We use this for the 5–7 comparable sales as a 4×2 grid.
  - **Data-ink ratio** — every pixel earns its place. No gridlines, no decoration. Our charts strip to essentials.
- **[FT Visual Vocabulary](https://github.com/Financial-Times/chart-doctor/blob/main/visual-vocabulary/README.md)** — chart selection by intent. Use intent first, pick chart second. **John Burn-Murdoch principle: charts change minds.** We adopt the FT discipline of headlining each chart with its conclusion in the title.

### 3.2 Editorial / magazine
- **[Monocle](https://monocle.com/magazine/design-directory/1/)** — assumes an intelligent reader. Rigid grid, dense typography, premium feel through *information density*. **The Monocle bet:** trust the reader with detail. Our deep-reader layer follows this.
- **Cereal** — minimalism by restraint. Wide white space, restrained type, large photography. Our cover and section dividers borrow this restraint.
- **Apartamento** — authenticity over staged perfection. Useful tonal model: a Fields report should feel honest, not polished into meaninglessness.
- **Wallpaper\*** — large-format imagery + considered typography + editorial confidence.

### 3.3 Premium brand reports
- **Stripe / Airbnb design language** — typography does most of the work. Airbnb commissioned a custom typeface (Airbnb Cereal, Dalton Maag, 2018) because off-the-shelf type signals off-the-shelf brand. We should consider commissioning a custom display weight for Fields headlines, or selecting a non-default Google Fonts pairing that no other agency uses.
- **Spotify Wrapped** — drives ~40% YoY engagement spikes. The user is the hero, not the brand. Our microsite end-of-year recap follows this model.

### 3.4 Five design principles distinguishing "expensive" from "generic real estate"
1. **Typography earns trust before content does.** [Lindgaard 50ms study](https://www.thesigma.co/journal/halo-effect-ux): users assess credibility in 50 milliseconds. Custom or curated type signals craft.
2. **Information density signals expertise.** Premium readers respect being trusted with detail. (Monocle, FT, Tufte.)
3. **Photography is editorial, not promotional.** Inigo's listings work because the photo never apologises for the home. Stock photography is forbidden.
4. **Charts have a single message.** Burn-Murdoch's rule: every chart should headline its conclusion in the title. ("72 of every 100 buyers scroll past auction listings" not "Buyer behaviour by listing type".)
5. **No real-estate clichés.** No agent photo on the cover. No stock images. No gold borders. No serif scripts. No "Sold!" stickers. No "exceeding expectations" pull-quotes.

## 7. Concrete numbers we should use to authority-build

Pulled from research — every number traceable, all to be used in our own marketing copy *and* in the report's methodology footnotes when relevant:

- **Professional photography reduces DOM by 32%** (89 vs 123 days) and adds **$934 to $116,076** vs amateur photos in matched-pair comparisons (VHT Studios, 200,000+ listings).
- **Drone photography → 68% faster sale**; virtual tours → **31% less time on market**; **+87% inquiry likelihood**; **+403% inquiries on listings with video** (NAR data via CubiCasa).
- **Aerial visuals → 65% more in-person showings booked.**
- **Sellers using an agent earn $46,603 more on average than FSBO** (US data 2022–2023, Clever).
- **89% of sellers have regrets**; **63% considered withdrawing from market** due to open-home stress; **76% spent more money on prep than expected.** (Survey aggregates — multiple sources cited in `01_psychology_principles.md`.)
- **Off-market sales sell for 4.3% less than listed properties** (PropTrack 2024) — ~$60K Sydney, ~$30K Melbourne.
- **Trust gap:** real estate ranks near the bottom of professions in public trust (~11% in US data, declining globally per Edelman 2024–2025). **This is the floor we start from.** Every page of our report has to spend trust capital, not consume it.
- **OpenAgent 2025:** more than 1 in 3 Australians cried during their property sale.

These numbers belong in the report's methodology pages and in the marketing copy that points sellers towards Fields. They do not all belong on every property's appraisal — but having the catalogue available is part of the moat.

## 8. Red flags the audit surfaced

Things we are currently doing or risk doing that the audit suggests we should reconsider:

### 8.1 The May 6 design-style render is a content regression vs the April 10 v2 (already-shipped to Dee)
Compare the two PDFs in this folder:
- `2026-04-10_13-terrace-court_dee_v2.pdf` (the one Dee actually opened 6 times, viewed all 11 pages, spent 51 minutes on page 1)
- `2026-05-06_13-terrace-court_dee_designer-style.pdf`

The May 6 cover is stronger (FA design from `4274 - Marty_Fields - Property Report_FA.pdf` ported into HTML/CSS). But page 2 has lost content quality:
- Verdict is shorter, less specific, names only "4 MULL COURT and 3 ISLAY COURT" (only 2 of 3 comps)
- Strengths panel reduced to raw adjustment values ("Internal floor area: +$106,000 adjustment vs 4 MULL COURT") instead of the v2 prose ("9/10 condition with stone benchtops, inground pool, outdoor kitchen, and 52.5 sqm entertaining deck — roughly $165,000–$230,000 of renovation already done")
- Trade-off panel says **"Refer to the detailed comparable adjustment analysis for feature-by-feature value impacts"** — this is placeholder boilerplate that signals lack of analysis. The v2 said *"658 sqm lot (107 sqm less than the nearest comp), 221 sqm internal floor area, and a two-storey layout that rules out single-level living."*
- Specs say "6bd + Study" and the original was **5bd**. Data quality issue.

**The design upgrade has come with content regression.** This is the single most urgent fix in the appraisal pipeline before the next report goes out. Not a strategy issue — a code/template-and-data integration issue. See `06_production_plan.md`.

### 8.2 Adjustment numbers exposed without their named features
Showing "+$106,000 adjustment vs 4 MULL COURT" without saying *why* (because the comp had less floor area) breaks the transparency principle. Every adjustment must be named *and* sourced. This is fixable in the editorial prompt.

### 8.3 The strengths panel currently has fewer drivers in May 6 than April 10
v2 had 3 strength bullets (condition, dual living, school proximity). May 6 has 3 strength bullets that are all generic adjustment numbers. Editorial regression, not design regression.

### 8.4 Single-figure verdict risk
"We estimate a selling range of $1,724,207 to $2,803,328" (May 6) is a $1.08M range, ~63% of the lower bound. That width signals low confidence. Either narrow the comp set, or use the directional-only flag (per `valuation_directional.md`), or explicitly call out why the range is wide and what would tighten it. Sellers compare ranges; if ours is wider than the next agent's, we lose unless we explain why.

### 8.5 Drift from the Marty FA reference
The original FA file (April 29) had 11 pages of content including the strong page-2 trade-off panel ("658 sqm lot, 107 sqm less than the nearest comp..."). The May 6 render is only the cover + page 2 (the rest haven't been rebuilt yet). Re-render the full 11 pages to FA design before the next send.
