# Vulnerable

I need to tell you something uncomfortable before we start.

In 2019, I was a buyer. My wife and I had just moved to the Gold Coast from the UK. We didn't know the suburbs, the streets, the agents. We didn't know what anything was worth. We were, in every meaningful sense, vulnerable.

Money that went to a vendor who probably didn't even know they were getting above market value. # Houses Sell Themselves

There's a phrase you hear in real estate, usually muttered between agents at an open home after a quick sale: *"That one was always going to sell itself."*

But here's what that phrase actually means when you translate it from industry shorthand into dollars: *"This property will transact at some price, so the campaign quality doesn't matter."*

That's the most expensive myth in real estate.

A home that "sells itself" still sells at a price. The question is whether that price reflects the property's full value or whether it leaves $50,000 to $120,000 on the table because the campaign was adequate instead of excellent.

I see it every week in Robina. A new listing on the golf course streets — four bedrooms, decent block, renovated in the last five years. The photos are agent-phone quality. Slightly dark, slightly crooked, taken at 2pm when the sun is blasting through the front windows. The price is set to move fast — $30,000 to $50,000 below where the comparables say it could land with a proper campaign. It sells in two weeks. The vendor is happy because it was quick. The agent is happy because the commission came easy.

Then I look at the property three doors down that sold two months earlier. Same bedrooms, similar block, comparable condition. But that one had a twilight shoot, a floor plan, a video walkthrough, and a price that reflected its actual value. It sold for $80,000 more. Not because it was a better house. Because the campaign was better.

Those discount-listed homes consistently sell for $50,000 to $100,000 less than equivalent properties with proper campaigns. And no one tells the vendor, because the vendor doesn't know what the comparables said.

[PHOTO: HST-1 — Side-by-side comparison: agent-phone photo of a property vs professional twilight photo of a similar property. The visual argument for the entire chapter in a single image.]

Houses don't sell themselves. They sell at a price determined by a series of decisions — most of which are made before a single buyer walks through the door. The valuation method. The pricing strategy. The sale method. The photography. The timing. The agent selection. Each decision either adds value or leaves it behind.

The difference between a well-executed sale and a poorly-executed one isn't a rounding error. It's a renovation. It's a year of school fees. It's the gap between your next home being the one you wanted and the one you settled for.

This book will walk through each of those decisions with the data in front of you — from 931 transactions across the southern Gold Coast, supported by academic research across millions more.

Let's start with the most important question.

# Chapter 1: What Is Your Home Actually Worth?

Sarah checked her Domain estimate on a Tuesday evening. $1,580,000 for their four-bedroom home in Robina. She showed Mark. They'd bought it for $1,120,000 in 2019. The number felt right — they'd renovated the kitchen, added a deck, the market had been strong.

It became the number in their heads.

They mentioned it at a dinner party that weekend. A friend said, "Yeah, that sounds about right — the Taylors on Medinah got $1.6 for theirs." Another friend pulled up a Realestate.com.au estimate on her phone: $1,540,000. Close enough. The $1.5-million-plus identity settled in. They started talking about what it meant for the next house — the four-bedroom in Burleigh Waters they'd been watching, the one backing onto the reserve with the outdoor kitchen.

Here's what happened when that number met reality.

## The Online Estimate Problem

Domain, Realestate.com.au, and similar platforms offer automated property estimates. They're free, they're instant, and they're wildly popular. They're also, on average, wrong — and wrong in a direction that costs sellers money.

Fields Real Estate has tested 1,689 automated valuations against actual sale prices across the Gold Coast. The results are stark: 89% of properties were overvalued by the online estimate.

Let that settle for a moment. Not 89% were inaccurate — that would be unremarkable for an automated tool. Eighty-nine percent were overvalued. The error has a direction, and that direction creates a specific problem for sellers.

When an automated estimate tells you your home is worth $1,580,000 and the comparable sales suggest $1,400,000, the estimate hasn't just been wrong. It's anchored you to a number that is $180,000 above reality. Every agent who walks through your door now has to overcome that anchor. The ones who tell you the truth sound pessimistic. The ones who confirm the fantasy sound optimistic. Guess who gets hired.

[CHART: CH1-1 — Bar chart: Domain Estimate Accuracy by Price Bracket. X-axis: price brackets ($500K-$1M, $1M-$1.5M, $1.5M-$2M, $2M+). Y-axis: median error %. Shows 25-34% error at lower end dropping to 8% at premium. Source: Fields Estate analysis of 1,689 Domain estimates.]

The platforms aren't being dishonest. Their models use publicly available data — land size, bedroom count, postcode, recent sales in the area. But they can't see inside your home. They don't know that your renovated kitchen adds value or that your bathroom hasn't been touched since 1997. They don't know the house behind you just built a second storey that now overlooks your backyard. They don't know the flight path shifted. They're doing their best with limited information, and their best overvalues your home almost nine times out of ten.

## Why "Comparable Sales" Isn't Enough Without a Method

I was at an open home recently. The property was going to auction — no price guide, as Queensland law requires. An older woman approached the agent and asked what kind of price the property might achieve.

He pointed vaguely across the street: "Well, I sold a three-bedroom home over there last week for $1,300,000. And before that I sold a four-bedroom for $1,450,000."

She looked at him, nodded politely, and walked away.

He'd given her two comparable sales. Technically, that's the right starting point. But without any context for how those sales related to the property she was standing in, they were just numbers. Was this home a three-bedroom or a four-bedroom? Was the block the same size? Was the condition comparable? Was she closer to the $1,300,000 sale or the $1,450,000 one? She had no framework to evaluate what she'd just been told.

[PHOTO: CH1-2 — A Gold Coast open home scene. Agent with clipboard, buyers walking through.]

That moment captures the problem this chapter exists to solve. Comparable sales are the foundation of every credible valuation — but raw comparable sales, without adjustment and weighting, tell you almost nothing. Two prices from two different properties on two different streets are not a valuation. They're a starting point that requires a method.

## How Comparable-Sales Analysis Actually Works

Here is that method — the same one used by banks, licensed valuers, and agents when they're being rigorous.

The process looks like this. You'll see three to eight properties that recently sold near you, selected because they share the characteristics that actually drive price in your market. Same suburb. Similar land size. Comparable floor area. Similar bedroom and bathroom count. Sold within the last six to twelve months. Close enough geographically that the street-level factors — the park access, the traffic noise, the school catchment — are roughly comparable.

This is how professional comparable-sales valuation works — whether performed by a licensed valuer, a bank, or Fields Estate. Each comparable sale is adjusted for measurable differences. If a comparable has a larger floor area, the price is adjusted down to reflect what it would have sold for at your property's size. If it has fewer bedrooms, it's adjusted up. If it's on a busier road, the adjustment goes the other way. These aren't guesses — they're derived from statistical analysis of what each feature is actually worth in your specific suburb.

The adjusted prices are then weighted. A sale from last month gets more weight than one from ten months ago. A property two streets away gets more weight than one a kilometre distant. A comparable with a high-confidence adjustment gets more weight than one where the adjustment was large and uncertain.

The result is a range — not a single number. Fields Real Estate's comparable-sales model produces valuations that are accurate within the stated range 97% of the time in Robina. That's not a marketing claim. It's a tested figure across hundreds of properties where we had the valuation before the sale occurred.

[FIGURE: CH1-3 — Infographic: How Comparable-Sales Valuation Works. Simple 4-step visual: Select comparables, Adjust for differences, Weight by quality, Produce a range. Clean, minimal, not cluttered.]

The reason a range matters is that your home's value genuinely is a range. It depends on who walks through the door, what else is on the market that week, whether it rains on open-home day, whether the buyer's pre-approval gets extended. A single number creates false precision. A range reflects reality.

Understanding the valuation is not the end of the process — it is the beginning. The valuation tells you what the market considers a fair price. A premium sale price sits above that figure. The rest of this book covers how that premium is created: through pricing strategy, positioning, agent selection, and marketing. The goal is not to accept the valuation as the ceiling. It is to use it as the foundation from which a higher price can be built.

## What Actually Drives Your Home's Value

This is where the data starts to challenge common assumptions.

When you ask most homeowners what makes their property valuable, you'll hear about the renovation. The pool. The location, in general terms. The "feel" of the street. These aren't wrong, exactly, but they're not where the money is.

**Floor area is the strongest driver of price in every suburb we've analysed.**

The correlation between internal floor area and sale price ranges from 0.68 to 0.79 across Robina, Varsity Lakes, and Burleigh Waters. In statistical terms, that's a strong-to-very-strong relationship. It means that floor area alone explains between 46% and 62% of the variation in sale prices within each suburb.

This makes intuitive sense when you think about it. A buyer comparing two four-bedroom homes on similar streets in Robina — one at 180 square metres and one at 240 square metres — isn't just comparing floor plans. They're comparing how the house feels to live in. The larger home has wider hallways, a bigger living area, more storage, a kitchen where two people can cook without colliding. Those aren't luxury features. They're daily-life features. And buyers pay for them consistently, across hundreds of transactions.

**Bedrooms carry significant premiums — but they vary by suburb.**

An additional bedroom is worth between $255,000 and $607,000, depending on the suburb and the specific bedroom count. In Burleigh Waters, the jump from three to four bedrooms carries the highest premium we've measured: $607,000. That's not a typo. The four-bedroom family home market in Burleigh Waters is a fundamentally different price tier from the three-bedroom market, driven by the suburb's reputation as a family area and the limited supply of large homes.

In Robina, the premium is more moderate — around $255,000 per bedroom — reflecting higher supply and more diverse housing stock. Varsity Lakes falls between the two.

The takeaway isn't that you should add a bedroom before selling. Construction costs almost never deliver a positive return in the short term. The takeaway is that if your home has four bedrooms, it competes in a different market from three-bedroom homes, and your comparables need to reflect that.

**Corner lots trade privacy for presence — and the data shows the trade-off.**

This one surprised me. Corner lots in Robina and Burleigh Waters sell at approximately a 14% discount compared to mid-block properties with similar characteristics. Fourteen percent of $1,400,000 is $196,000. That's not trivial.

The reasons are practical, not mysterious. Corner lots have more street frontage, which means more foot traffic, more headlight glare at night, and more fence to maintain. They often have side setback requirements that reduce usable yard space. In suburbs like Robina, where the streets curve through golf-course layouts, a corner lot might face two streets while a mid-block property faces one and backs onto a reserve.

This isn't a flaw — it's a value trade-off. Some buyers actively seek corner lots for the extra street presence, the wraparound garden potential, the sense of openness. But the data says more buyers prefer mid-block, and that preference is priced in.

**Pools add almost nothing.**

The relationship between having a pool and the price per square metre is between 0.6% and 3.7% across the three suburbs. Statistically, this is not significant. The confidence intervals overlap zero in most analyses.

This doesn't mean pools are worthless to buyers — many families on the Gold Coast want a pool and will filter for one. But it means the pool itself doesn't increase your home's value in a way that survives rigorous analysis. What pools often do is correlate with other features that add value: larger blocks, newer builds, homes that have been maintained and updated. The pool is along for the ride, not driving it.

If you've spent $60,000 on a pool and expected to see $60,000 more at sale time, the data doesn't support that expectation. This is one of those findings that surprises no one in the valuation industry and surprises almost every homeowner I mention it to.

## What Doesn't Drive Value (As Much As You'd Think)

Renovations are the big one here, and the nuance matters.

A kitchen renovation clearly makes a home more attractive to buyers. Walk into an open home with a stone benchtop, integrated appliances, and soft-close drawers versus one with laminate counters and a standalone oven — the difference is visceral. Buyers react to it. Agents photograph it. It features in the marketing.

But here's the complication: renovated homes also tend to be larger. They tend to be on better streets. They tend to be owned by people who maintain the entire property, not just the kitchen. When you control for floor area — when you compare a renovated 200-square-metre home to a non-renovated 200-square-metre home on the same street — the renovation premium shrinks dramatically.

This doesn't mean renovations don't matter. It means they matter less independently than most people assume, because floor area captures a large portion of what people attribute to renovation quality. A beautifully renovated small home still sells like a small home. A large home with an original kitchen still sells like a large home.

The practical implication for sellers is this: renovating before sale can help your home sell faster and with less buyer resistance, but the data suggests it rarely adds dollar-for-dollar value in the sale price. A $40,000 kitchen renovation might make your home easier to sell at its comparable-sales value, but it's unlikely to push the price $40,000 above that value.

I see this play out in Robina constantly. Two homes on the same street, built in the same year, same number of bedrooms. One has a beautifully renovated kitchen and bathroom — stone benchtops, matte-black tapware, the full 2024 Pinterest aesthetic. The other has the original kitchen, clean but dated. The renovated home sells for $30,000 more. The renovation cost $65,000. The owner is happy because they "got more than the neighbours." But the numbers tell a different story: they invested $65,000 to recover $30,000 at the point of sale. The remaining $35,000 was the cost of enjoying the renovation while they lived there — which is perfectly valid, but shouldn't be confused with an investment return.

This finding is counter-intuitive for many homeowners, and I want to be careful with it. I'm not saying renovations are pointless. I'm saying the financial return on renovation at point of sale is usually lower than people expect, because floor area captures so much of the value that renovations appear to add. If your motivation for renovating before sale is enjoyment during the marketing period and faster buyer decisions — both legitimate — that's a clear-eyed choice. If your motivation is to add $65,000 to the sale price, the data suggests you'll be disappointed.

## Sarah and Mark Meet Reality

Sarah and Mark's comparable-sales valuation came back at $1,380,000 to $1,430,000. Their Domain estimate had been $1,580,000. The gap was $150,000.

The valuation identified six comparable sales within 800 metres of their home, all four-bedroom homes sold in the previous eight months. The adjustments accounted for floor area differences (their home was slightly smaller than two of the comparables), condition (their kitchen renovation lifted them above the median), and lot position (mid-block, which worked in their favour).

The $1,380,000 to $1,430,000 range was tighter than average, reflecting the quality of the comparables — three of the six were on the same street or one block away, sold within the last four months.

Mark's reaction was what you'd expect. "That can't be right. The Taylors got $1.6 for theirs." When they looked at the Taylors' property in the comparable data, the explanation was clear: the Taylors' home was 260 square metres internal, compared to Sarah and Mark's 210 square metres. An additional 50 square metres of living space in Robina, at a rate consistent with the suburb's floor-area premium, accounted for almost the entire difference.

The Domain estimate hadn't known that. It had seen four bedrooms, a Robina postcode, a 600-square-metre block, and recent sales in the $1.4 to $1.7 million range. It had produced a number that was mathematically reasonable and practically misleading.

I see this pattern every week. A homeowner anchors to an online number, rejects the first agent who tells them the truth, and hires the one who confirms the fantasy. The ending is always the same. The property lists too high, sits on the market for six or eight weeks, takes a price reduction, and eventually sells at or below where the comparables said it would. Except now it's sold with the stigma of a stale listing and a price drop, which often pushes the final price below what a correctly-priced day-one campaign would have achieved.

The first step in maximising your sale price is understanding what your home is actually worth. Not what you hope it's worth, not what a website guesses, not what your neighbour got for a larger home on a better street. What the comparable sales, adjusted for the specific characteristics of your property, say it's worth.

Everything else in this book builds on that foundation.

# Chapter 2: When Is the Right Time to Sell?

The Hendersons in Varsity Lakes waited eighteen months for "the right time." They watched the market reports, listened to economists, delayed listing three times. When they finally sold in March 2025, they got $1,185,000. An almost identical property two doors down had sold for $1,210,000 in October 2023 — the month the Hendersons first considered selling.

Eighteen months of waiting. Two rent payments on their new apartment while they maintained a property they'd mentally left behind. Weekends spent mowing the lawn of a house they no longer lived in, driving back from their rental in Mudgeeraba to make sure the gardens looked presentable for inspections. And the result was $25,000 less than they would have received if they'd listed the day they first thought about it.

The Hendersons aren't unusual. The desire to time the market is one of the most powerful instincts in property, and one of the least useful.

## Life Timing vs Market Timing

Most people don't sell because they've identified a peak in the property cycle. They sell because something changed in their life. The kids started school and the commute to Robina Town Centre doesn't work anymore. A parent needs care and you need to be closer to the hospital at Varsity Lakes. You got a promotion in Brisbane and the M1 commute twice daily has gone from tolerable to soul-destroying. The marriage ended. The house is too big now that the kids have moved out. You inherited a property and carrying two mortgages isn't sustainable.

These are life reasons, and they operate on life timelines. The school year starts in January. The new job starts in six weeks. The divorce settlement has a deadline. You don't get to choose October because the data says October is optimal. You sell when your life says it's time.

And here's the thing the market-timing advocates rarely mention: that's fine. The data shows that selling when your life demands it, rather than waiting for a theoretically better moment, almost always produces a better outcome than waiting. Because waiting has costs — financial, emotional, and practical — that the "optimal month" analyses never account for.

The Hendersons spent $39,600 on rent while they waited. They spent weekends maintaining a property they no longer lived in. They spent eighteen months with their capital locked in a house instead of deployed toward their next chapter. The $25,000 difference in sale price was dwarfed by the cost of waiting for it.

That said, if you have the luxury of choosing when to list — if your timeline has flexibility of a few months — the data does reveal meaningful patterns. Ignoring them would be as foolish as obsessing over them.

## Supply Dynamics: What to Watch

Individual sellers can't control the market. But understanding what the market is doing when you list can inform decisions about timing and pricing.

The simplest metric is absorption rate: how many months of inventory are currently available? You calculate it by dividing the number of active listings by the average monthly sales volume.

As of the current market:

*(Data as of April 2026)*

- **Robina:** 55 active listings, averaging roughly 30 sales per month = approximately 1.8 months of inventory
- **Varsity Lakes:** 32 active listings, averaging roughly 25 sales per month = approximately 1.3 months of inventory
- **Burleigh Waters:** 37 active listings, averaging roughly 23 sales per month = approximately 1.6 months of inventory

All three suburbs are firmly in seller-favourable territory. Anything below three months of inventory tends to create competitive conditions for well-priced properties. Below two months, buyers feel urgency.

What you're watching for is a sudden spike in listings — a month where supply jumps by 40% or 50% — because that dilutes buyer attention and can soften prices even when underlying demand is strong. On the Gold Coast, these spikes tend to occur in January (New Year's resolution sellers) and September (spring rush). If your timeline allows you to avoid listing in the same week as ten other properties in your suburb, that small competitive advantage can translate into faster sale times and stronger offers.

I track this data weekly across all three suburbs. I can tell you right now that Varsity Lakes has 32 active listings — the lowest inventory of the three. A well-priced home in Varsity Lakes right now faces less competition than at almost any point in the last twelve months. Robina, with 55 active listings, has more competition — but also a deeper buyer pool. Burleigh Waters sits at 37, which, at its median price of $1,710,000, represents a relatively thin market where each new listing is noticed.

These numbers shift constantly. But the habit of checking them before you finalise your listing date — rather than just picking the first available Saturday — is a small advantage that costs nothing and can influence how many buyers walk through your door on opening weekend.

[PHOTO: CH2-3 — Varsity Lakes lake and park area. Lifestyle shot that says "this is why people buy here."]

One more thing about supply that's worth understanding: inventory in your specific price bracket matters more than total suburb inventory. If there are 55 listings in Robina but only six of them are four-bedroom homes priced between $1,300,000 and $1,500,000, your competitive set is six, not fifty-five. The other forty-nine listings are noise. Knowing your competitive set — the properties that your buyer is comparing you to — helps you understand whether you're listing into a crowded field or an open one.

## Sarah and Mark's Timing

Sarah and Mark listed in early November — Robina's strongest month historically. Whether they chose it for that reason or because their school-year timing required it, the data was in their favour.

Their agent had suggested late October as an alternative. "Get ahead of the November rush," she'd said. But Sarah's parents were visiting from interstate until the end of October, and the house wouldn't show well with two extra people in it. They chose November for practical reasons.

It turned out to be the right call for data reasons too. The first open home had fourteen groups through. The second had eleven. By the third weekend — day 18 of the listing — they were reviewing two written offers.

That timeline reflected good fundamentals. Not because they'd engineered it, but because the property was priced correctly (more on that in Chapter 4), photographed well, and listed in a month when buyers in Robina are actively looking.

## A Personal Note on Timing

I've stopped having opinions about market timing. I've seen too many people wait for the perfect moment and watch it pass. I've watched sellers hold off listing because an economist on television said rates might drop in six months. I've watched others rush to market because a newspaper headline said the boom was ending. In most cases, the economist was wrong, the headline was wrong, and the seller's original timeline was the right one all along.

What I do have is six years of data showing which months produce the best results in each suburb. That's more useful than any prediction. It's not a crystal ball — it's a rearview mirror. But in a world where everyone is trying to guess what happens next, knowing what has consistently happened before is an underrated advantage.

The most reliable path to a strong sale price isn't finding the perfect month. It's pricing correctly, marketing well, and giving the market enough time to do what it does. The sellers who do those three things well tend to get good outcomes regardless of whether they list in the "best" month or not.

Time your life first. Then check the data. If they align, wonderful. If they don't, know that correct pricing on day one covers more ground than any seasonal advantage ever could.

# Chapter 3: Auction or Private Treaty?

Imagine you're a buyer on a Saturday morning, scrolling through Realestate.com.au. You see a four-bedroom home in Burleigh Waters. Good photos, nice street, leafy outlook. Where the price should be, it says: "Auction." No guide. No range. Nothing.

Do you click? Or do you scroll to the next listing that tells you what it costs?

If you're like 72% of property buyers, you scroll past.

That number comes from research into buyer behaviour on property portals, and it reflects something psychologists have understood since Daniel Ellsberg's work (Ellsberg, 1961): people avoid ambiguity. Given a choice between a known quantity and an unknown one, most people choose the known quantity — even if the unknown one might be better.

In property, this is called ambiguity aversion, and it's one of the most powerful forces working against auction sellers. When a listing shows a price — even a range — buyers can quickly decide whether it's in their budget. They can picture themselves living there. They can start building an emotional connection. When a listing shows "Auction" and nothing else, the buyer has to do work just to figure out whether it's worth their time. Most won't bother.

Uri Gneezy's research (Gneezy, List, and Wu, 2006) at UC San Diego has demonstrated this across multiple contexts: people don't just prefer certainty — they'll pay a premium for it. And Fox and Tversky's work (Fox and Tversky, 1995) at Stanford showed that the aversion to ambiguity increases as the stakes get higher. Property is about the highest-stakes purchase most people make.

So the question isn't just "auction or private treaty?" It's "do you want to sell your home using a method that 72% of buyers instinctively avoid?"

## The Largest Study Ever Conducted

The University of Sydney, in partnership with the Reserve Bank (University of Sydney, multiple authors), analysed 1.2 million residential sales across Australia. The study compared auction sales to private treaty sales, controlling for property type, location, and market conditions.

The finding: no statistically significant difference in sale price between auction and private treaty.

This is worth repeating because it contradicts the single most common claim made by agents who advocate for auction — that auction "creates competition" which "drives the price up." Across 1.2 million transactions, that claim has no support. Properties sold by auction achieved, on average, the same prices as comparable properties sold by private treaty.

Now, absence of difference isn't absence of nuance. There may be specific property types, markets, or conditions where auction produces better outcomes. But across the broadest dataset ever assembled on this question — 1.2 million Australian homes — the advantage simply doesn't appear.

What does appear is a cost difference. Auction campaigns on the Gold Coast typically cost $5,000 to $10,000 in marketing alone -- the auctioneer's fee, larger print advertising footprint, signage, and the compressed timeline that demands more aggressive spend. A private treaty campaign for the same property might cost $2,000 to $5,000. If the University of Sydney's 1.2-million-sale study shows no price difference between the two methods, the additional $3,000 to $5,000 becomes hard to justify -- especially when auction simultaneously reduces the buyer pool by 72%.

## Why Queensland Is Different

In New South Wales and Victoria, auction is a cultural norm. In Sydney's eastern suburbs and Melbourne's inner ring, buyers expect auction. They understand the process. They come prepared with pre-approval, solicitor's review, and a bidding strategy. The ambiguity aversion is softened by familiarity.

Queensland is different, and legislation is a big part of why.

Under Queensland law, agents are prohibited from providing price guides at auction. Unlike in New South Wales, where agents can (and must) provide a non-binding price guide, Queensland auction sellers go to market with no public indication of their expectations. The buyer sees "Auction" and nothing else.

This amplifies the ambiguity aversion problem significantly. In Sydney, a buyer might see "Auction Guide: $1,500,000" and know roughly where they stand. In Queensland, that same buyer sees "Auction" and has to call the agent, arrange an inspection, do their own research, obtain a valuation, and still wonder whether they're in the right ballpark — all before auction day. The drop-off rate at each step is substantial.

The result is visible in the local data. The vast majority of properties on the southern Gold Coast sell via private treaty. This isn't because agents don't suggest auction — many do, particularly those from national franchises that operate heavily in southern states. It's because the local market has collectively decided, through thousands of individual buying decisions, that private treaty works better here.

Walk through any open home in Robina on a Saturday and ask the buyers how they found the listing. Almost without exception, they searched by price range. They typed "$1,200,000 to $1,500,000" into Domain, filtered for four bedrooms, selected Robina, and scrolled through what came back. That's the reality of how buyers shop on the Gold Coast. An auction listing without a price guide doesn't appear in that filtered search. It might appear in a broader search, but it's already fighting an uphill battle before the buyer has even read the address.

There's an honest conversation worth having about why some agents still recommend auction in a market overwhelmingly dominated by private treaty. Auction campaigns tend to have shorter timelines, which means faster commissions. They also tend to require larger marketing budgets, which in some agency models generates additional revenue. These aren't nefarious motivations — they're business incentives — but sellers deserve to know they exist.

## The Agent Conversation

One practical difference between the two methods rarely gets discussed, and it matters more than most sellers realise.

In a private treaty sale, when a buyer expresses interest and asks about price, the agent has a number to work with. They can say: "The property is listed at $1,415,000, and the sellers are committed to that range based on comparable sales." The buyer can then decide whether they want to proceed, negotiate, or walk away. The agent can advocate for the seller's price because there is a price to advocate for.

In an auction sale, that conversation is legally different in Queensland. The agent cannot provide a price guide. When a buyer asks what the sellers expect, the agent is limited to vague responses: "Register your interest and come along on auction day." This isn't the agent being evasive — they're complying with the law. But from the buyer's perspective, it feels evasive. It creates friction. It reduces trust.

For serious buyers — the ones who are genuinely qualified and ready to make a decision — this friction is often enough to redirect their attention to a property where the price is transparent. The auction listing hasn't just failed to attract these buyers. It has actively pushed them toward competing properties.

## When Auction Might Make Sense

In the interest of completeness, there are circumstances where auction can produce strong results on the Gold Coast:

Truly unique properties where comparable sales don't exist — the waterfront estate, the architecturally significant home, the development site with unusual zoning — can benefit from auction because there's no objective way to set a price. Letting the market determine value through competitive bidding can be more efficient than guessing.

Properties with extremely strong emotional appeal — the one home on the street that everyone in the suburb knows about — can generate the kind of auction-day energy that does drive prices. But "extremely strong emotional appeal" describes perhaps one in a hundred listings. If your home is one of them, you'll know, and so will every agent who walks through it.

Deceased estates and court-ordered sales sometimes require auction for legal or administrative reasons. In those cases, the method is chosen for the seller, not by them.

For the vast majority of Gold Coast properties, the data consistently points toward private treaty.

Their agent recommended private treaty without hesitation. "The Gold Coast is overwhelmingly a private treaty market," she told them. "Auction makes sense in Sydney where buyers expect it. Here, it limits your buyer pool."

Sarah had been mildly curious about auction — her sister in Melbourne had sold at auction and described the experience as exciting, competitive, fast. But the agent walked her through the Queensland legislation, the cost difference, and the buyer behaviour data. The decision was straightforward.

They would sell by private treaty, with a clear price that told every buyer scrolling past on Saturday morning exactly where they stood.

I've attended dozens of Gold Coast auctions. The atmosphere is different from Melbourne — quieter, fewer bidders, more passed-in results. # Chapter 4: Setting the Right Price

A three-bedroom home on Federal Place in Robina listed at $1,450,000 in January. It was a nice home — good street, one of those quiet cul-de-sac pockets near the golf course where the traffic is minimal and the trees are mature. Renovated kitchen, decent-sized pool, well-maintained gardens. The kind of property that, with the right campaign, could generate genuine buyer interest within the first week.

The comparable sales data suggested a value of $1,280,000 to $1,320,000.

The listing price was at least $130,000 above reality.

Here's what happened: nothing, for six weeks. The first open home drew reasonable numbers — Federal Place is a known street, and any new listing generates curiosity. But no one made an offer. The second weekend was quieter. By the fourth weekend, the agent was calling buyers who'd inspected and hearing the same thing: "We liked it, but the price doesn't stack up."

At week six, the price dropped to $1,380,000. A few buyers who'd been watching came back. But the damage was done — the listing had been on the market long enough that every buyer who looked it up could see the original price, the price drop, and the time it had been sitting. The perception shifted from "here's a nice home" to "what's wrong with this place?"

It eventually sold for $1,290,000, after 97 days on market. Within the original comparable range, but at the bottom of it, not the top. The overpricing hadn't just failed to achieve a higher price. It had likely cost the seller $20,000 to $30,000 compared to a correctly priced day-one campaign.

This pattern is so common that the research literature has a name for it: the overpricing penalty.

## What the Research Says About Overpricing

The academic evidence is extensive and consistent across markets, countries, and decades.

**Taylor (1999)** studied the relationship between listing price and time on market across thousands of transactions. Properties listed more than 10% above their market value took two to five times longer to sell than correctly priced properties. Not slightly longer. Two to five times. A home that should have sold in three weeks sat for six to fifteen weeks.

**Knight (2002)** found a similar pattern and added a critical insight: overpriced homes that eventually sold after price reductions achieved final prices lower than homes that had been correctly priced from the start. The price reduction itself became a negative signal to buyers, who interpreted it as evidence of a problem rather than a correction.

**Anglin, Rutherford, and Springer (2003)** analysed the dynamic further: sellers who overpriced by more than 10% were significantly more likely to experience multiple price reductions, each of which further eroded buyer confidence. The downward spiral was self-reinforcing.

**Zillow Research (2019)** — working with a dataset of 25,000 sales — put a specific number on the penalty: homes overpriced by 12% or more were 50% less likely to sell within 60 days. Half the probability of selling in a normal timeframe, simply because the opening number was wrong.

The mechanism isn't mysterious. A buyer looking at properties in the $1,250,000 to $1,400,000 range in Robina has perhaps 15 to 20 options on any given Saturday. They have time to inspect maybe four or five. They will shortlist the ones that appear to offer the best value for the price shown. A property listed at $1,450,000 that is worth $1,300,000 doesn't appear on their shortlist — not because they can't afford $1,450,000, but because other properties at that price are clearly better value. The overpriced listing is invisible to its natural buyer pool and uncompetitive in the pool it's been placed into.

The seller's mistake wasn't wanting a high price. Everyone wants a high price. The mistake was confusing the asking price with the sale price. They're different things, and the relationship between them is more complex than "list high, negotiate down."

**The asking price is a marketing tool. The sale price is the outcome. They are not the same thing. Confusing them is where most overpricing mistakes begin.**

## The Underpricing Question

If overpricing is so dangerous, why not underprice and let competition drive the number up?

This is the logic behind the auction-adjacent strategy of listing at an attractive price to generate multiple offers. In theory, it works — lower price creates more interest, more interest creates competition, competition drives the price above where it would have been with a higher starting point.

In practice, in a private treaty market, it usually doesn't.

A study of 14,000 private treaty transactions (Bucchianeri and Minson, 2013) found that deliberate underpricing rarely generated the bidding wars sellers hoped for. In a private treaty sale, the dynamic is different from auction. There's no public forum where buyers compete in real time. Instead, each buyer submits an offer privately, often unaware of competing offers, and the negotiation happens between the agent and individual buyers.

When a buyer sees a property priced attractively, their instinct is not to offer more than the asking price. Their instinct is to offer the asking price, or slightly below, and negotiate from there. The anchor works in the buyer's favour, not the seller's.

The exceptions are extreme undersupply situations — markets where there are genuinely more qualified buyers than available properties and every listing generates immediate interest. In those conditions, underpricing can work because the scarcity creates urgency that overrides normal buyer psychology. But those conditions are temporary and unpredictable. Building a pricing strategy around them is like planning your retirement around winning the lottery.

The sweet spot, as the data consistently shows, is accurate pricing from day one. Not high. Not low. Right.

## The Agent Incentive Problem

Here's something most pricing discussions avoid: the person advising you on price has different incentives from yours.

Steven Levitt and Chad Syverson (Levitt and Syverson, 2008), economists at the University of Chicago, studied what happens when real estate agents sell their own homes versus when they sell clients' homes. The data came from thousands of transactions in the Chicago market, comparing identical agents in their two roles.

When agents sold their own properties, they held out for 3.7% higher prices and kept them on the market for an average of 10 additional days.

Three-point-seven percent of $1,400,000 is $51,800.

The agents weren't bad at their jobs. They were responding rationally to different incentive structures. When you're the seller, holding out for an extra $52,000 is obviously worth waiting ten more days. When you're the agent earning a 2.5% commission, that extra $52,000 in sale price translates to roughly $1,300 in additional commission — but it costs ten more days of open homes, buyer follow-ups, and marketing. The agent's rational calculation is to accept a slightly lower price now rather than invest ten more days of effort for $1,300.

This isn't an indictment of every agent. Many agents fight hard for every dollar on behalf of their clients. But the structural incentive is real, and the Levitt and Syverson study — published in one of the world's top economics journals — found it operating consistently across thousands of transactions.

What it means for sellers is straightforward: price your home based on comparable-sales data, not on an agent's recommendation alone. The agent's recommendation may be excellent. But you should be able to verify it against the data, and any agent who resists that verification is telling you something about their confidence in their own number.

## The Science of Precise Pricing

This is where the research gets genuinely fascinating.

David Cardella and Michael Seiler (Cardella and Seiler, 2016), economists who study pricing psychology, identified a specific pricing strategy that outperforms round numbers across multiple studies. The strategy has four conditions, all of which need to be met:

**Condition 1: The price must be precise, not round.** $1,315,000, not $1,300,000. Round numbers signal approximation — the seller doesn't know what it's worth, so they've picked a convenient figure. Precise numbers signal knowledge — the seller has done the work and arrived at a specific value.

**Condition 2: The precise price must be above the round number, not below.** $1,315,000, not $1,285,000. This matters because a precise price below a round number — say $1,285,000 — signals a seller who wanted $1,300,000 but couldn't justify it. It reads as weakness. A precise price above — $1,315,000 — reads as a seller who started at $1,300,000 and added value based on specific property features.

**Condition 3: The gap between the precise price and the round number should be small.** $1,315,000 works. $1,387,000 doesn't. The precision needs to feel deliberate, not arbitrary. When the gap is small, buyers interpret it as a considered adjustment. When the gap is large, it looks like the seller picked a random number.

**Condition 4: The property must present as high-quality for its price range — the buyer must perceive it as being at the upper end of its comparable range.** In Cardella and Seiler's experimental framework, precise pricing above a round number works best when the property presents well, when the marketing materials are professional, and when the buyer's first impression supports a premium. When the property is clearly average for its price range, precise pricing can feel presumptuous.

When all four conditions are met, the research shows that buyers anchor to the precise number more strongly than they anchor to round numbers, they negotiate less aggressively, and final sale prices are higher. The effect size is meaningful — in Cardella and Seiler's data, the equivalent of several percent of the sale price.

To put this in Gold Coast terms: the difference between listing at $1,400,000 and $1,415,000 isn't cosmetic. It's a pricing signal. The round number says "we think it's worth about $1.4 million." The precise number says "we've analysed the comparables, assessed the specific features of this property, and arrived at $1,415,000." The buyer reads that signal, consciously or not, and it changes how they approach the negotiation.

I've tracked this in our data. Properties in Robina listed at precise prices above round numbers — $1,315,000 instead of $1,300,000, $1,415,000 instead of $1,400,000 — show a measurably tighter spread between listing price and sale price. The negotiation gap shrinks. Sellers who use precise pricing tend to achieve higher final sale prices and retain a larger share of the difference between their asking price and the buyer's opening offer, not because the buyers are being tricked, but because the precision communicates that the price is already considered.

The practical application looks like this: if your home's comparable-sales range is $1,380,000 to $1,430,000, and you're pricing at the top end, $1,415,000 is a better listing price than $1,400,000 or $1,430,000. It's precise. It's above the round number. The gap is small. And if your marketing materials present the home at its best, the fourth condition is met.

## Price Brackets and Portal Visibility

There's a second, purely mechanical reason why your listing price matters: property portal search brackets.

Most buyers search within price ranges. On Domain and Realestate.com.au, the common brackets for the Gold Coast market are $1,000,000 to $1,250,000, $1,250,000 to $1,500,000, and $1,500,000 to $2,000,000. A buyer searching $1,250,000 to $1,500,000 will see every listing priced within that range.

A home listed at $1,510,000 is invisible to that buyer. A home listed at $1,495,000 appears in their search. The difference between visibility and invisibility is $15,000 in listing price — which may or may not be $15,000 in achievable sale price.

This isn't a reason to underprice. It's a reason to be aware of where the brackets fall and to think carefully about whether being at the bottom of a higher bracket serves you better than being at the top of a lower one.

In most cases, being at the top of the bracket that contains your natural buyer pool is the stronger position. A home priced at $1,485,000 in the $1,250,000 to $1,500,000 bracket is seen by every buyer in that range — including the ones who were hoping to spend $1,350,000 but might stretch for the right property. That same home priced at $1,510,000 is now competing with properties in the $1,500,000 to $2,000,000 bracket, where it's the cheapest option and perceived as the least desirable.

The bracket strategy is simple: identify your natural buyer pool, find the bracket they're searching in, and price within that bracket.

### Building Emotion to Secure the Best Sale Price

Maximising sale price is all about building emotion with buyers. When buyers start considering a home purchase, they embark on a journey towards what we call their emotional pinnacle.

To secure the best possible price — or even exceed expectations — an agent must possess the expertise to artfully elevate buyer emotions to a peak and then skillfully extract their optimal offer. Timing is crucial; if done too late or without proper guidance, buyers may fail to reach this emotional high or lose interest altogether.

[FIGURE: CH4-3 — The buyer's emotional peak and decline. Shows rising emotional investment peaking then declining as doubt sets in.]

The emotional peak cannot be sustained. It will drop at which point the buyer begins to second-guess their decision. Offers must be obtained at the emotional peak — not before it (when the buyer hasn't committed enough to stretch) and not after (when doubt has set in and the willingness to pay has declined).

This is where the quality of photography, videography, and listing content plays a direct role. Between inspections, buyers return to the listing. They scroll through the photographs. They show their partner the video tour. Each time they revisit this content, they are reinforcing their emotional connection to the property — building and sustaining the peak rather than letting it decay. A buyer who returns to professional twilight photography and a detailed video walkthrough deepens their emotional investment with every viewing. A buyer who returns to dark, blurry photos has nothing to sustain their imagination.

## Visual Content and the Emotional Peak

Professional visual content extends that emotional window. Twilight photography -- shot in the golden hour before sunset, with interior lights on and exterior colours warm -- creates an emotional response that agent-phone photos cannot match. A floor plan converts emotional interest into practical consideration, removing hesitation about layout. Video walkthroughs give interstate and overseas buyers -- a significant portion of the Gold Coast market -- a way to engage deeply before flying in.

The data from 931 transactions shows that properties with professional visual content sell faster and closer to their comparable-sales value than properties without it.

The Gold Coast light is both an asset and a trap. At twilight, a Robina home with the garden lights on and the sky turning purple behind the Hinterland creates an emotional response you can't manufacture in Photoshop. At 2pm in January, that same home looks bleached and flat. Emotionally, they're selling two different homes. The twilight version invites you in. The midday version is a documentary record.

Sellers who treat photography as an expense to be minimised are choosing to present their home at half its emotional potential to every buyer who scrolls past in the first critical week.

## Sarah and Mark's Price

Their valuation came back at $1,380,000 to $1,430,000. Their agent suggested listing at $1,415,000.

Let's break down why that number was chosen.

It's precise — not a round $1,400,000. It's above the round number — $15,000 above $1,400,000, not $15,000 below $1,430,000. The gap is small and feels deliberate, not arbitrary. When a seller lists at $1,450,000 and gets an offer at $1,380,000, the buyer already knows the listing price is inflated. The negotiation starts with the buyer feeling like they've caught you out."

They listed at $1,415,000.

## Early Offers and How to Think About Them

Sarah and Mark received their first offer on day twelve: $1,385,000, subject to building and pest inspection. It was below their listing price but within their comparable range.

Here's where seller psychology gets complicated. The instinct — almost universal — is to feel that an early offer means the price was too low. "If someone offered this quickly, we could have asked for more." This instinct is wrong, and it costs sellers money. I've watched it derail sales in real time.

A seller gets an offer on day ten, panics, and thinks: "We priced too low — let's reject it and wait for something higher." Two weeks later, no higher offer materialises. The original buyer has purchased elsewhere. The property sits for another month, takes a price reduction, and eventually sells for less than the rejected day-ten offer. The seller's instinct to protect themselves against undervaluing their home actually produced the outcome they feared most.

An early offer is a signal of correct pricing. It means a qualified buyer, with pre-approval and intent, looked at the property and the price, assessed them as aligned, and decided to act before someone else did. That's exactly what a well-priced, well-marketed property should produce.

The alternative — no offers for four weeks, followed by a reluctant offer at the bottom of the range — is a signal of overpricing. That's the outcome sellers who reject early offers or raise their expectations mid-campaign often end up with.

Sarah and Mark didn't accept the first offer. Their agent countered. After a brief negotiation, the buyer came up to $1,420,000 — within the comparable range, above the median, achieved in 16 days.

Compare that to the Federal Place property from the opening of this chapter: 97 days on market, a price reduction that everyone could see, and a final sale at the bottom of the comparable range. Same suburb. Same type of home. Different outcomes, separated entirely by the day-one pricing decision.

## The Hardest Conversation

The most valuable thing an agent can give you is an accurate starting point. Not an inflated number to win the listing. Not a deflated number to guarantee a quick sale. An accurate number — because every strategy in this book depends on knowing where real value sits. From there, the work of achieving a premium begins.

Sellers who engage with that range honestly — who let go of the number in their heads and work with the number the data produces — tend to achieve outcomes at the top of the range. They price correctly, market well, and sell within three weeks to a buyer who is emotionally engaged and financially qualified.

Sellers who fight the range — who list 10% or 15% above it because they "want to test the market" or because another agent told them what they wanted to hear — tend to achieve outcomes at the bottom of the range. They list high, sit for weeks, reduce the price, attract bargain hunters, and sell below where they would have been with a correct day-one price.

I've never seen a homeowner regret pricing correctly on day one. I've seen many regret starting too high.

Sarah and Mark priced correctly. They sold in 16 days. Their final price — $1,420,000 — sat comfortably within their comparable range, above the median of comparable sales, and $5,000 above their listing price. The negotiation took two days. Both parties moved, both felt they'd been treated fairly, and the contract was signed on a Thursday afternoon while Sarah picked the kids up from school.

That's what it looks like when the data informs the decisions. Not perfection. Not maximum-possible-price-in-a-parallel-universe. A considered, transparent, well-executed sale that reflected what the home was worth in the market that existed at the time.

The second half of this book will cover the remaining decisions: choosing the right agent, executing the marketing campaign, navigating the negotiation, and managing the legal process from contract to settlement. Each one matters. But none of them can overcome a wrong price.

Get the price right, and everything else gets easier.

# Chapter 5: The Three Things That Actually Move the Price

Every homeowner I've met believes their renovation added value. Most of them are wrong -- at least in Robina and Varsity Lakes. The data on this is surprising, counterintuitive, and potentially worth tens of thousands of dollars.

I've spent two years building a database of property sales across the southern Gold Coast. When I started, I assumed what everyone assumes: that a beautiful kitchen adds serious money to a sale price, that a pool is a no-brainer in Queensland, and that a freshly renovated home will always outperform an original one.

The data told a different story. And after synthesising 60 academic studies, 14 peer-reviewed papers, and 2,153 sold properties, I can tell you this: the three things that actually move a property's sale price are not the three things most sellers spend money on.

They are simpler, cheaper, and more powerful than any renovation.

---

### The Framework

Think of a buyer's journey through your property as three layers. Each layer operates at a different stage, triggers a different response, and has a different return on investment. Get all three right, and you create the conditions for a premium sale. Get even one wrong, and the other two can't compensate.

**Layer 1: What buyers see first.**
**Layer 2: What buyers feel.**
**Layer 3: What buyers compare.**

These aren't marketing concepts. They're backed by behavioural research and verified against local sales data. Let me walk through each one.

### Layer 1: What Buyers See First

The first three seconds of a buyer's experience with your property will determine whether they ever see the rest.

This is not metaphor. It is how realestate.com.au works. A buyer sets their filters -- suburb, bedrooms, price range -- and scrolls. Each listing appears as a thumbnail image, a price, and an address. The buyer decides to click or keep scrolling in approximately three seconds. If the lead image doesn't stop them, the description is never read. The floor plan is never opened. The virtual tour never plays. The $45,000 kitchen renovation is invisible.

Listings with professional photography receive 118% more online views than those without. That is not a marginal improvement. It is the difference between 100 potential buyers seeing a property and 218 potential buyers seeing it. Every additional buyer is a potential competitor, and every competitor raises the price.

This is where the highest-ROI pre-sale spending lives:

**Fresh paint.** Properties with fresh paint sell approximately 25% faster and receive offers up to 5% higher. On a $1,400,000 Robina home, 5% is $70,000. A full interior and exterior paint costs $3,000 to $8,000. The ratio is hard to argue with.

**Landscaping.** Research on curb appeal consistently shows that the exterior presentation shapes buyer expectations before they walk through the front door. Well-presented gardens create up to a 20% increase in perceived value. Not actual value -- perceived value. But in property, perceived value becomes actual value the moment a second buyer makes an offer. A load of fresh mulch, seasonal colour in the front beds, trimmed hedges, and a pressure-washed driveway costs $500 to $2,000.

**Professional photography.** This is non-negotiable. The gap between an agent's iPhone photo and a professional property photographer's work is larger than most homeowners realise. Wide-angle lenses that show rooms honestly. Professional lighting that works even on an overcast Wednesday. Twilight exterior shots -- that twenty-minute window after sunset when the warm interior glow meets the evening sky. Drone photography that shows the neighbourhood context: the park across the street, the canal path, the hinterland behind.

**Virtual tours.** Research shows virtual tours generate 77 seconds of engagement compared to 44 seconds for static images -- a 75% increase in time spent with the property. A 2024 NBER study (Soleymanian and Qian, 2024) analysing 75,000 home sales in Los Angeles confirmed that virtual tours still increase sale prices by approximately 1%. But the study also noted the differential advantage has narrowed as tours have shifted from novelty to expectation. Virtual tours are now table-stakes: not having one hurts more than having one helps.

The pattern across all of these is the same: they address what happens in the first three seconds. Paint and landscaping create the curb appeal that survives a drive-by. Photography creates the scroll-stopping moment on the portal. Virtual tours extend the buyer's attention from seconds to minutes. None of them cost more than a few thousand dollars. All of them have measurable effects on both speed and price.

### Layer 2: What Buyers Feel

Once a buyer has clicked on the listing, once they've scrolled through the photos, once they've walked through the front door at the open home -- the decision shifts from visual to emotional.

This is where the story of the property takes over. And the data on narrative positioning is some of the most compelling in the entire research base.

Consider the difference between two listing descriptions for the same home:

*"4-bedroom family home with pool and outdoor entertaining area."*

*"The north-facing backyard catches afternoon light until 6pm in winter. From the living room, the spotted gums frame a view of the hinterland that most visitors don't expect."*

The first describes features. The second creates a picture of a specific life. Behavioural research shows that specific, vivid descriptions activate what psychologists call "mental simulation" -- the buyer begins imagining themselves in the home. They picture their Saturday afternoon on that north-facing deck. They imagine the light through those trees. They haven't made an offer yet, but something has already shifted: they've started to feel like the home is theirs.

This is the endowment effect in action. Once a buyer mentally takes ownership -- once they've imagined their morning coffee on that deck, their children walking to school along that tree-lined path, their evening view of the hinterland fading to dusk -- they are measurably less willing to let go. Their willingness to pay increases. Their sensitivity to price decreases.

Here's another example:

*"Renovated 3-bed home, close to schools, open-plan living."*

*"The 8-minute walk to Robina State School follows the path along the canal. The kitchen opens directly to the deck where the afternoon breeze comes through until the light fades behind the hinterland."*

The first could describe hundreds of properties. The second can only describe one. The specificity is what creates credibility, and credibility is what creates trust, and trust is what creates willingness to pay a premium.

This matters between inspections too. After an open home, buyers return to the listing. They scroll through the photographs again. They show their partner. They zoom in on the backyard, the street, the view. Each time they revisit, they are reinforcing their emotional connection -- building and sustaining the peak rather than letting it decay. Professional photography and video give buyers material to keep building that vision. Blurry phone photos give them nothing to return to.

**What backfires: manufactured urgency.**

One positioning approach the data explicitly warns against is manufactured urgency. Phrases like "Don't miss out!", "Once in a lifetime opportunity!", and "Offers closing soon!" are common in Gold Coast property marketing. Our analysis from Burleigh Waters found that listings using urgency-style opening lines had an average of 33 days on market, compared to 20.5 days for listings using factual, data-driven openings.

That is not a marginal difference. The properties positioned with manufactured urgency sat on the market 61% longer.

Buyers in these suburbs are experienced and information-rich. They recognise manufactured urgency for what it is, and it erodes the trust that Layer 2 depends on. If genuine scarcity exists -- "one of only two four-bedroom homes with a pool currently listed in Robina under $1,400,000" -- that is a factual statement worth making. If scarcity does not exist, manufacturing it damages the property's positioning.

---

### Layer 3: What Buyers Compare

No property is valued in isolation. Every buyer looking at a four-bedroom home in Robina between $1,200,000 and $1,500,000 is simultaneously looking at every other four-bedroom home in Robina in that range. On any given week, there might be eight to twelve of them. The buyer inspects most. Each property is mentally ranked against the others: which has the best outdoor area? The biggest bedrooms? The shortest walk to school? The most natural light?

This is competitive positioning, and it is the most overlooked layer in the selling process.

Effective competitive positioning starts with understanding what's on the market right now. Not in general. Not historically. Right now. A feature that would be unremarkable when fifteen competing properties are listed might be genuinely scarce when there are five. "One of only three homes with a north-facing pool and four bedrooms currently listed in Robina under $1,500,000" is a factual statement that creates real competitive advantage -- because the buyer can verify it with a search.

The second part of competitive positioning is trade-off reframing. Every property has compromises. The ones that sell well are the ones where those compromises are reframed as value -- honestly, but deliberately.

A smaller block near a park is not a "small block." It is "low maintenance with park frontage -- no mowing, no pool costs, and the children have more play space than any backyard could offer."

A home on a busier road is not "noisy." It is "exceptional accessibility -- two minutes to the M1, walking distance to the bus, without the premium of a cul-de-sac that often adds $50,000 to $100,000 to the asking price."

These are not spin. They are legitimate reframings that help the buyer see the property in its best honest light. A seller who reads this and thinks "that's just what my place is like" should feel reassured: trade-offs are features when positioned correctly.

A skilled agent performs this competitive analysis before listing and updates it as competing properties appear and disappear during the campaign. This is strategic intelligence, not marketing fluff. It informs the lead photograph, the listing description, the price bracket, and every conversation with a buyer.

### Where Renovation Fits in the Framework

Now, with the framework established, let me address the question every seller asks: "What about our renovation?"

Here is what our analysis of 2,153 sales and 60 academic studies shows:

**High-return improvements (Layer 1 -- what buyers see first):**

| Action | Expected Impact | Cost Range |
|--------|----------------|-----------|
| Fresh paint (interior and exterior) | Up to 5% value increase, 25% faster sale | $2,000 - $8,000 |
| Landscaping and garden presentation | Up to 20% increase in perceived value | $500 - $5,000 |
| Professional deep clean | Significant impact on first impression | $300 - $800 |
| Decluttering and depersonalising | Measurable improvement in buyer engagement | $0 - $500 |
| Minor repairs (fences, fixtures, grout) | Removes objections that reduce offers | $200 - $2,000 |

**Low-return improvements:**

| Action | Typical ROI | The Data |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Full kitchen renovation | ~57% | A $40,000 kitchen renovation recovers approximately $22,800 in sale price |
| Full bathroom renovation | ~75% | A $20,000 bathroom recovers approximately $15,000 |
| Pool installation | 0.6-3.7% increase per sqm | A $50,000 pool adds negligible value on a per-square-metre basis |

The pattern is clear. The improvements that generate the highest returns are the ones that operate in Layer 1 -- what buyers see first. Paint, landscaping, presentation. The improvements that generate the lowest returns are the ones sellers most commonly invest in -- kitchens, bathrooms, pools.

This doesn't mean renovations are worthless. It means they operate in a different part of the framework. A beautifully renovated kitchen sits in Layer 2 -- what buyers feel. It contributes to the emotional experience of the home. But it doesn't stop the scroll. The buyer has to get past Layer 1 before they ever see the kitchen.

**The suburb exception.** One finding that warrants careful attention: the renovation return varies dramatically by suburb.

In Robina and Varsity Lakes, fully renovated properties showed *lower* price per square metre than original-condition properties. In Burleigh Waters, renovation appeared to add approximately 14% to the price per square metre.

**An important caveat:** this finding may partly reflect a floor-area effect. Larger homes tend to have lower price per square metre -- that is a well-established pattern across all suburbs -- and larger homes are also more likely to be renovated. Without controlling for floor area within size brackets, some of the apparent renovation discount in Robina and Varsity Lakes may be explained by renovated homes being larger, not by renovation itself reducing value. Further analysis is underway.

What the data does support more clearly is the suburb difference in buyer demographics. In Robina (median $1,400,000) and Varsity Lakes (median $1,224,000), buyers are predominantly families seeking value. They are often price-sensitive and plan to renovate to their own taste after purchase. At these price points, over-capitalising on renovation before sale is a genuine risk.

In Burleigh Waters (median $1,710,000), the buyer demographic skews toward established professionals and downsizers who expect turnkey quality. At this price point, buyers are less willing to project-manage a renovation. A well-renovated property in Burleigh Waters is more likely to recover renovation costs because the buyer pool values presentation completeness differently.

# Chapter 6: Choosing the Right Agent

One buyer had a top priority: watching his children ride their bikes to school. Not just "near a school" -- he wanted to stand on his front veranda and watch them pedal down the street, turn the corner, and roll through the school gate. When his agent learned about a property coming to market that would allow exactly that, the buyer drove past, loved the location, and decided to purchase before the first open home. The house needed work -- dated kitchen, original bathrooms, tired paint. None of it mattered. The ability to see his children commuting safely each morning was the single most compelling factor.

The property sold above valuation.

That story illustrates something the data confirms across 1,475 Gold Coast sales: the agents who achieve the highest prices are the ones who know their buyers deeply enough to connect the right home to the right need at the right moment. And the agents who know their buyers best are, counterintuitively, the ones who sell the fewest properties.

---

### What 1,475 Sales Reveal

Fields Estate analysed 1,475 property sales across the Gold Coast, examining the relationship between an agent's sales volume and the price premium achieved for their clients.

The results challenge one of the most deeply held assumptions in the property industry.

**Agents who sold 3-5 properties per year achieved sale prices 9.6% higher on average than agents who sold 20+ properties per year.**

On a property valued at $1,000,000, that difference equates to approximately **$96,000.**

The names on the billboards, the faces on the letterbox flyers, the agents who dominate every bus shelter and shopping centre bench -- they sell the most homes. But high volume does not equal high prices. The data is unambiguous.

Shows the 9.6% gap between low-volume high-performers and high-volume average performers. Title: "Does the busiest agent get the best price? 1,475 sales say no."]

---

### How Skilled Agents Actually Create Higher Prices

The gap between high-volume and high-performance agents is not about talent or effort. It is about time. An agent managing five listings simultaneously can invest fundamentally more time in each buyer relationship than an agent managing twenty-five.

That time investment translates into three specific capabilities.

**1. They know their buyers deeply.**

A great agent accumulates comprehensive knowledge of each buyer's situation, motivations, and limits. This goes far beyond "3-bedroom house, $1,200,000 budget." The questions that distinguish great agents from average ones are personal:

- What specific aspects of a home and location does this buyer need?
- What do their children need -- school proximity, park access, quiet streets?
- What bad experiences have they had in previous property searches?
- What emotional factors make them love this property specifically?
- What is their stretch budget -- the price they'd pay if they truly believed this was the one?

This depth of knowledge allows an agent to build emotion in a specific buyer by connecting specific features of the property to specific needs of that buyer. It enables the agent to help the buyer reach their emotional peak at precisely the right moment, and to frame the discussion around the stretch budget rather than the initial budget.

The bike-to-school buyer is the perfect example. A high-volume agent processing twenty-five concurrent listings would likely have marketed that property as "close to schools" and waited for enquiries. The agent who knew that one specific buyer's deepest priority was watching his children ride to school made a phone call, and the property was under contract before the first open home.

**2. They become the buyer's trusted source.**

A great agent works to become the person a serious buyer turns to for guidance. Not sales pressure -- guidance. At the highest level, buyers actively seek the agent's opinion on what to do next. This trust is built over weeks of honest, knowledgeable interaction, and it gives the agent significant influence over the buyer's willingness to commit and stretch.

Trust is not built by telling buyers what they want to hear. It is built by telling them the truth, consistently, including the uncomfortable parts. "This home has a smaller backyard than most in this price range, but it's one of only two properties in Robina right now with a direct line of sight to the school." That kind of honest, specific assessment builds the trust that, when the moment comes to make a difficult decision, the buyer defers to the agent's judgement.

**3. They match buyers with homes better than anyone else.**

Better-matched buyers pay higher prices. This is not theory -- it is a direct consequence of the emotional journey described in the previous chapters. When a buyer finds a home that meets a specific, urgent, personal need -- the right school zone, the right commute, the view they've dreamed about -- they are willing to pay more because the alternative is waiting months for another match.

An agent who knows that a young family needs to be within walking distance of a specific primary school, and who presents a property that meets that need when only one other comparable property exists in the area, has created a competitive dynamic that no amount of marketing can replicate.

---

### The Commission Conversation

Commission rates on the Gold Coast typically range from 1.5% to 2.75%, with the average around 2.56%.

The instinct to minimise commission is understandable. It's a significant cost on an already significant transaction. But the data reframes this question completely.

Let me work through the maths on a property valued at $1,200,000.

A seller chooses Agent A, who charges 1.5% commission -- $18,000. Agent A has a large team, handles many listings, and the property sells at the valuation price of $1,200,000. The seller nets $1,182,000.

A seller chooses Agent B, who charges 2.5% commission -- $30,000. Agent B takes fewer listings, invests more time in buyer relationships, and the data shows agents in this category achieve a 5% premium. The property sells for $1,260,000. The seller nets $1,230,000.

The seller who chose the cheaper agent saved $12,000 in commission. The seller who chose the more effective agent netted $48,000 more after commission.

**The $12,000 saving cost $48,000.**

| | Commission | Sale Price | Net to Seller |
|---|---|---|---|
| Agent A (1.5%, sells at valuation) | $18,000 | $1,200,000 | $1,182,000 |
| Agent B (2.5%, 5% premium) | $30,000 | $1,260,000 | $1,230,000 |
| **Difference** | +$12,000 | +$60,000 | **+$48,000** |

This is not to suggest that all expensive agents are good or all affordable agents are bad. Commission rate is a poor proxy for agent quality. The data that matters is the agent's track record of achieving price premiums in the relevant suburb and price range.

But the principle holds: in a transaction involving more than $1,000,000, even small differences in agent effectiveness translate into large differences in financial outcomes. A 2% difference in sale price on a $1,400,000 Robina home is $28,000. That dwarfs any commission differential.

### The Cost of Getting It Wrong

The flip side of the commission argument is the cost of an incompetent agent. Poor pricing that leads to stigma. Ineffective marketing that limits the buyer pool. Weak negotiation that accepts the first offer without testing the buyer's limits. Missed enquiries. Lack of follow-up. Poor communication that leaves the seller unprepared for decisions.

The compounding effect is significant. An agent who overprices by 8%, markets poorly, and negotiates weakly can leave $100,000 or more on the table on a $1,200,000 property. Their lower commission rate is irrelevant against that loss.

---

### What to Look For

The data points to five indicators that distinguish agents likely to achieve higher prices:

**1. They provide a valuation backed by comparable sales data.** Not an opinion. Not a promise. Not "I think we can get..." A data-backed valuation with specific comparable properties, adjustments explained, and a clear rationale. An agent who arrives at a listing presentation without printed comparable sales analysis is not prepared.

**2. They listen more than they talk.** The best agents spend the first meeting understanding the seller's circumstances, timeline, and priorities. The worst agents spend the first meeting talking about themselves.

**3. They are transparent about risks.** Every property and every market has challenges. An agent who only tells a seller what they want to hear is running the "buy the listing" playbook -- inflating the price estimate to win the listing, then applying pressure to reduce once the property is on the market and failing to attract offers.

**4. They can explain their marketing strategy in detail.** A clear plan for how the property will reach both active and passive buyers, with a budget breakdown and rationale for each channel. An agent who says "we'll put it on REA and see what happens" is signalling that marketing is an afterthought.

**5. They demonstrate buyer relationships.** An agent's network of qualified buyers is one of their most valuable assets. Asking about their current buyer pipeline for the property's price range and type is a reasonable and revealing question. The answer will tell you whether this agent knows their buyers -- or just knows their listings.

# Chapter 7: The Marketing That Actually Matters

A property in Varsity Lakes sat on the market for 47 days with a Standard listing on realestate.com.au and no social media campaign. The listing had decent photography. The price was reasonable. The agent was experienced. But after six weeks with two offers -- both below the valuation range -- the vendor switched agents.

The new agent upgraded to Premiere Plus on realestate.com.au and launched a targeted Facebook campaign within a 20-kilometre radius. Three enquiries arrived in the first week. One of those enquiries became the buyer, at $35,000 above the previous agent's recommended price reduction.

The property hadn't changed. The market hadn't changed. What changed was who saw it.

---

### The Two Buyer Pools

Every market contains two types of buyers, and most marketing strategies only reach one of them.

**Active buyers** are on realestate.com.au every day. They've set up alerts. They attend open homes most Saturdays. They know the market. They can name the median price in their target suburb. They are visibly in the market and agents know who they are.

**Passive buyers** know they want to move eventually. They have a rough idea of what they want. But they're not searching portals. They're not attending open homes. They're scrolling Facebook on Saturday morning, reading the news, driving through suburbs they like. If the right property appears in front of them -- the right combination of images, location, price, and story -- they're interested. And they're ready to act.

Research consistently shows the split:

| Market Condition | Active Buyers | Passive Buyers |
|-----------------|--------------|---------------|
| Strong market | 60-70% | 30-40% |
| Softer market | 40-50% | 50-60% |

In any market condition, at least 30% of potential buyers are passive. In a softer market, the majority are passive. A marketing strategy that only reaches active buyers -- by listing on property portals and waiting -- is leaving 30% to 60% of the potential buyer pool untouched.

Missing one motivated buyer means one less person competing for the property. One less competitor can mean $20,000, $50,000, or more in the final sale price. The financial risk of insufficient marketing is not theoretical. It is the difference between two bidders and three.

[CHART: CH7-1 — Infographic: The Buyer Pool. Two groups: Active (searching REA, attending opens) and Passive (not looking but would buy the right property). Show the 30-60% split. Title: "Up to 60% of your potential buyers aren't looking on realestate.com.au."]

---

### REA Is Essential -- And Not Enough

In Australia, realestate.com.au is the dominant property platform. It receives 3.6 times more traffic than Domain. Nearly all active buyers search on REA, making it a non-negotiable part of any marketing campaign.

The premium listing tier generates measurably better results:

| Metric | Premiere Plus vs Standard |
|--------|--------------------------|
| Email enquiries | 2.6x more |
| Listing views | 2.9x more |
| Search appearances | 2.1x more |
| Speed of sale | 10 days faster |

These are not small improvements. Premiere Plus essentially triples the property's visibility within the portal.

The cost of NOT being listed -- or being listed at the basic tier -- is quantifiable. PropTrack analysis found that Brisbane properties sold off-market (not listed on realestate.com.au at all) sold for 3.6% less than those listed on the platform. On a $1,400,000 Robina home, 3.6% is approximately $50,000.

[FIGURE: CH7-2 — Visual comparison: Standard listing vs Premiere Plus. Show the size difference in search results, with the 2.6x/2.9x/2.1x stats overlaid. Title: "What the buyer sees: Standard vs Premiere Plus."]

But REA, even at the premium tier, only reaches active buyers. The buyers already searching. The buyers who already know what suburb they want. It does not reach the family in Helensvale who hasn't considered Robina yet. It does not reach the couple in Brisbane who are thinking about the Gold Coast but haven't started looking. It does not reach the parents of a buyer who would forward the listing if they saw it on social media.

For that, the marketing needs to go where those people already are.

---

### Social Media: Reaching the Other 30-60%

There is a persistent assumption in the property industry that social media is "for young people" and therefore irrelevant to property marketing. The data says otherwise.

More than 7.5 million Australians aged 45 and over are active on Facebook. This is the demographic most likely to be buying or selling established family homes on the Gold Coast.

Australia's under-16 social media ban, effective December 2025, has further concentrated Facebook's audience in the adult demographics most relevant to property transactions.

Social media marketing for property offers several advantages over portal-only strategies:

**Geographic reach.** Unlike realestate.com.au, where buyers filter by suburb, a Facebook ad can reach buyers who haven't yet considered a suburb. A buyer searching Varsity Lakes might discover a Robina property through a targeted ad that better meets their actual needs. The data shows this cross-suburb discovery effect is real -- buyers don't always know where they want to live until they see the right property.

**Visual storytelling.** Platform algorithms favour high-quality visual content. Well-photographed properties are exactly the kind of content that performs well in social media feeds. A twilight shot of a Robina backyard with the hinterland behind it will stop a buyer's thumb just as effectively on Facebook as it does on REA -- and it reaches an entirely different audience.

**Cost efficiency.** Reaching 1,000 relevant people on Facebook costs a fraction of equivalent reach on property portals.

**Cross-generational sharing.** Younger family members frequently share property listings with parents and other decision-makers. A property that appears in a thirty-year-old's feed gets shared in the family group chat. The buyer who eventually walks through the door may be the mother, the father, or the daughter who saw it first.

Fields Estate's own advertising data confirms that property-specific content -- individual homes with specific prices and addresses -- generates 4 times more engagement than generic market content. The most effective approach is informational rather than aspirational: data and specifics outperform lifestyle imagery at a ratio of approximately 5:1.

### The Vendor-Paid Marketing Question

This is one of the most debated topics in Australian property sales. The homeowner's first instinct is usually: "I'm paying them a commission -- they should pay for advertising."

It's an understandable position. Let me walk through the maths step by step.

**Scenario: A property valued at $1,200,000. Commission: 2.5%.**

With minimal, agent-funded marketing (cost to agent: $4,000), the property sells at valuation for $1,200,000.

With comprehensive, vendor-funded marketing (cost to seller: $8,000 -- an additional $4,000), the property sells for $1,260,000 because the expanded reach generated one additional competing buyer.

Now follow the money for the additional $4,000 in marketing:

**If the agent pays the extra $4,000:**

The agent earns 2.5% on the extra $60,000 = $1,500. They spent $4,000 to earn $1,500. That is a net loss of $2,500 for the agent. No rational agent will make this investment.

**If the seller pays the extra $4,000:**

The seller receives $60,000 more from the sale. They pay $4,000 in marketing costs and $1,500 in additional commission (2.5% of the extra $60,000). Their net gain is $54,500.

| | Cost | Gain | Net |
|---|---|---|---|
| Agent pays extra marketing | -$4,000 | +$1,500 commission | -$2,500 |
| Seller pays extra marketing | -$4,000 (marketing) - $1,500 (commission) | +$60,000 sale price | +$54,500 |

The incentive structure is clear. When marketing costs are left to agents, they will rationally spend the minimum. The homeowner is the party with the most to gain from a higher sale price. Vendor-paid advertising aligns the marketing budget with that incentive.

This is not an argument for limitless spending. It is an argument for strategic spending, directed by the seller, with full visibility of what each dollar buys.

# Chapter 8: The Selling Process

Selling a home involves a sequence of steps that typically spans eight to sixteen weeks from the decision to list through to handing over the keys. For most homeowners, this is the first time -- or at most the second or third time -- they've been through this process. Not knowing what comes next is one of the biggest sources of stress.

This chapter maps the entire process so there are no surprises.

[FIGURE: CH8-1 — Timeline infographic: The Selling Process. Five phases on a horizontal timeline: Preparation (Weeks 1-3), On Market (Weeks 3-7), Negotiation (Weeks 4-8), Under Contract (Weeks 6-10), Settlement (Weeks 10-16). Clean, with key milestones marked.]

---

### Phase 1: Preparation (Weeks 1-3)

**Valuation.** The foundation. Establish what the property is worth using comparable sales analysis (Chapter 1). This figure becomes the benchmark against which every subsequent decision is measured.

**Pre-sale improvements.** The high-return items from Chapter 5: paint, landscaping, deep clean, minor repairs, decluttering. The data shows these generate the highest ROI of any pre-sale spending.

**Agent selection.** The indicators from Chapter 6: comparable sales data, listening, transparency, marketing strategy, buyer relationships. Interview at least three agents. Compare their valuations, their marketing plans, and their communication styles.

**Legal preparation.** Engage a conveyancer or solicitor before the property is listed. In Queensland, the seller needs a disclosure statement (Form 10 for houses, Form 14 for units) prepared before listing. This form discloses known defects, encumbrances, and relevant information.

Choosing a conveyancer is often an afterthought, but their responsiveness can make or break a deal. A conveyancer who takes three days to return a call during the negotiation phase can cost a seller an offer. Key questions: How quickly can they turn around a contract? Are they responsive on weekends -- when most offers come through?

---

### Phase 2: On Market (Weeks 3-7)

**Listing goes live.** Photography, floor plans, and listing copy are finalised. The property appears on realestate.com.au, Domain, social media, and any other agreed channels.

**Open homes.** The first two weeks of open homes are typically the most active. This is when the largest pool of active buyers sees the property for the first time. The data from Rossini et al. (2010) confirms this: the majority of homes sell within the first two weeks, and 10% sell in nine days or fewer.

**Buyer feedback.** Skilled agents collect and report feedback from inspections. Consistent negative feedback on a specific issue is a data point worth acting on. Isolated criticism is noise. The distinction matters.

**The critical window.** The first two to three weeks on market are when buyer interest is strongest. The preparation work in Phase 1 is designed to ensure the property makes its strongest impression during this period.

---

### Phase 3: Negotiation (Weeks 4-8)

**Receiving offers.** Offers can arrive at any time, including before the first open home. Early offers are not necessarily low offers. Buyers who make an offer in the first two weeks have often been searching for months. They've missed out on previous properties. They've calibrated their expectations against dozens of homes. When they recognise the right one, they move quickly -- and they move at their emotional peak.

**Evaluating offers.** Every offer can be evaluated against the valuation and premium price range from Phase 1. This removes emotion from the decision. An offer of $1,420,000 on a property valued at $1,380,000-$1,430,000 is a measurable outcome that can be assessed objectively, not just emotionally.

**Counter-offers and negotiation.** This is where the agent's skill is most directly tested. The buyer's emotional state, their competing options, their timeline, whether they've been pre-approved, whether they've missed out on other properties recently -- all of these factors influence how far a price can stretch.

**Conditional vs unconditional offers.** Conditional offers (subject to finance, building inspection) carry different risk profiles than unconditional offers. A slightly lower unconditional offer may represent better net value than a higher conditional offer that could collapse two weeks later.

---

### Phase 4: Under Contract (Weeks 6-10)

**Contract execution.** Once the buyer's offer is accepted, the contract is signed by both parties. In Queensland, the buyer has a 5-business-day cooling-off period for private treaty sales (unless waived). During cooling-off, the buyer can withdraw by paying a 0.25% termination penalty.

**Building and pest inspections.** Typically completed during the conditional period. Significant findings may lead to renegotiation. A reasonable approach: obtain quotes for rectification and negotiate an adjustment based on actual costs rather than assumptions.

**Finance approval.** The buyer's lender conducts their own valuation and finalises the loan. This is a common point of delay. If the bank's valuation comes in lower than the contract price, the buyer may need to renegotiate or find additional funds. This is not uncommon and experienced agents and conveyancers navigate it regularly.

---

### Phase 5: Settlement (Weeks 10-16)

Settlement day is when legal ownership transfers.

The conveyancers manage the process: documents are signed, adjustment statements balance costs (rates, water, body corporate), the buyer's lender releases funds, any existing mortgage is discharged from the proceeds, the title transfers, and the keys are collected.

**Costs at settlement:**
- Remaining mortgage balance
- Pro rata adjustments for rates and utilities to settlement date
- Conveyancer fees (typically $800 - $2,000)
- Agent commission
- Any agreed contractual adjustments

**Common questions:**

*Can the settlement date change?* Yes, with agreement from both parties. The requesting party may compensate the other for costs incurred by the delay.

*Can settlement be brought forward?* Yes, if both parties agree and finance is ready.

The property was listed on November 4.

Their agent had spent the previous two weeks preparing: the landscaping refresh, the photography sessions, the furniture repositioning on the deck, the REA Premiere Plus listing ready to go live, the Facebook campaign scheduled to launch simultaneously.

### The Emotional Arc

Beyond the practical steps, selling a home has an emotional trajectory that nearly everyone experiences in some form.

**Excitement at listing.** The decision is made, the campaign begins, the first inspections generate energy and validation. People want your home. It feels good.

**Anxiety in the middle.** If the first week doesn't produce an offer, doubt creeps in. Is the price too high? Did we choose the wrong agent? Is the market turning? This anxiety is almost universal and is usually temporary. The data shows most properties that are accurately priced and well-marketed sell within three weeks.

**Relief at contract.** When a satisfactory offer is accepted, the relief is physical. The uncertainty resolves. The number is known. The deal is done -- pending the formalities.

**Patience through settlement.** The weeks between contract and settlement feel long. The hard work is done but the transaction isn't complete. This is a waiting game managed by the conveyancer.

**Closure at handover.** Keys are exchanged, proceeds are received, and the chapter closes.

Knowing this arc is normal -- that nearly everyone feels the anxiety, that the quiet periods pass, that the relief comes -- makes the journey less isolating.

---

### Common Surprises

Even well-managed campaigns encounter complications. Knowing they are normal reduces the stress when they occur.

**The bank valuation comes in low.** Bank valuers tend to be conservative. When the buyer's lender values the property below the contract price, the buyer may need to contribute more of their own funds, renegotiate, or apply to a different lender. This is common, not a crisis.

**The building inspection finds issues.** That is the purpose of a building inspection. Minor findings -- cracked grout, worn seals, minor timber damage -- are expected in established homes and are typically not deal-breakers. Major findings may lead to renegotiation based on rectification quotes.

**The buyer's finance falls through.** A pre-approved buyer may have their formal approval declined. If the contract was conditional on finance, it may be terminated. The property returns to market. This is disappointing but not uncommon, and a well-marketed property that attracted one buyer will attract another.

**The quiet second week.** After the initial flurry, a quieter second week is normal market rhythm. The first week captures existing active buyers. Subsequent weeks capture new entrants. Consistent marketing maintains visibility.

After weeks of paperwork and waiting, the phone rings and the conveyancer says it's done. The relief is immediate. You look around the house you're leaving and you feel two things at once: sadness at what you're leaving behind, and certainty that you did it well.

# Appendix B: Why Auction Clearance Rates Don't Matter

Auction clearance rates are the most commonly quoted statistic in Australian property reporting. Every Saturday evening, the media reports what percentage of auctioned properties sold at auction. These figures are frequently used to imply market strength or weakness.

The problem: auction clearance rates are unreliable as market indicators.

### Why the Numbers Are Misleading

Research from Deakin University has identified several structural issues:

**1. Selective reporting.** Clearance rates are calculated from results voluntarily submitted by agents. Agents have an incentive to report favourable results and withhold unfavourable ones. This creates a systematic upward bias.

**2. Inflated "sold at auction" counts.** Many properties that fail to sell at auction are subsequently sold by private negotiation in the hours or days after. These are often counted as "sold at auction," inflating the clearance rate.

**3. Withdrawn properties excluded.** If a vendor withdraws their property before auction day, this result may not be counted at all, further inflating the figure.

**4. Biased sample.** Clearance rates only measure properties that went to auction -- a self-selected sample. This tells us nothing about the broader market where the vast majority of properties sell via private treaty.

On the Gold Coast, where auction rates are below 2% of total sales, the auction clearance rate is essentially irrelevant to the local market dynamic.

### What to Watch Instead

More useful indicators of market conditions:

- **Days on market trends** -- Are properties selling faster or slower than recent averages?
- **Listing volumes** -- Is supply increasing or decreasing?
- **Price-to-valuation ratios** -- Are properties selling above, at, or below comparable-sales valuations?
- **Buyer enquiry levels** -- How many enquiries per listing compared to recent benchmarks?

These metrics reflect the actual market that Gold Coast sellers participate in.

# Appendix C: What Really Drives Gold Coast Property Prices

Standard property drivers -- supply/demand balance, interest rates, new construction pipelines -- are well documented elsewhere. This appendix focuses on two factors specific to the Gold Coast that are less commonly discussed.

### Wages: The Most Predictive Indicator

Of all the factors that influence property prices, wages are the most underappreciated and one of the most predictive. Academic research by Abelson et al. (2005) found a correlation of 0.940 between wages and property prices in Australia -- they move almost in lockstep over time.

The income elasticity of 1.71x means that for every 1% increase in wages, property prices tend to increase by approximately 1.7%. When wages grow by 4% in a year, property prices tend to follow by approximately 6.8%. Tracking wage growth provides a more grounded forward signal than media headlines about auction clearance rates.

### Population Growth

The Gold Coast adds approximately 15,300 residents per year, much of it interstate migration from Sydney and Melbourne drawn by relative affordability -- a family home in Burleigh Waters at $1,710,000 is significantly below equivalent suburbs in Sydney's northern beaches or Melbourne's inner east. This sustained population growth underpins housing demand across the southern Gold Coast.
