# Vulnerable

You're feeling vulnerable because you are vulnerable. Or you have been vulnerable.

I know because it happened to me.

Seven years ago my wife and I were considering moving into a slightly bigger home. We had a baby on the way and were looking for more space.

We lived in Brisbane in a nice suburb called Corinda, just west of Indooroopilly on the other side of the river. Our house was at the top of a hill and from our bedroom, you could see the night lights of the city. It was within walking distance of the train, cafes, and a good school.

We loved our house. It was just a bit small. My wife was pregnant and it didn't have quite enough room to raise a family, so we considered selling.

First of all, I wanted to get a clear idea of what price we might achieve so I could understand better the quality of home we could move to.

House prices were expensive, terribly expensive, and we had just spent $80,000 doing renovations. We put in a pool, a carport, and back deck. We painted the house and had all-new landscaping put in by a well-renowned gardener.

The place looked great.

I started looking at comparable homes for sale and their listing prices. Given the number of bedrooms, the size of our block, and the recent renovation, it seemed our home would conservatively be worth somewhere between $870,000 to $970,000. This was 2016.

My next thoughts turned to our local real estate agent. He was very well established in the area and often had himself and his team sprawled across a massive billboard on the main route to Indooroopilly. He was a name everyone in the area was familiar with, and I really felt he was someone I could trust even though I had never met him. I wanted his opinion on what he thought our house was worth and how long it might take to sell.

I called the agency and told them what I was thinking of doing. They made the process very easy and we organised a time and date to do an appraisal.

Two days later the agent arrived with his lovely wife who also worked at the office. They were very friendly and approachable. It wasn't very long before the conversation turned to price.

"Look, you've done some great renovations here but the trouble is the market won't pay extra for them. At the end of the day, this is a small home on a small lot. We see it bringing a best price scenario of around $750,000."

I was shocked, but I tried not to show it. How could that be? We had bought the property for $675,000 in 2014. At the time it had an old tree hanging perilously across the roof, the external paint was faded, and nobody wanted it. I know I had bought it at a good price because I had it valued by an external valuer before I bought it. How could it now only be worth $75,000 more three years later and with $80,000 of renovations? The market had been solid.

What was going on? The agent's valuation just didn't seem right. At least, if it was, it was going to take more evidence to convince me. I had seen other properties on the market for much more than that. They were no different to our place. Some looked worse.

Maybe I couldn't trust that popular local agent after all.

### Agents and Underquoting

Underquoting is the practice of real estate agents deliberately listing properties with unrealistically low price guides to lure buyers in and get homes sold, fast.

Agents following this practice typically take homes to auction, where, if there are enough bidders, the property will sell at a higher price than what was underquoted. However, the property often sells for less than what the sellers had hoped for. The practice works well from the agent's perspective because it maximises interest in the property, drawing large numbers of people to auctions and improving chances of a sale at auction or shortly after.

In an effort to tackle underquoting, the state government of Queensland banned the practice of advertising price guides on homes going to auction. However, agents can still be found verbally suggesting low price ranges to both sellers and buyers to produce the same effect.

Any house listed at an attractive price on realestate.com.au will sell. That's a fact. It's why discount agents exist. Their model is simple: list the property with minimal effort, price it where buyers want it (not where sellers want it), and wait. The home sells. The agent takes their cut. Transaction complete.

That process gets homes sold. But it does not get homes sold well.

The difference between "sold" and "sold well" is tens of thousands -- sometimes hundreds of thousands -- of dollars. A property valued at $1,300,000 that sells for $1,300,000 has been sold. The same property that sells for $1,380,000 has been sold well. The gap between those two outcomes is not luck. It is the result of a specific process involving accurate valuation, strategic pricing, effective positioning, quality marketing, and skilled negotiation.

If a homeowner is prepared to sell at or below that anchor, the home will sell itself. But the goal is not to sell at the anchor. The goal is to sell above it. Achieving a premium above what buyers expect to pay requires a method -- a series of informed decisions, each backed by data, each building on the last.

# Chapter 1: What Is Your Home Actually Worth?

The first step in any property sale is understanding what your home is worth. Not what you hope it's worth. Not what an agent tells you it's worth to win your listing. What the data says it's worth.

This matters because without an accurate valuation, every decision that follows is built on a guess. The listing price becomes arbitrary. The agent's performance becomes unmeasurable. The offers you receive become impossible to evaluate objectively.

### The Three Prices Every Seller Needs to Understand

Every property has three distinct price points:

1. **Valuation Price** -- This is the price that the majority of buyers would agree represents a logical, fair market value for the property. It's determined by comparable sales data, not by hope or opinion.

2. **Listing Price** -- This is the price or price range the home is advertised at, designed to capture all relevant buyers. It sits in a strategic relationship to the valuation price.

3. **Premium Price** -- This is the price range above the logical valuation where multiple buyers compete to purchase the property. This is where skilled selling, strong marketing, and effective positioning create real financial value.

Understanding where the valuation price sits allows everything else to fall into place. Without it, there is no way to distinguish between a low offer and a fair one, between an ambitious listing price and a reckless one.

### Why Online Valuations Are Unreliable

Most homeowners begin their journey where I did: checking what the internet says their home is worth. The major platforms -- Domain, realestate.com.au, and property.com.au -- all offer automated property estimates. These figures feel authoritative. They appear precise. Banks reference similar data.

But how accurate are they?

We tested this. Fields Estate compared 1,689 automated Domain property estimates against actual sale prices across the Gold Coast. The results were striking:

**Domain overvalued 89% of properties in our sample.**

That means in nearly nine out of ten cases, the figure homeowners saw on their screen was higher than what the property actually sold for. This is not a trivial gap. Across our sample:

- Properties under $1,000,000 showed a median error of 25-34%
- Properties over $2,000,000 showed a median error of 8%
- Accuracy varied dramatically by suburb: Robina maintained 97% accuracy within the estimated range, while some Gold Coast suburbs achieved as low as 19%

The pattern is clear. Automated estimates are least reliable for the properties where most homeowners live -- standard family homes in the $800,000 to $1,500,000 range. The algorithms work better at the premium end where properties are more homogeneous, and worse where individual property differences (renovation quality, aspect, layout, condition) have the greatest impact on value.

**What does this mean for sellers?**

Consider a scenario that plays out regularly across the Gold Coast. A homeowner in Varsity Lakes checks their Domain estimate and sees $1,350,000. They've been thinking about selling, and this number feels right -- it's what they hoped for. They mention it to their partner. It becomes the number in their head. When an agent comes to appraise the property and suggests $1,220,000 based on comparable sales, they feel the agent is undervaluing their home. They choose a different agent who "confirms" the higher figure. The property is listed at $1,350,000. Four weeks pass with minimal interest. The price is reduced. The reduction signals to the market that the property was overpriced. When it eventually sells at $1,195,000 -- below the original comparable-sales valuation -- the homeowners feel they were unlucky. In reality, the mistake was made on day one, when an inaccurate online estimate was trusted over local sales data.

This is not an isolated case. Based on our analysis, there is an 89% chance that the Domain figure is too high. If they anchor their expectations to that number, they risk:

- Setting a listing price that's unrealistically high
- Rejecting early offers that are actually strong
- Sitting on the market too long and entering the stigma zone (more on this in Chapter 4)
- Choosing an agent who "confirmed" the inflated number over one who told the truth

### How Comparable Sales Valuation Actually Works

The most reliable method for determining a property's value is the same method used by licensed valuers, banks, and courts: comparable sales analysis. This is the method Fields Estate uses, and it is the method that underpins every valuation figure in this book.

The principle is straightforward. Find properties similar to yours that have sold recently in the same area, and use those sale prices as reference points for your property's value.

Here is what the process looks like in practice.

**Finding the right comparables.** The starting point is identifying 3-8 properties that recently sold near yours and are most similar to it -- same suburb, similar size, similar number of bedrooms and bathrooms, similar property type. These are your "comparables." The more similar they are, the more reliable the valuation.

For example, if you own a four-bedroom house on a 600-square-metre block in Robina, the ideal comparables would be other four-bedroom houses of similar size that sold within the last six months within a few streets of your home.

**Adjusting for differences.** No two properties are identical. A comparable property might have an extra bathroom, a larger block, or a more recent renovation. Each of these differences has a measurable effect on value. The valuation adjusts each comparable sale price to account for these differences -- adding value where the comparable is inferior to your property, and subtracting where it is superior.

The most important adjustment factor is floor area. Across our three suburbs, liveable floor area is the strongest single predictor of sale price, with a statistical correlation between 0.68 and 0.79. More liveable space consistently equals higher value.

**Weighting by quality.** Not all comparables are equally useful. A property that sold last month two streets away is a better reference point than one that sold eight months ago in a different part of the suburb. The valuation weights each comparable based on how closely it matches, how recently it sold, and how reliable the data is. The final figure is the weighted average of all adjusted prices.

**Understanding confidence.** Every valuation comes with a confidence rating -- High, Medium, Low, or Very Low. A High confidence valuation means the comparable properties closely agree on value -- there are several recent, similar sales nearby and they paint a consistent picture. A Low confidence valuation means there is significant spread, which typically occurs when a property is unusual for its area or comparable data is limited.

The confidence rating is as important as the valuation itself. A High confidence valuation of $1,250,000 means: the data strongly supports this figure. A Low confidence valuation of $1,250,000 means: this is our best estimate, but the true value could vary significantly. Knowing the difference shapes every subsequent decision.

### What Drives Value (and What Doesn't)

Our analysis of these 931 sales, combined with positioning research synthesising 60 academic studies, reveals consistent patterns about what actually affects property prices:

**The three factors that drive price the most:**

1. **Location** -- There is a 67% variance in price per square metre between suburbs, and even within a single suburb, street-level premiums can range plus or minus 40%. Two identical homes on different streets in Burleigh Waters can differ by hundreds of thousands of dollars in value.

2. **Floor Area** -- This is the strongest single predictor of sale price, with a statistical correlation between 0.68 and 0.79 depending on the suburb. More liveable space equals higher value, consistently across all three markets.

3. **Bedroom Count** -- Each additional bedroom adds between $255,000 and $607,000 to the sale price, depending on the suburb and property type.

**What does NOT significantly affect price per square metre:**

Our data shows several factors that sellers commonly believe add substantial value but do not show statistical significance:

- **Full kitchen renovation** -- In Robina and Varsity Lakes, fully renovated properties actually showed *lower* price per square metre than original-condition properties. Burleigh Waters is the exception, where renovation adds approximately 14% to the rate.

- **Kitchen finishes** -- Island benches, stone benchtops, and similar upgrades showed no significant impact on price per square metre (p > 0.1 in our analysis).

- **Swimming pools** -- Pools added between 0.6% and 3.7% to price per square metre. Pools contribute to buyer interest and lifestyle appeal -- particularly on the Gold Coast -- but they do not command a measurable per-square-metre premium over comparable properties without pools. The lifestyle value is real; the financial return on installation is not.

- **Condition scores** -- The overall condition rating of a property showed near-zero correlation with price per square metre.

- **Corner lots** -- In Robina and Burleigh Waters, corner lots actually sold at a *discount* of approximately 14%.

These findings may challenge assumptions. Many homeowners invest tens of thousands of dollars in renovations before selling, expecting a dollar-for-dollar return. The data tells a different story, which is explored in detail in Chapter 5.

**A note on corner lots:** The corner-lot discount is one of the more surprising local findings. Conventional wisdom suggests corner lots should command a premium due to their larger frontage and more prominent position. In practice, across Robina and Burleigh Waters, corner lots sell at approximately 14% less per square metre than mid-block lots. Corner lots trade private backyard space for prominent street presence and typically larger total land area. The data shows Gold Coast families value private outdoor space more heavily than street frontage, which accounts for the per-square-metre difference. For homeowners on corner lots, this context is important: the discount is a reflection of buyer priorities in these suburbs, not a quality judgement about the property itself.

**The bedroom premium varies dramatically by suburb.** In Varsity Lakes, an additional bedroom adds approximately $255,000 to the sale price. In Burleigh Waters, the same additional bedroom adds approximately $607,000. This reflects the different price ranges: at Burleigh Waters' median of $1,710,000, each bedroom represents a larger absolute value. For homeowners considering converting a study or rumpus room to a fourth bedroom before selling, this data quantifies the potential return -- and it is substantial in all three suburbs.

### Beware of Agents Who Back Inflated Price Estimates Without Data

Some agents use a tactic known in the industry as "buying the listing." They tell a homeowner what they want to hear -- a high price estimate -- to win the listing agreement. Once the property is on the market and fails to attract offers at the inflated price, the agent then applies pressure to reduce.

This is the pattern described by the Consumer Action Law Centre (Stephanie Tonkin, CEO): "The temptation is that agents who will tell an owner what they want to hear are more likely to get the listing." [Source: Consumer Action Law Centre submission to Victorian parliamentary inquiry into the real estate industry]

The data on what happens next is covered in Chapter 4, but in summary: homes that enter the market overpriced take significantly longer to sell and ultimately sell for *less* than they would have if priced correctly from the start.

A reliable agent provides a valuation backed by recent comparable sales with clear adjustments explained. An unreliable agent provides a number with no supporting evidence.

### The Valuation Is Not the Selling Price

It's worth emphasising: the valuation price is not the final selling price. It is the starting point for strategy.

A skilled selling process -- the right pricing, the right marketing, the right agent, the right positioning -- can achieve a premium above the valuation. That premium is real and measurable, as the data in the following chapters demonstrates.

But the premium is only meaningful if the valuation underneath it is accurate. A $50,000 "premium" above an inflated valuation is just the real market price. A $50,000 premium above an accurate valuation is genuine financial value created through the selling process.

# Chapter 2: When Is the Right Time to Sell?

Timing is one of the most debated topics in property. Everyone has an opinion. Agents often say "now is a good time to sell" regardless of the month. Homeowners wait for the "perfect" market. News headlines alternate between boom predictions and crash warnings.

What does the data actually say about timing?

### What 13,585 Gold Coast Sales Reveal About Seasonal Timing

Fields Estate analysed 13,585 property sales across the Gold Coast to identify whether there are genuine seasonal patterns in sale prices and selling speed.

The results are nuanced. Seasonal timing effects exist, but they vary significantly by suburb:

**Best months for highest sale prices:**

| Suburb | Best Price Month | Fastest Selling Month |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Robina | November | May |
| Varsity Lakes | April | May |
| Burleigh Waters | March | March |

These patterns emerge from years of data, not single observations. In Burleigh Waters, March consistently produces both the highest sale prices and the fastest sales -- a rare alignment that suggests strong buyer demand in the early autumn period. In Robina, the story is different: the highest prices come in November (spring), but the fastest turnaround is in May.

### The Three-Week Sweet Spot

One of the most consistent findings across all suburbs is a relationship between how quickly a property sells and the price it achieves.

Properties that sell within 15-21 days of listing consistently achieve the highest prices relative to their valuation.

This is not about rushing a sale. It reflects a pattern where well-priced, well-presented properties generate immediate buyer interest. Multiple interested buyers arrive in the first two weeks. Competition drives the price above the listing point. The transaction completes while buyer emotion is at its peak (a concept explored further in Chapter 4).

Properties that linger beyond this window face a different dynamic. After three to four weeks, the initial pool of active buyers has seen the property and moved on. New buyers entering the market see a listing that has been sitting -- and begin to wonder why. This is the beginning of market stigma, which is covered in Chapter 4.

### Days on Market Across Our Three Suburbs

The speed at which properties transact differs meaningfully between suburbs:

| Suburb | Average Days on Market |
|--------|----------------------|
| Robina | 22 days (median) |
| Varsity Lakes | 21 days (median) |
| Burleigh Waters | 31 days (median) |

*Note: Median days on market is used rather than the average, because a small number of properties that sit on the market for extended periods can skew the average significantly upward.*

Burleigh Waters properties take longer to sell at a median of 31 days. This reflects the higher price point (median $1,710,000) -- properties above $1,500,000 naturally take longer as the pool of qualified buyers is smaller. This is normal premium-market behaviour, not a sign of weakness.

Robina and Varsity Lakes move at a very similar pace (22 and 21 days respectively), with the majority of properties transacting within three weeks -- consistent with the "three-week sweet spot" discussed above.

### Life Timing vs. Market Timing

Before considering seasonal patterns and market indicators, it's worth acknowledging a reality that data-focused analysis sometimes overlooks: most people sell their home because of life, not because of the market.

A growing family needs more space. A couple whose children have left wants to downsize. A job relocation requires moving suburbs or cities. A relationship change means the family home is no longer appropriate. An inheritance creates new options. Health changes demand a different layout.

These are the real reasons most homes come to market. The idea of perfectly "timing the market" is appealing but it assumes the seller has the luxury of choosing when to sell -- and most don't. Life events set the timeline.

This is not a problem. The seasonal data in this chapter shows that the difference between the best and worst months for selling is meaningful but not dramatic -- typically in the range of 3-8% in median price variation across the year. A seller whose life circumstances dictate selling in February rather than November is not making a catastrophic error. They are making a rational decision based on the full picture of their life, not just the property market.

The seasonal data matters at the margin. It can help a seller who has flexibility choose a slightly better month to list. But it is not a reason to delay a sale that life circumstances require.

### What the Broader Data Shows About Market Timing

The academic research on market timing offers a sobering message. Predicting short-term property price movements with any reliability is extremely difficult. Consider the Gold Coast in 2022.

Between January 2021 and July 2022, Gold Coast house prices dropped significantly. In late July 2022, the Gold Coast Bulletin published forecasts from leading economists predicting further falls of 7-10%, with some suggesting as much as 15%.

Here's what actually happened: prices started recovering just weeks after those predictions were published, rose 3.6% over the following twelve months, and haven't turned back since.

The lesson from the data is not that timing doesn't matter at all. Seasonal patterns exist and are worth being aware of. But attempting to time the broader market is a gamble that research consistently shows most people get wrong.

For homeowners who are both selling and buying within the same market, the timing question largely cancels out. If the market is soft, the sale price may be lower -- but so is the purchase price of the next home. As long as the gap between selling and buying is short, the net financial impact is minimal.

### Supply Dynamics: When Fewer Homes Are for Sale

One market indicator that does show a strong relationship with sale prices is supply -- specifically, the number of competing properties currently listed in a suburb.

When there are fewer homes for sale in a market, individual properties receive more attention, more inspections, and more competitive offers. Across our three suburbs, the current active listings are:

| Suburb | Active Listings |
|--------|----------------|
| Robina | 55 |
| Varsity Lakes | 32 |
| Burleigh Waters | 37 |

These numbers are monitored continuously and updated on the Fields Estate website. The relationship between supply and price outcomes is visible in our market-metrics data: periods of low supply correspond to shorter days on market and higher price-to-valuation ratios.

To put these numbers in context: an absorption rate below 3 months is generally considered a seller's market. All three suburbs currently sit well within this threshold, with absorption rates between 1.3 and 1.8 months. This means the current stock of listed properties would sell out in less than two months at the current rate of transactions -- a strong position for sellers.

Monitoring supply is one of the most actionable market indicators available to homeowners. Unlike interest rate predictions or economic forecasts, supply is directly observable. Anyone can count the number of listings in their suburb on realestate.com.au. When that number is low relative to recent sales volume, conditions favour sellers. When it rises significantly, the competitive landscape shifts.

# Chapter 3: Auction or Private Treaty?

One of the most significant decisions in the selling process is the method of sale: auction or private treaty. Agents often have strong opinions on this, and those opinions frequently align with their own business model rather than the seller's best interest.

What does the research show?

### The Largest Study Ever Conducted

The most comprehensive study on this question was performed by the University of Sydney, covering 1.2 million home sales across Sydney and Melbourne. The research accounted for the wide array of individual differences between properties that affect price -- location, size, condition, features, and timing.

Their conclusion was definitive: **"When accounting for variables between individual properties, there is no difference in the sale price of homes sold at auction versus those sold via private treaty."**

This finding challenges the common claim from auction-focused agents that auctions "always get higher prices." Across 1.2 million transactions, when you control for property characteristics, the method of sale does not determine the sale price.

### Queensland's Unique Auction Problem

Queensland has legislation that creates specific challenges for auction sellers that don't exist in Sydney or Melbourne.

**Since May 2014, Queensland law prohibits agents from advertising price guides on properties going to auction.** Agents and auctioneers cannot offer any indication of price to prospective buyers for auction properties.

This creates two measurable problems.

**Problem 1: The majority of buyers skip properties without a price guide.**

Imagine scrolling through realestate.com.au on a Saturday morning. You see a four-bedroom home in Burleigh Waters. The photographs look good. The description is appealing. But where the price should be, it reads: "Auction." No price guide. No indication of what this property might sell for. Do you click to learn more, or do you scroll to the next listing that shows a clear price range?

Research shows that 72% of buyers scroll past. That is not a small number -- it means that an auction listing in Queensland starts its campaign visible to barely more than a quarter of the active buyer pool. Every private treaty listing with a displayed price is immediately accessible to 100% of buyers who search in the relevant price range.

This means an auction listing in Queensland starts with a fundamentally smaller buyer pool than an equivalent private treaty listing with a displayed price.

**Problem 2: Without a price guide, buyers form their own estimate -- and it's usually low.**

When buyers cannot see a price, they must come to their own conclusions about value. In the absence of professional guidance (which the legislation prevents the agent from providing), buyers tend to estimate conservatively. This is a well-documented psychological effect known as ambiguity aversion: when people face uncertainty about the value of an option, they systematically discount it. The effect was first described by Ellsberg (1961) and has been extensively studied in decision science -- Frisch and Baron (1988) found that "people have a rule of thumb to avoid options where information is missing." In property, the missing information is the price, and the avoidance manifests as lower offers or no offer at all.

Consider this from the agent's perspective. In a private treaty sale, when a buyer asks "what's it worth?", the agent can say: "Based on comparable sales, here's what similar properties have sold for. Here's what makes this property worth the asking price. Here's the data." That conversation builds value. It defends the price. It gives the buyer confidence that the price is justified.

In a Queensland auction, when the same buyer asks the same question, the agent is legally required to say: "I can't provide a price indication. Here are some comparable sales -- you can form your own view." The buyer is left to estimate value on their own -- and without professional guidance, that estimate tends to be conservative.

### What the Gold Coast Data Shows

Across our three target suburbs, the data tells a clear story about method of sale:

**Less than 2% of properties in Robina, Varsity Lakes, and Burleigh Waters sell at auction.**

This is an overwhelmingly private treaty market. The reasons align with the research: Queensland's legislation removes the key advantages of auction (competitive bidding with price transparency) while retaining all the disadvantages (shorter campaign windows, reduced buyer pools, limited price discussion).

The few properties that do go to auction in these suburbs tend to be at the premium end, where the agent is confident enough buyer interest exists to overcome the 72% skip rate. For the vast majority of family homes in the $800,000 to $2,000,000 range, private treaty is the dominant and data-supported method of sale on the Gold Coast.

### When Auction Might Make Sense

The data does not show that auction is *never* appropriate. In specific circumstances, auction may be worth considering:

- Unique properties where comparable sales are limited and the market may value the property higher than any estimate suggests
- Properties with very high demand and very low supply in their specific category
- Where the seller wants a defined timeline and is prepared to accept whatever the market delivers on auction day

These circumstances are the exception, not the rule, in our three target suburbs.

### The Cost Difference

Beyond outcomes, there's a cost consideration. Auction carries additional expenses:

- Auctioneer fees: $500 to $1,000
- Shorter, more intensive marketing campaigns (often higher weekly spend)
- If the property doesn't sell at auction, it then moves to private treaty -- having already been on the market for 3-4 weeks without a price, visible to 72% fewer buyers than a private treaty listing would have been

Private treaty has no additional method-of-sale costs. The marketing budget can be spread over a longer, more strategic campaign.

# Chapter 4: Setting the Right Price

Getting the listing price right the first time is the single most important tactical decision in the selling process. The research is unambiguous: the initial listing price determines the trajectory of the entire campaign.

### Start Too High, Sell Too Low

The most common mistake homeowners make is going to market with an inflated price.

A comprehensive study of the Sydney housing market, published in *Applied Economics*, analysed over 25,000 home sales and found two key points:

1. **Homes listed at higher prices take significantly longer to sell.**
2. **The longer a property stays on the market, the lower its eventual sale price.**

These findings were echoed in a separate study in the *Journal of Real Estate Economics*, which examined 3,490 sales of detached dwellings: "Homes that are overpriced not only take longer to sell but also ultimately sell at a lower price."

The mechanism is straightforward. When a property is priced well above what the market will pay, qualified buyers don't enquire. Without enquiries, the property doesn't sell. It sits. Over time, the current pool of buyers assumes something must be wrong with the property, the seller, or the deal. The listing becomes stigmatised.

Multiple studies confirm the damage caused by overpricing. Taylor (1999) found that properties overpriced by more than 10% above market value take 2-5 times longer to sell and develop a stigma effect that persists even after price reductions. Knight (2002) demonstrated that getting the initial price right matters more than any subsequent adjustment -- once a property enters the market overpriced, the damage to buyer perception is difficult to reverse. Anglin, Rutherford, and Springer (2003) found a direct, measurable relationship between the degree of overpricing and the eventual discount below market value at which the property sells.

When a property fails to sell, the homeowner faces two options:

1. **Lower the price incrementally** -- which often leads to a final sale price below the property's actual valuation, as the studies confirm.
2. **Withdraw and relist** -- taking the property off the market for three to four months to allow the current buyer pool to cycle through, then relisting at a more realistic price. This is a significant disruption and carries its own risks.

### Start Too Low, Sell Too Low

The opposite mistake is less common but equally damaging. Some agents advocate setting a low listing price to generate a "bidding war" that drives up the final price. The research does not support this strategy.

A study published in the *Journal of Economic Behaviour & Organisation* analysed over 14,000 transactions and found: "While the recommendations of real estate agents seem to favour underpricing, alluding to a potential herding effect, our market data does not provide any support for this strategy. In fact, underpriced homes systematically fared less well in the data we examined, even in hot markets with frequent transactions."

Research by Bucchianeri and Minson (2013), examining 14,000 transactions, confirms that underpricing does not generate bidding wars in private treaty markets. The low listing price becomes the anchor. Buyers assume it is the minimum acceptable price. Offers cluster around that anchor rather than moving significantly above it.

It is worth noting that agents have a structural incentive to recommend lower listing prices. A lower price increases the probability of a quick sale with less effort, which is financially rational for the agent even when it costs the homeowner money. A study by Levitt and Syverson (2008), analysing 98,000 transactions, found that when real estate agents sell their own homes, they achieve sale prices 3.7% higher and hold their properties on the market 9.5 days longer than when they sell comparable properties for their clients. This suggests agents apply a different -- and more patient -- strategy when their own money is at stake.

### The Optimal Listing Price

The optimal listing price attracts interest from all relevant buyers while excluding those who cannot afford the property.

**Attracts interest:** From the moment a property is listed, the goal is to establish genuine buyer interest. This is achieved through strong presentation, quality photography, and a listing price that sits just behind the valuation price -- close enough that informed buyers can see real value, which creates urgency.

**Relevant buyers:** The listing price functions as a filter. Set too low, it attracts buyers who cannot afford the property. Set too high, it excludes buyers who could and would pay the right price. The goal is to capture the attention of every buyer who has both the means and motivation to purchase the property.

### The Power of Precise Pricing

Research by Cardella and Seiler (2016) reveals a pricing strategy that consistently improves outcomes with minimal effort.

**Setting a precise listing price -- slightly above a rounded number -- generates the highest final sale prices and smallest negotiation discounts.**

Cardella and Seiler tested four pricing strategies on equivalent properties. Around a $200,000 property, the conditions were: a rounded price ($200,000), a "just below" price ($199,000), a low precise price ($198,674), and a high precise price ($201,326).

The high precise price -- precise AND above the round number -- was the clear winner. The study found: "A high precise price generates the highest final sale price, smallest percentage discount off the list price, and the largest fraction of the surplus to the seller."

**What this means in practice:** For a property valued around $1,300,000, the optimal listing price strategy would be something like **$1,315,000** -- a precise figure that sits above the round number. Not $1,300,000 (rounded, signals negotiability). Not $1,299,000 (just below, buyers see it as a marketing trick and negotiate harder -- this was the worst-performing strategy in the study). Not $1,285,000 (precise but below, leaves money on the table).

The psychological mechanism is anchoring. A precise price above the round number signals two things simultaneously: (1) the seller has done careful analysis and knows exactly what the property is worth, and (2) the price is not a starting point for negotiation downward. A round number signals approximation and invites lower offers.

### Price Brackets and Portal Visibility

There is an additional pricing consideration that many sellers and agents overlook: search brackets on property portals.

When buyers search on realestate.com.au and Domain, they set price filters -- typically in $50,000 or $100,000 increments. A property listed at $1,255,000 appears in the "$1,250,000 to $1,500,000" bracket. A property listed at $1,249,000 appears in both that bracket AND the "$1,000,000 to $1,250,000" bracket.

This means the difference between listing at $1,249,000 and $1,255,000 can be the difference between appearing in one search bracket or two -- potentially doubling the number of buyer searches in which the property appears.

Optimal bracket positioning means pricing at the top of a search bracket rather than the bottom of the next one, maximising visibility to the largest possible pool of buyers.

### Local Asking Price vs. Sold Price Gaps

Across our three suburbs, we track the gap between asking prices and sale prices. This data reveals how realistic local pricing has been:

In Burleigh Waters, asking prices recently crossed above comparable-sales valuations for the first time in our tracking period -- meaning homeowners and agents began listing properties above where the comparable-sales data suggested they should be valued. Asking prices have since fluctuated, at one point reaching 136% above valuations before correcting sharply. This volatility is a market indicator worth monitoring, as historically, persistent asking-price premiums above comparable-sales valuations have preceded periods of price moderation in other markets.

In Robina and Varsity Lakes, asking prices have more closely tracked valuation-supported levels, though individual properties regularly test the upper boundary.

### Building Emotion to Secure the Best Sale Price

Everything discussed so far -- the valuation, the timing, the method of sale, the listing price -- creates the conditions for a premium sale. But conditions alone don't close the deal. What closes the deal is buyer emotion.

This is the part of the selling process that is most often underestimated. Homeowners focus on the logical elements: the comparable sales, the price per square metre, the days on market statistics. Buyers, however, do not make their final purchasing decision based on logic alone. They make it based on how they feel about the property at the moment they write their offer.

**The Emotional Pinnacle**

When a buyer begins seriously considering a property, they embark on an emotional journey. With each inspection, each conversation with the agent, each evening spent imagining their family in the home, their emotional investment rises. This investment builds to what researchers describe as an "emotional pinnacle" -- the peak moment of commitment, where the buyer feels most certain that this is the right home for them.

[FIGURE: Illustrative view of a buyer's emotional peak and decline]

This peak cannot be sustained. It is temporary. After reaching the pinnacle, the buyer's emotional state begins to decline. Doubts creep in. They start second-guessing. They think about other options, other suburbs, other prices. The offer they would have written on Tuesday becomes the offer they hesitate about on Friday.

**Skilled agents understand this cycle.** The role of the agent is not just to find buyers and show them through the home. It is to recognise when a buyer has reached their emotional peak and to extract the best possible offer at that moment -- not before (when the buyer hasn't committed enough to make a strong offer) and not after (when doubt has begun to erode their willingness to pay).

**The Role of Visual Content in Building the Peak**

Between inspections, buyers return to the listing. They scroll through the photographs again. They show their partner the video tour. They zoom in on the kitchen, the backyard, the street view. Each time they revisit this content, they are reinforcing their emotional connection to the property -- building and sustaining the peak rather than letting it decay.

This is why the quality of photography and videography matters so much. A buyer who returns to blurry phone photos and a sparse listing description has nothing to sustain their imagination. A buyer who returns to professional twilight photography, a walkthrough video that captures the feeling of moving through the home, and drone footage that shows the park across the street and the hinterland behind -- that buyer's emotional investment deepens with every viewing.

The highest-quality visual content functions as a tool for buyers to keep justifying and building their desire to own the property. It gives them material to build a vision of what their life would look like in this home. Research in behavioural science on "elaboration" -- the process of mentally engaging with and expanding on information -- suggests that richer, more detailed content enables buyers to construct more vivid mental simulations of ownership, which in turn increases their willingness to pay and reduces their sensitivity to price.

This pattern explains why early offers frequently represent strong market signals -- the buyers behind them have often been searching longest and are at their most motivated. Buyers who make an offer in the first two weeks of a listing have often been searching for months. They have missed out on previous properties. They have calibrated their expectations against dozens of homes. When they recognise the right one, they move quickly -- and they move at their emotional peak.

Research by Rossini et al. (2010), studying over 4,500 property sales, confirmed that the majority of homes sell within the first two weeks. Of those, 10% sold in nine days or fewer. These early buyers are not bargain hunters. They are motivated, qualified, and emotionally ready to commit at a premium.

**What does this mean for pricing?**

The interplay between pricing strategy and buyer emotion is direct. An accurately priced property generates immediate interest. Multiple buyers arrive at their emotional peaks in the same window. Competition between emotionally committed buyers is what produces premium sale prices.

An overpriced property, by contrast, generates initial curiosity but no emotional commitment. Buyers inspect, compare the price to comparable sales, and move on. The property sits. The window of peak emotion passes without offers.

**Navigating Hard Markets**

Even in markets where conditions favour buyers, the emotional pinnacle still exists -- it may just be lower and reached more slowly. In these conditions, the premium above valuation may be smaller, but it is still achievable through the same mechanism: accurate pricing, effective positioning, and skilled timing of the offer process.

One pattern visible in historical data: in markets where conditions are softening, properties listed earlier in the cycle have consistently achieved higher prices than properties listed later. This is observable in retrospect but unpredictable in real time. The data shows that timing the market bottom is as unreliable as timing the top.

For homeowners who are both selling and buying in the same conditions, the mathematics often work out. A lower sale price is offset by a lower purchase price on the next home, as long as the gap between transactions is short.

Knowing what the property is worth, the strength of an offer, and the range of premium prices -- these indicators make the decision to accept or reject an offer significantly less stressful because the decision is informed, not emotional.

# Chapter 5: Positioning Your Home for a Premium

This chapter addresses one of the most common questions sellers have: what should I do to my home before listing it?

The answer from 60 academic studies and 2,153 analysed property sales is more nuanced than most advice columns suggest. Some actions consistently add value. Others are expensive wastes of money. The difference is measurable.

### What "Positioning" Means

Positioning is everything about how a property is presented to buyers. It encompasses:

- The physical presentation and condition of the home
- The quality and style of photography used in listings
- How the property's features are described and framed
- The narrative that emerges about what kind of life the property offers
- How the property relates to competitors currently on the market

Each of these elements has been studied, and each has measurable effects on buyer behaviour and sale outcomes.

### Pre-Sale Improvements: Where to Invest and Where Not To

The data is clear on which pre-sale improvements generate returns and which do not.

**High-Return Improvements:**

| Action | Expected Impact | Cost Range |
|--------|----------------|-----------|
| Fresh paint (interior and exterior) | Up to 5% value increase | $2,000 - $8,000 |
| Landscaping and garden presentation | Up to 20% increase in perceived value | $500 - $5,000 |
| Professional deep clean | Significant impact on buyer first impression | $300 - $800 |
| Decluttering and depersonalising | Measurable improvement in buyer engagement | $0 - $500 (storage) |
| Minor repairs (fences, fixtures, grout) | Removes objections that reduce offers | $200 - $2,000 |

These improvements share common characteristics: they are relatively low cost, they address what buyers notice first, and they remove reasons for buyers to discount their offer.

This is broadly consistent with Australian property expert Andrew Winter's findings and recommendations on pre-sale preparation: the improvements that generate the best returns are those that address first impressions and remove objections, not those that add new features. Fresh paint, in particular, is consistently identified across multiple sources as the single highest-ROI pre-sale action -- it is low cost, visually transformative, and signals to buyers that the property has been maintained. Landscaping operates similarly: research on "kerb appeal" consistently shows that the exterior presentation shapes buyer expectations before they enter the property, and a well-presented garden creates a perception of value that influences the entire inspection experience.

**Low-Return Improvements:**

| Action | Typical ROI | Why |
|--------|-----------|-----|
| Full kitchen renovation | ~57% return on investment | A $40,000 kitchen renovation recovers approximately $22,800 in sale price. The data shows renovated kitchens do not recover their cost in Robina and Varsity Lakes -- the price per square metre doesn't significantly increase. |
| Full bathroom renovation | ~75% return on investment | A $20,000 bathroom renovation recovers approximately $15,000. Better return than kitchens, but still a net loss on the investment. |
| Pool installation | 0.6-3.7% increase in price per sqm | A pool can cost $40,000-$80,000 to install. The price data shows it adds negligible value on a per-square-metre basis. |
| Over-renovating for the street | Negative in many cases | A home that is renovated far beyond the norm for its street can actually lose money. Buyers in that location are drawn by the area's price point. Excessive renovation pushes the asking price above what the location supports. |

The most important finding from our local data: **in Robina and Varsity Lakes, fully renovated properties showed *lower* price per square metre than original-condition properties.** Burleigh Waters is the exception, where renovation adds approximately 14% to the rate. This suburban difference likely reflects buyer demographics -- Burleigh Waters buyers are purchasing at a higher price point where renovation quality is expected, while Robina and Varsity Lakes buyers are more price-sensitive and make their own renovation decisions after purchase.

### Photography: The First 3 Seconds

Consider how buyers actually search for property. They open realestate.com.au, set their filters, and begin scrolling. Each listing appears as a thumbnail image with a price and an address. The buyer's decision to click or keep scrolling takes approximately three seconds.

In those three seconds, the photograph is the only thing that matters. The address is secondary. The price is secondary. The feature list is invisible. If the first image doesn't stop the scroll, the rest of the listing -- the carefully written description, the floor plan, the agent's insights -- is never seen.

According to research, listings with high-quality photography receive 118% more online views than those with poor-quality images. That is the difference between 100 potential buyers seeing a property and 218 potential buyers seeing it. More eyes means more competition. More competition means higher prices.

Professional property photography is not a luxury. It is the most direct link between a listed property and a buyer opening the listing to read more.

**What "professional" actually means:**

The gap between agent-phone photography and professional property photography is larger than most homeowners realise. Professional photography involves:

- **Wide-angle lenses** that accurately represent room sizes without the distortion that makes rooms look larger than they are (buyers who feel misled by photos are less likely to make offers)
- **Professional lighting** -- either natural light management or supplementary lighting that makes rooms feel warm and inviting even on overcast days
- **Twilight exterior photography** -- properties photographed at twilight (the 20-minute window after sunset) generate significantly higher engagement. The warm interior glow against the evening sky creates an emotional response that daytime exteriors do not
- **Drone or elevated photography** that shows the property in its neighbourhood context -- proximity to parks, waterways, schools, and the overall streetscape. This is particularly powerful in our three suburbs where the Gold Coast hinterland and coastline are visible from many properties
- **Virtual tours**, which research shows generate 77 seconds of engagement compared to 44 seconds for static images -- a 75% increase in time spent with the property

[PHOTOGRAPH: Comparison of agent-phone photo vs professional property photography of a similar room]

*Note: Much of the virtual tour engagement data originates from the 2020-2022 period when physical inspections were restricted. While virtual tour usage has moderated since restrictions lifted, the underlying behavioural mechanism -- the endowment effect -- is well-established in decision science and is not dependent on pandemic conditions. The trend toward richer digital property experiences (3D walkthroughs, video tours, interactive floor plans) continues to grow as buyer expectations for online property presentation increase.*

Virtual tours are particularly effective because they trigger what behavioural scientists call the "endowment effect." When buyers navigate through a home at their own pace, choosing which rooms to enter and which views to linger on, they begin to mentally take ownership. This mental ownership -- the feeling that the home is already "theirs" -- increases their willingness to pay. It is the same psychological mechanism that makes car dealerships encourage test drives: once you've sat in the driver's seat, you're harder to walk away.

**How to evaluate an agent's photography quality:**

Before signing with an agent, review their recent listings online. Look at the photography of their last 10 properties. Are the images professional quality? Are they consistently high standard, or do some listings have poor or inconsistent photos? An agent who consistently invests in quality photography for every listing -- not just the expensive ones -- is demonstrating a commitment to marketing that directly affects sale prices.

### Staging: What the Data Actually Shows

Home staging is a topic where industry opinions often diverge from the evidence.

The data shows that staged homes sell faster. Evidence also shows that staging helps buyers more clearly envision themselves living in the property, which increases emotional engagement.

However, the data does *not* clearly show that staging produces higher sale prices in all circumstances.

For properties where the existing furniture and decor are in good condition and broadly appealing, the benefit of professional staging is primarily in the listing photography and online presentation. For properties that are vacant, under-furnished, or decorated in a way that limits buyer imagination, staging can make a meaningful difference to how the property photographs and how it feels during inspections.

The decision is property-specific. A well-maintained home with contemporary furniture may not benefit from staging. A vacant investment property almost certainly will.

### The Story Your Property Tells

Every property tells a story to buyers, whether intentionally or not. The listing description, the photography sequence, the agent's language during inspections -- all of these create a narrative about what life in this home looks like.

Research into how narrative positioning affects buyer behaviour shows that properties with clear, specific lifestyle narratives generate stronger emotional responses than those with generic descriptions.

Consider the difference between:

- "4-bedroom family home with pool and outdoor entertaining area"
- "The north-facing backyard catches afternoon light until 6pm in winter. From the living room, the park across the street is visible through the spotted gums."

The first describes features. The second creates a picture of a specific life. Both describe the same property, but the second approach activates the buyer's imagination in ways that feature lists cannot.

Consider another example:

- "Renovated 3-bed home, close to schools, open-plan living"
- "The 8-minute walk to Robina State School follows the tree-lined path along the canal. The kitchen opens directly to the deck where the afternoon breeze comes through until the light fades behind the hinterland."

The first could describe hundreds of properties. The second can only describe one. A buyer reading the second description is already imagining their morning routine, their child's school walk, their evenings on the deck. They haven't inspected the property yet, but they're already forming an attachment.

This is not about exaggeration or salesmanship. It is about precision. The most effective property narratives are highly specific and factual -- they name the exact park, the exact light direction, the exact trees. Specificity creates credibility, and credibility creates trust, and trust creates willingness to pay.

### Competitive Positioning: Your Home vs. What's on the Market Right Now

One of the most overlooked aspects of positioning is competitive context. A property is not valued in isolation -- it is valued relative to the other properties a buyer is considering at the same time.

Imagine a buyer looking at three-bedroom homes in Robina between $1,200,000 and $1,400,000. On any given week, there might be 8-12 properties in that range. The buyer will inspect most of them. Each property is mentally ranked against the others: which has the best kitchen? The biggest backyard? The shortest walk to school? The most natural light?

This means a property's positioning must account for what else is currently on the market. A feature that would be unremarkable in a market with 15 competing properties might be genuinely scarce when there are only 5. "One of only two four-bedroom homes with a pool currently listed in Robina under $1,400,000" is a factual statement that creates real scarcity -- because the buyer can verify it by searching the portal themselves.

Effective competitive positioning involves:

1. **Identifying what makes the property distinctive** relative to current competition (not in general, but right now)
2. **Quantifying scarcity** where it genuinely exists -- how many properties with this specific combination of features are currently available?
3. **Framing trade-offs as value** -- every property has compromises. A smaller block near a park is not a "small block" -- it is "low maintenance with park frontage." A home on a busy road is not "noisy" -- it is "exceptionally accessible with 2-minute highway access." The framing must be honest and the trade-off real, but the perspective is within the seller's control.

A skilled agent performs this competitive analysis before listing the property and updates it as competing listings appear and disappear during the campaign. This is not marketing spin. It is strategic intelligence that informs pricing, photography emphasis, and the narrative used in descriptions and conversations with buyers.

### The Suburb Effect: Why the Same Renovation Has Different Returns

One finding from our data that warrants careful interpretation: fully renovated properties in Robina and Varsity Lakes showed *lower* price per square metre than original-condition properties, while in Burleigh Waters, renovation appeared to add approximately 14% to the rate.

**An important caveat:** This finding may partly reflect a floor-area effect. Larger homes tend to have lower price per square metre (a well-established pattern across all suburbs), and larger homes are also more likely to be renovated. Without controlling for floor area within size brackets, some of the apparent renovation discount in Robina and Varsity Lakes may be explained by renovated homes being larger rather than renovation itself reducing value. Further analysis controlling for floor area is underway.

What the data does support more clearly is the suburb difference:

**In Robina and Varsity Lakes** (median prices $1,400,000 and $1,224,000 respectively), the buyer demographic is predominantly families seeking value. These buyers are often price-sensitive and plan to renovate to their own taste after purchase. At these price points, over-capitalising on renovation before sale is a genuine risk.

**In Burleigh Waters** (median $1,710,000), the buyer demographic skews toward established professionals and downsizers who expect turnkey quality. At this price point, buyers are less willing to project-manage a renovation. A well-renovated property in Burleigh Waters is more likely to recover renovation costs because the buyer pool at this level values presentation completeness differently.

### What NOT to Do: Manufactured Urgency

One positioning approach that data shows is counterproductive -- particularly in premium markets -- is manufactured urgency.

Phrases like "Don't miss out!", "Offers closing soon!", and "Once in a lifetime opportunity!" are common in property marketing. Our data from Burleigh Waters shows that listings using urgency-style opening lines had an average of 33 days on market, compared to 20.5 days for listings using factual, data-driven openings.

Buyers in premium markets are experienced and information-rich. They recognise manufactured urgency for what it is, and it erodes trust rather than building it.

If genuine scarcity exists -- for example, "One of only three four-bedroom homes currently listed in Burleigh Waters with a pool and north-facing backyard" -- that is a factual statement worth making. If scarcity does not exist, manufacturing it damages the property's positioning.

# Chapter 6: Choosing the Right Agent

When choosing an agent, many homeowners start with the same assumption: the agent who sells the most properties in the suburb must be the best choice.

The data tells a different story.

### What 1,475 Gold Coast Sales Reveal

Fields Estate analysed 1,475 property sales across the Gold Coast, examining the relationship between an agent's sales volume and the price premium they achieved for their clients.

The results were striking:

**Agents who sold 3-5 properties per year achieved sale prices 9.6% higher on average than agents who sold 20+ properties per year.**

On a property valued at $1,000,000, that difference equates to approximately **$96,000.**

This finding challenges one of the most deeply held assumptions in the property industry. High-volume agents dominate market share through visibility and marketing spend. They are the names on the billboards and the faces on the letter-box flyers. But high volume does not equal high prices.

The data suggests a straightforward explanation, and it connects directly to the buyer emotional journey described in the previous chapter. The mechanism is buyer relationships.

### How Skilled Agents Actually Create Higher Prices

The gap between high-volume and high-performance agents is not about talent or effort. It is about time. An agent managing 5 listings simultaneously can invest fundamentally more time in each buyer relationship than an agent managing 25. That time investment translates into three specific capabilities:

**1. They Know Their Buyers Deeply**

A great agent accumulates comprehensive knowledge of each buyer's situation, motivations, and limits. This goes far beyond "3-bedroom house, $1.2M budget." The questions that distinguish great agents from average ones are personal:

- What specific aspects of a home and a location do the buyers need?
- What aspects of this particular property would be essential to them?
- What do their children need -- school proximity, park access, quiet streets?
- What bad experiences have they had in previous property searches?
- What emotional factors make them love this house specifically?
- What is their stretch budget -- the price they'd pay if they truly believed this was the one?

This depth of knowledge allows an agent to build emotion in a specific buyer by connecting specific features of the property to specific needs of that buyer. It enables the agent to help the buyer reach their emotional peak at the right moment, and to frame their offer around their stretch budget rather than their initial budget.

One buyer had a top priority: watching his children ride their bikes to school. When the agent learned about a property coming to market that would allow him to do exactly that, the buyer drove past, loved the location, and decided to purchase immediately -- even though the house itself needed work. The ability to see his children commuting safely each morning was the single most compelling factor. The house sold above valuation because the agent knew what this buyer truly valued and connected it to the right property.

**2. They Become the Buyer's Trusted Source of Information**

A great agent works to become the go-to source of advice for serious buyers, guiding them based on extensive knowledge of their situation. At the highest level, buyers actively seek the agent's opinion on what to do next. This trust is built over weeks of honest, knowledgeable interaction -- and it gives the agent significant influence over the buyer's willingness to commit and stretch.

**3. They Match Buyers with Homes Better Than Anyone Else**

Better-matched buyers pay higher prices. This is not a theory -- it is a direct consequence of the emotional journey. When a buyer finds a home that meets a specific, urgent, personal need -- the right school zone, the right commute, the view they've dreamed about -- they are willing to pay more because the alternative is waiting months for another match.

An agent who knows that a family needs to be within walking distance of a specific primary school, and who presents a property that meets that need when only one other comparable property exists in the area, has created a competitive dynamic that no amount of marketing can replicate.

The better the relationship between the buyer and the agent, the smoother the process and the higher the selling price. This is why the 1,475-sale data shows lower-volume agents achieving higher premiums: they have the time to build these relationships.

### What Drives Agent Behaviour

Understanding *why* agents adopt different approaches helps in evaluating which approach aligns with a seller's goals.

**Volume-focused agents** prioritise fast turnovers. Their business model rewards listing volume, short campaigns, and minimal per-listing investment. They often favour auction or aggressive pricing to ensure quick sales. This approach maximises the agent's annual income but does not necessarily maximise any individual property's sale price.

**Fee-focused agents** compete primarily on commission rate. Lower fees may appeal to cost-conscious sellers but often mean lower marketing budgets, fewer resources devoted to each listing, and less incentive to push for the highest price.

**Price-focused agents** build their reputation on achieving premium results. They tend to favour private treaty, invest more time in buyer relationships, and take fewer concurrent listings. Their campaigns may take slightly longer, but the data shows they achieve measurably higher prices.

None of these approaches is inherently wrong. But sellers benefit from understanding which approach their agent follows, because it directly affects the outcome of their sale.

This is not to suggest that all expensive agents are good or all affordable agents are bad. The point is that commission rate is a poor proxy for agent quality. The data that matters is the agent's track record of achieving price premiums in the relevant suburb and price range.

### The Cost of Getting It Wrong

The flip side of the commission argument is the cost of an incompetent agent. Poor pricing strategy -- whether overpricing that leads to stigma or underpricing that leaves money on the table -- directly reduces the sale price. Ineffective marketing limits the buyer pool. Weak negotiation skills result in accepting lower offers. Missed enquiries and lack of follow-up lose motivated buyers. Poor communication leaves the seller uninformed and unprepared for decisions.

The compounding effect of these failures is significant. An agent who overprices by 8%, markets poorly, and negotiates weakly can leave $100,000 or more on the table on a $1,200,000 property. Their lower commission rate is irrelevant against that loss.

Competency creates value. In a transaction involving $1,000,000 or more, even small differences in competency translate into large differences in financial outcomes.

# Chapter 7: The Marketing of Your Home

The key success factor in achieving a premium price is having at least two -- and ideally as many people as possible -- compete passionately for the right to own the property.

To achieve this, every potential buyer in the market needs to know the property is for sale. Marketing is the mechanism that makes this happen, and the data shows it has a direct, measurable impact on sale prices.

### Active vs. Passive Buyers

Every market contains two types of buyers:

**Active buyers** are actively searching on realestate.com.au, attending open homes, and engaging with agents. They are visibly in the market.

**Passive buyers** know they want to move and have an idea of what they want, but aren't actively searching. However, if the right property appears in front of them, they're interested and ready to act.

Research consistently shows that passive buyers represent a significant portion of any market:

| Market Condition | Active Buyers | Passive Buyers |
|-----------------|--------------|---------------|
| Strong/Hot Market | 60-70% | 30-40% |
| Weak/Cool Market | 40-50% | 50-60% |

In any market condition, at least 30% of potential buyers are passive. A marketing strategy that only reaches active buyers -- by listing only on property portals, for example -- is leaving 30-60% of the potential buyer pool untouched.

The financial risk is real. Missing one motivated buyer means one less person competing for the property. One less competitor can mean tens of thousands of dollars in difference to the final sale price.

### Realestate.com.au: Essential but Expensive

In Australia, realestate.com.au is the dominant property platform. It receives 3.6 times more traffic than Domain. Nearly all active buyers search on realestate.com.au, making it a non-negotiable part of any marketing campaign.

The premium listing tier (Premiere Plus) generates measurably better results:

- 2.6x more email enquiries compared to a Standard listing
- 2.9x more views compared to a Standard listing
- 2.1x more search result appearances compared to a Standard listing
- Sells 10 days faster compared to a Premiere listing alone

However, realestate.com.au pricing is dynamic and expensive. Listing costs scale with property value, and premium positioning carries significant fees. This is why marketing budget allocation matters -- and why the seller's understanding of what they're paying for is critical.

**The cost of NOT being on realestate.com.au is significant.** Recent PropTrack analysis found that Brisbane properties sold off-market (not listed on realestate.com.au) sold for 3.6% less than those listed on the platform. On a $1,200,000 home, that equates to approximately $43,200 in lost value -- far exceeding the cost of a premium listing.

### Social Media: Reaching Passive Buyers

Social media -- particularly Facebook and Instagram -- is the most cost-effective channel for reaching passive buyers.

There is a common assumption that social media is "for young people" and therefore irrelevant to property marketing. The data says otherwise. In Australia, the Facebook user base breaks down as follows:

| Age Group | Active Users |
|-----------|-------------|
| 13-17 years | 820,000 |
| 18-24 years | 4,000,000 |
| 25-34 years | 5,100,000 |

*(Source: Australian Facebook Demographics analysis, October 2023, Genroe. Note: These figures have likely grown since this analysis -- Meta's global user base has continued to expand, with the 45+ demographic being the fastest-growing segment across multiple markets.)*

More than 6.9 million active Facebook users are aged 45 and over. This is the demographic most likely to be buying or selling established family homes on the Gold Coast. Dismissing social media as a marketing channel means dismissing a platform that reaches the exact demographic most likely to be your buyer.

Social media marketing for property offers several advantages:

- **Broad geographic reach.** Unlike realestate.com.au, where buyers filter by suburb, social media can reach buyers who haven't yet considered a suburb. A buyer searching Varsity Lakes might discover a Robina property through a targeted ad that better meets their needs.

- **Visual storytelling.** Platform algorithms favour high-quality visual content -- exactly what well-photographed properties provide.

- **Cost efficiency.** Reaching 1,000 relevant people on social media costs a fraction of equivalent reach on property portals.

- **Younger generation sharing.** Younger family members frequently share property listings with parents and other decision-makers, extending the property's reach beyond the direct audience.

This broader reach matters because buyers don't always know where they want to live until they see the right property. A family searching exclusively in Varsity Lakes might discover a Robina home through a Facebook ad that happens to match their actual needs -- the right number of bedrooms, closer to the husband's workplace, near the school they didn't know existed. That buyer would never have found the property through a realestate.com.au search filtered to Varsity Lakes only. Social media put the property in front of them anyway.

Fields Estate's own advertising data confirms that property-specific content -- individual homes with specific prices and addresses -- generates 4 times more engagement than generic market content. The most effective approach is informational rather than aspirational: data and specifics outperform lifestyle imagery at a ratio of approximately 5:1.

### Marketing Budget Allocation

For a typical property sale on the Gold Coast in 2026, marketing costs include:

- **Photography:** $300 - $800 for a professional package (essential)
- **REA Premium Listing:** Variable by property value, typically $1,500 - $4,000
- **Social media advertising:** $500 - $2,000 for a targeted campaign
- **Signage and print collateral:** $300 - $800
- **Staging (if appropriate):** $2,000 - $5,000
- **Drone/twilight/virtual tour:** $300 - $600

A reasonable marketing budget guideline is approximately 1% of the property's value, though the specific allocation depends on the property type, target buyer, and competitive landscape. This budget is an investment, not a cost -- the data shows that well-marketed properties achieve significantly higher sale prices than those with minimal marketing.

### Who Pays for Marketing: Vendor-Paid vs. Agent-Paid

This is one of the most debated questions in Australian property sales. The homeowner's first instinct is usually: "I'm paying them a commission -- they should pay for advertising." It's an understandable position. Let's work through the maths.

**Scenario: A home with a valuation of $1,000,000. Commission rate: 2.5%.**

**With cheap, minimal advertising** (agent-funded, cost: $4,000), the home sells for $1,000,000. The agent earns $25,000 in commission and spent $4,000 on advertising. Agent's net: $21,000.

**With strong, comprehensive advertising** (better photography, REA premium, social media campaign, cost: $8,000), the same home sells for $1,200,000 because the marketing reached more buyers and generated competitive interest.

Now consider who benefits from the extra $4,000 in marketing spend:

**If the agent bears the extra cost:**

- Commission on extra $200,000: $5,000
- Extra advertising cost: -$4,000
- **Agent's net gain: $1,000**

The agent invested an extra $4,000 in effort and risk to earn an extra $1,000. This is not a compelling business case. A rational agent will choose to spend less on advertising and accept a slightly lower sale price -- because the lower sale price barely affects their income.

**If the homeowner bears the extra cost:**

- Extra sale price: $200,000
- Extra advertising cost: -$4,000
- Extra commission on the premium: -$5,000
- **Homeowner's net gain: $191,000**

The homeowner invested $4,000 to earn $191,000. This is an overwhelming return.

| | Agent's Gain | Homeowner's Gain |
|---|---|---|
| Agent pays, cheap marketing | Saves $4,000 | Loses up to $200,000 |
| Vendor pays, strong marketing | $5,000 more commission | $191,000 more net proceeds |

The incentive structure is clear. If marketing costs are left to agents, they will rationally spend the minimum. The Australian real estate industry has this right: vendor-paid advertising is in the homeowner's best interests, because the homeowner is the party with the most to gain from a higher sale price.

Vendor-paid advertising aligns the marketing budget with the person who has the most to gain from higher sale prices: the seller.

# Chapter 8: The Selling Process

Selling a home involves a sequence of steps that typically spans 8-16 weeks from the decision to list through to handing over the keys. For most homeowners, this is the first time -- or at most the second or third time -- they've been through this process. Not knowing what comes next is one of the biggest sources of stress.

This chapter maps the entire process so there are no surprises. Every stage has a predictable rhythm, common challenges, and known solutions. The goal is not to eliminate the emotional weight of selling a home -- it is a significant life event -- but to ensure that uncertainty about the process itself is not adding to that weight.

[PHOTOGRAPH: Timeline infographic -- decision to settlement in 10-16 weeks]

### Phase 1: Preparation (Weeks 1-3)

**Property assessment and valuation.** The first step is establishing what the property is worth using comparable sales analysis as described in Chapter 1.

**Pre-sale improvements.** The ROI data in Chapter 5 identifies which improvements show measurable returns (paint, landscaping, deep clean, minor repairs, declutter) and which do not (full kitchen renovations, bathroom renovations, pool installation).

**Agent selection.** Chapter 6 covers the data on what distinguishes agents who achieve price premiums from those who don't, including the specific indicators visible in their track records and the questions that reveal their approach.

**Legal preparation.** Engage a conveyancer or solicitor early -- ideally before the property is listed. In Queensland, the seller needs a disclosure statement (Form 10 for houses, Form 14 for units) prepared before listing. This form discloses known defects, encumbrances, and relevant information about the property.

Choosing a conveyancer is often an afterthought, but their role is significant. They prepare the contract, manage the disclosure requirements, handle the settlement process, and protect the seller's legal interests throughout. Fees typically range from $800 to $2,000, and the quality of the service can make a material difference to the smoothness of the transaction.

Key questions for a conveyancer: How quickly can they prepare the contract? Are they responsive on weekends (when most offers come through)? Do they have experience with properties in the seller's suburb? A conveyancer who takes three days to return a call can cost a seller an offer.

### Phase 2: On Market (Weeks 3-7)

**Listing goes live.** Photography, floor plans, and listing copy are finalised. The property appears on realestate.com.au, Domain, social media, and any other agreed channels.

**Open homes and private inspections.** The first two weeks of open homes are typically the most active. This is when the largest pool of active buyers sees the property for the first time.

**Buyer feedback.** Skilled agents collect and report feedback from inspections. Consistent negative feedback on a specific issue is a data point worth acting on. Isolated criticism is noise.

**The critical window.** As discussed in Chapter 2, properties that sell within 15-21 days achieve the highest prices. The preparation work done in Phase 1 is designed to ensure the property makes its strongest impression in this window.

### Phase 3: Negotiation (Weeks 4-8)

**Receiving offers.** Offers can come at any time, including before the first open home. Early offers are not necessarily low offers -- buyers may have been searching for months and recognise value immediately.

Research by Rossini et al. (2010), studying over 4,500 property sales, found that the majority of homes sell within the first two weeks on the market. 10% sold in nine days or less. Buyers who have been in the market for months often know exactly what they want and move quickly when they find it.

**Evaluating offers.** Every offer can be evaluated against the valuation and premium price range established in Phase 1. This removes emotion from the decision. An offer at $1,260,000 on a property valued at $1,220,000 is a measurable premium of 3.3%, which can be compared against average premiums in the suburb.

**Counter-offers and negotiation.** Negotiation is where the agent's skill is most directly tested. The buyer's emotional state, their competing options, their timeline -- all of these factors influence how far a price can stretch.

**Conditional vs. unconditional offers.** Conditional offers (subject to finance, building inspection, or other conditions) carry different risk profiles than unconditional offers. A slightly lower unconditional offer may represent better net value than a higher conditional offer that could collapse.

### Phase 4: Under Contract (Weeks 6-10)

**Contract execution.** Once a buyer's offer is accepted, the contract is signed by both parties. In Queensland, the buyer has a 5-business-day cooling-off period for private treaty sales (unless waived). During cooling-off, the buyer can withdraw by paying a 0.25% termination penalty.

**Building and pest inspections.** Typically completed during the conditional period. Significant findings may lead to renegotiation or, in some cases, the buyer exercising their conditions to withdraw.

**Finance approval.** The buyer's lender conducts a valuation and finalises the loan. This is a common point of delay or collapse -- if the bank's valuation comes in lower than the contract price, the buyer may need to renegotiate or find additional funds.

### Phase 5: Settlement (Weeks 10-16)

Settlement day is when the legal transfer of property ownership takes place.

**What happens:**
- All sale documents are signed by both parties' legal representatives and the buyer's lender
- Adjustment statements are prepared to balance costs such as council and water rates
- The buyer's lender releases funds to complete the purchase
- Any existing mortgages on the property are paid from the sale proceeds
- The title is transferred to the buyer
- The buyer coordinates with the agent to collect the keys

**Costs covered at settlement:**
- Remaining mortgage balance
- Pro rata adjustments for rates, utilities, or body corporate fees up to settlement date
- Conveyancer or solicitor fees (typically $800 - $2,000)
- Real estate agent commission
- Any agreed contractual obligations

**Common questions:**

*Can the settlement date be changed?* Yes, with agreement from both parties. The requesting party may need to compensate the other for additional costs incurred by the delay (interest, storage, temporary accommodation).

*Can settlement be brought forward?* Yes, if both parties agree and the buyer's finance is ready. This requires the conveyancer or solicitor to update the contract.

*What happens to adjustments if settlement is delayed?* All adjustments (rates, water, body corporate) are recalculated based on the actual settlement date. The conveyancer handles this to ensure each party only pays for their period of ownership.

### Common Surprises and How They're Handled

Even well-managed campaigns encounter complications. Knowing that these are normal -- and that solutions exist -- reduces the stress when they occur.

**The bank valuation comes in low.** When the buyer's lender sends their own valuer to assess the property, the bank's figure sometimes comes in below the contract price. This doesn't mean the property is overpriced -- bank valuers tend to be conservative. When this happens, the buyer may need to contribute more of their own funds, renegotiate the price, or in some cases, apply to a different lender. This is a common occurrence, not a crisis, and experienced agents and conveyancers navigate it regularly.

**The building inspection finds issues.** Building and pest inspections frequently identify issues -- that is their purpose. Minor findings (cracked grout, worn seals, minor timber damage) are expected in established homes and are typically not deal-breakers. Major findings (structural issues, significant termite damage, roofing problems) may lead to renegotiation. A reasonable approach is to obtain quotes for rectification and negotiate an adjustment based on actual costs rather than assumptions.

**The buyer's finance falls through.** This is the most disruptive common complication. A buyer who was pre-approved may have their formal approval declined -- due to changes in their circumstances, the bank's assessment, or lending policy changes. If the contract was conditional on finance and the buyer cannot obtain approval, the contract may be terminated. The property then returns to market. This is disappointing but not uncommon, and a well-marketed property that attracted one buyer will typically attract another.

**The quiet second week.** After the initial flurry of inspections and enquiries in the first week, many campaigns experience a quieter second week. This is normal market rhythm, not a sign of failure. The first week captures existing active buyers. Subsequent weeks capture new buyers entering the market. Consistent marketing maintains visibility and ensures the property continues to reach new audiences.

### The Emotional Arc of Selling

Beyond the practical steps, selling a home has an emotional trajectory that most homeowners experience in some form:

**Excitement at listing** -- the decision is made, the campaign begins, the first inspections generate energy and interest.

**Anxiety in the middle** -- if offers don't arrive immediately, doubt sets in. Am I priced too high? Did I choose the right agent? Is the market turning? This anxiety is almost universal and is usually temporary.

**Relief at contract** -- when a satisfactory offer is accepted, the relief is profound. The uncertainty resolves.

**Patience through settlement** -- the weeks between contract and settlement feel long, because the hard work is done but the transaction isn't complete. This is a waiting game, managed by the conveyancer.

**Closure at handover** -- keys are exchanged, the proceeds are received, and the chapter closes.

Knowing that this emotional arc is normal -- that nearly everyone feels the anxiety, that the quiet periods pass, that the relief comes -- makes the journey less isolating.

You now have the information to make every decision in this process from a position of knowledge rather than uncertainty. The next chapter puts all of this into context with the current state of your specific market.

# Appendix B: Why Auction Clearance Rates Don't Matter

Auction clearance rates are the most commonly quoted statistic in Australian property reporting. Every Saturday evening, the media reports what percentage of auctioned properties sold at auction. These figures are frequently used to imply market strength or weakness.

The problem: auction clearance rates are unreliable as market indicators.

### Why the Numbers Are Misleading

Research from Deakin University has identified several structural issues with how auction clearance rates are calculated and reported:

**1. Not all results are reported.** Clearance rates are calculated from results voluntarily submitted by agents. Agents have an incentive to report favourable results and withhold unfavourable ones. This creates a systematic upward bias in the reported figures.

**2. "Sold at auction" includes post-auction sales.** Many properties that fail to sell at the auction event are subsequently sold by private negotiation in the hours or days after. These are often counted as "sold at auction" in the reported statistics, inflating the clearance rate.

**3. Properties withdrawn before auction are often excluded.** If a vendor withdraws their property before auction day (perhaps because insufficient interest was generated), this result may not be counted in the clearance rate at all, further inflating the figure.

**4. The sample is biased.** Clearance rates only measure properties that went to auction -- a self-selected sample of properties where agents believed auction was the best strategy. This tells us nothing about the broader market where the vast majority of properties sell via private treaty.

### What Clearance Rates Actually Indicate

At best, auction clearance rates are a very rough indicator of buyer confidence among the subset of buyers active at auctions. They do not measure:

- Whether sellers achieved their desired price
- How the same properties would have performed via private treaty
- Overall market direction
- Conditions in private treaty markets (which represent more than 98% of sales in our target suburbs)

On the Gold Coast specifically, where auction rates are below 2% of total sales, the auction clearance rate is essentially irrelevant to the local market dynamic.

### What to Watch Instead

More useful indicators of market conditions include:

- **Days on market trends** -- Are properties selling faster or slower than the recent average?
- **Listing volumes** -- Is supply increasing or decreasing?
- **Price-to-valuation ratios** -- Are properties selling above, at, or below their comparable-sales valuations?
- **Buyer enquiry levels** -- How many enquiries per listing compared to recent benchmarks?

These metrics are tracked across all sale methods and reflect the actual market that Gold Coast sellers participate in.

# Appendix C: What Really Drives Gold Coast Property Prices

Property prices are determined by the interaction of supply, demand, and broader economic conditions. Understanding these drivers provides context for how the current market is behaving and what signals might indicate change.

### Supply and Demand

The most fundamental driver is the balance between the number of buyers and the number of properties available. When there are more buyers than properties (low supply, high demand), prices tend to increase and days on market decrease. When the reverse is true, prices moderate and properties take longer to sell.

Across our three target suburbs, the current supply levels are:

| Suburb | Active Listings | 12-Month Sales Volume |
|--------|----------------|---------------------|
| Robina | 55 | 357 |
| Varsity Lakes | 32 | 302 |
| Burleigh Waters | 37 | 272 |

These figures imply absorption rates (how quickly the current stock would sell at current sales velocity) of approximately 1.8 months for Robina, 1.3 months for Varsity Lakes, and 1.6 months for Burleigh Waters. An absorption rate below 3 months is generally considered a seller's market. All three suburbs are well within this threshold.

### Population Growth

The Gold Coast continues to experience strong population growth, adding approximately 15,300 residents per year. This population growth underpins housing demand and has been a consistent feature of the market for over a decade.

### Interest Rates

The Reserve Bank of Australia's cash rate directly affects borrowing capacity. As of early 2026, the cash rate sits at 3.60%. Changes in interest rates affect how much buyers can borrow, which in turn affects how much they can offer on a property.

Rate rises reduce borrowing capacity and tend to moderate prices. Rate cuts increase borrowing capacity and tend to support price growth. However, the relationship is not immediate -- there is typically a 6-12 month lag between rate changes and visible impacts on property prices.

### Wages and Income

Of all the factors that influence property prices, wages are the most underappreciated -- and one of the most predictive.

Academic research by Abelson et al. (2005) examined the long-run relationship between income and property prices in Australia and found a correlation of 0.940. To put that in context, a correlation of 1.0 would mean perfect alignment. At 0.940, wages and property prices move almost in lockstep over time.

The income elasticity of 1.71x is equally significant. This means that for every 1% increase in wages, property prices tend to increase by approximately 1.7%. If wages grow by 4% in a year, property prices tend to grow by approximately 6.8% over the same period or shortly after.

This relationship makes wages one of the more reliable forward indicators for property price direction. When wages are rising strongly -- as they have been across Queensland's healthcare, education, and professional services sectors -- property prices tend to follow. When wage growth stalls, property price growth typically moderates within 6-12 months.

For homeowners considering when to sell, tracking wage growth provides a more grounded signal than media headlines about interest rates or auction clearance rates.

### New Supply Pipeline

The volume of new residential construction in an area affects future supply. When new housing completions increase significantly, additional supply can moderate price growth. When the construction pipeline slows, reduced future supply supports existing property values.

On the Gold Coast, the apartment construction pipeline has been declining sharply. This is significant for homeowners in established suburbs. When apartment supply drops, some buyers who would have purchased new apartments redirect toward established homes -- increasing demand in suburbs like Robina, Varsity Lakes, and Burleigh Waters without any corresponding increase in supply.

For homeowners in established suburbs with limited vacant land, this supply dynamic is particularly relevant. Burleigh Waters, for example, is almost entirely built out. There is virtually no new land to develop. Every sale comes from an existing homeowner deciding to sell. This means supply is constrained by life decisions (moving, downsizing, divorcing, inheriting) rather than developer activity -- and it means that supply is unlikely to surge suddenly, providing a structural floor under prices.

Robina has marginally more development capacity in its southern sections, and Varsity Lakes has some remaining apartment sites around the lake. But in all three suburbs, the era of large-scale new development is largely complete, and the supply of established homes is essentially fixed.

### Local Infrastructure and Amenity

Changes to local infrastructure -- new schools, transport connections, retail developments, or public amenity improvements -- can affect the relative attractiveness of a suburb and therefore its property values. These effects tend to be gradual but persistent.

For our three suburbs specifically:

**Robina** benefits from ongoing investment in the Town Centre precinct, Robina Hospital expansion, and the light rail network that connects to the broader Gold Coast corridor. The proximity to Bond University creates a secondary rental demand that supports property values even in softer owner-occupier markets.

**Varsity Lakes** has benefited from maturing infrastructure -- the suburb was relatively new when it was first developed, and amenity has improved as the community has established itself. The lake and park network, the retail precinct on Varsity Parade, and the walkability of the suburb's core areas are now established assets rather than planning promises.

**Burleigh Waters** draws value from its proximity to Burleigh Heads -- the beach, the national park, and the cafe strip at James Street. While Burleigh Waters itself is residential, its value is inseparable from the lifestyle amenity of adjacent Burleigh Heads. Any investment that enhances Burleigh Heads' amenity (improved beach access, streetscaping, parking) indirectly supports Burleigh Waters property values.

### The Gold Coast's Structural Advantage

One macroeconomic factor worth noting is the Gold Coast's position in Australia's internal migration patterns. The Gold Coast has been a net recipient of interstate migration for over a decade, with population growth of approximately 15,300 residents per year. Much of this migration comes from Sydney and Melbourne, where families are drawn by the relative affordability (a Burleigh Waters family home at $1,710,000 is significantly below equivalent suburbs in Sydney's northern beaches or Melbourne's inner east), the climate, and the lifestyle.

This migration creates persistent demand that is largely independent of local economic cycles. Even in years when the Gold Coast economy softens, interstate arrivals continue to purchase property. For homeowners in desirable suburbs with established infrastructure, this has historically supported property values in desirable suburbs with established infrastructure.
