How to read this reportThe Fields Quarterly · Q1 2026

How to read this report.

Three minutes here will save you fifteen later. Plain-English explanations of the four numbers we lean on most. The technical names are in brackets — for the readers who want them.

The Fields Conviction Index (98.6 this issue)

One number we publish every quarter. We've designed it so that 100 means "a typical southern Gold Coast quarter pre-COVID". Anything above 100 means tighter than that — sellers in charge. Below 100 means looser — buyers in charge. This issue: 98.6.

We build it from three measurable things: prices, sales volume, and how long homes take to sell. Each is converted to a standard score against five years of its own history, then weighted and combined.

Formal: a weighted composite of indexed median price (40%/50%), latest-quarter sales-volume z-score (20%/25%), and inverse days-on-market z-score (20%/25%), rebased Q1 2020 = 100.

The rolling 12-month median

The middle sale price across the last twelve months. We prefer it to a single-quarter median because twelve months gives us a sample of 80-200 sales per suburb; a single quarter gives us 20-50. One $5M sale moves a small sample dramatically; the 12-month series barely flinches.

When we report a "year-on-year" growth number, this is what we compare to the same number a year ago.

Formal: median of all arms-length house transactions over the trailing 12 months, recomputed monthly.

The composite

When we say "the southern Gold Coast composite", we mean Robina, Burleigh Waters and Varsity Lakes combined, weighted by how many transactions each suburb produced. So Robina pulls more weight than Varsity Lakes (it has more sales), but no suburb is left out.

The composite is for the broad picture. The suburb pages are where individual stories live.

Formal: transaction-weighted aggregate of the three core suburbs.

Sample size and what we don't trust

Every chart in this report cites how many sales went into it. Below 30 sales in a quarter, we flag the number as small-sample and don't make strong claims off it. The rolling 12-month series is what we trust at suburb level; the single-quarter prints are colour and texture only.

We also flag when our data sources disagree, and we don't resolve disagreements by picking a winner — we report the gap and explain it.

Formal: minimum N≥30 for quarterly reporting, N≥100 for confidence-banded statistics.

Full methodology is on page 23. There is also a one-glance glossary on the inside back cover.

Editor's LetterThe Fields Quarterly · Q1 2026

The standoff.

Two facts that should not sit in the same paragraph: median house prices on the southern Gold Coast rose between 7.0% and 18.6% year-on-year by suburb in Q1 2026. The Fields Conviction Index for the same three suburbs combined (the composite) closed at 98.6 — just below the five-year baseline of 100.

That gap is the editorial subject of this first issue. Headline prices are loud and rising; underneath, the picture is uneven. Robina volume was essentially on its 5-year trend; Varsity Lakes was a touch below; Burleigh Waters volume came in 35.7% below its long-term Q1 norm. The few houses that sold sold close to their asking price. The houses that did not sell sat. The market is in a state where the loudest data point and the underlying signal of buyer-and-seller conviction are pointing in opposite directions.

Whichever way Q2 and Q3 resolve — sellers capitulating first, or buyers returning first — the answer reframes the year. The conditions under which each scenario unfolds are the work of the next twenty-five pages.

I believe decisions are better made with data, and that property markets — where people are making some of the biggest decisions of their lives — are severely lacking in essential decision-making data. This issue does not tell you what to do. It tells you what is, what we measure, what we admit we cannot yet measure, and which signals we are watching. The Fields Conviction IndexThe Fields Quarterly · Q1 2026

98.6 — just below baseline, marginal cooling tilt.

The composite Fields Conviction Index for the southern Gold Coast finished Q1 2026 at 98.6, against a five-year baseline of 100. Within the index's nine-year history, this reading sits close to the long-run average — much closer to "typical" than to either the post-COVID peak (116) or the post-rate-hike trough (92).

The Conviction Index sits at 98.6 — just below the five-year baseline of 100. Within the index's history this is a balanced reading with a marginal cooling tilt: not far enough below to call a turn, not above the line either. The composite has been higher (the post-COVID peak in 2021 lifted it to 116) and lower (the post-rate-hike trough in 2022 took it to 92). 98.6 is much closer to "typical" than to either extreme.

What drives the current reading is the gap between the three components. Prices, after adjusting for the mix of houses sold (the indexed price), remain firmly above their five-year trend. Sales volume has softened, particularly in Burleigh Waters. Median time-to-sell has lengthened. The price line and the conviction line have separated — not dramatically, but visibly.

Section 01

The Standoff

When the price line and the conviction line separate, what does the data say happens next? It depends.

The StandoffThe Fields Quarterly · Q1 2026

When the price line and the conviction line separate

The two lines tracked closely through the first three quarters of 2023 — both staying within ~1.5 points of one another. They began separating in Q4 2023, when the indexed median pulled ahead of the Conviction Index by seven points. The gap widened progressively: ~18 points by Q4 2024, ~30 points by Q3 2025, peaking near 43 points in Q4 2025. Q1 2026 closed with the indexed median at 138.4 — 38 points above its early-2023 level — while the Conviction Index sat at 98.9, fractionally below the 100 baseline.

The data behind the standoff:

  • Sales volume. 105 transactions across the three suburbs combined in Q1 2026, against a five-year same-quarter average of approximately 127 — a 17% shortfall. Burleigh Waters carried the largest share (30 sales versus a 5-year average of 46.7, 35.7% below). Robina came in on trend (54 versus 53.2). Varsity Lakes was 25% below the prior year but only marginally below its 5-year average.
  • Prices. Rolling 12-month medians by suburb: Robina $1,450,000 (+7.0% year-on-year), Burleigh Waters $1,840,000 (+8.2%), Varsity Lakes $1,400,000 (+18.6%). Across the three combined, growth runs at approximately +10.5% — well above the 5-year average annual growth of 6.7%.
  • Time-to-sell (days-on-market). 27 days median across the three suburbs, lifted from a 5-year median of 22.8 days. Robina and Varsity Lakes contribute most of the lift; Burleigh Waters is essentially in line with its historical median.
  • The Fields Conviction Index. 98.6 for the three combined. Robina at 97.9, Burleigh Waters at 96.7; Varsity Lakes the only suburb above the 100 baseline at 103.1.

The thing the data does not say: which side breaks first. Two scenarios resolve a standoff. The first: stock-on-market keeps shrinking (sellers withholding) while transaction volume picks up — buyers absorbing the scarce listings. Historically that combination has preceded renewed price acceleration. The second: transaction volume picks up because sellers capitulate on price — same volume recovery, but with prices easing rather than accelerating. The Q2 data will likely distinguish the two.

Between now and the August edition we watch four numbers: how much stock sits on market (stock-on-market), what share of asking price vendors actually achieve (sale-to-list ratio), what share of listings get pulled within 60 days, and how lenders respond to the new APRA debt-to-income lending cap. Each will be reported next quarter.

The two outcomes look like opposites. They are not. The pattern repeats across the three core suburbs. The reward for accurate pricing has rarely been higher; the cost of inaccurate pricing has rarely been longer.

How fast the market movesThe Fields Quarterly · Q1 2026

Quarterly sales volume by suburb

Sales volume is the most under-reported number in property reporting. A 10% price rise on stable volume means something different from a 10% rise on 30% lower volume. In Q1 2026, the southern Gold Coast composite ran 17% below the five-year same-quarter average. Most of the shortfall is concentrated in Burleigh Waters, which printed 30 sales against a 5-year average of 46.7 — a 35.7% gap. Robina printed 54, essentially on its 5-year average of 53.2. Varsity Lakes printed 21, 25% below its prior year.

The pattern is consistent with supply tightness, not demand weakness: the few houses that sold sold close to ask, days-on-market for closed sales remained reasonable, and the sale-to-list ratio held. The sellers who are not transacting are not transacting because they have chosen to wait.

Leading indicatorsThe Fields Quarterly · Q1 2026

What moves before prices move.

Most market commentary mistakes confirmation for prediction. By the time interest-rate cuts have moved house prices, the market has already moved — for between three and twelve months. The indicators that genuinely lead prices are different from the ones the news leads with.

This report's underlying methodology leans on Australian property economics — particularly Abelson, Joyeux & Knight (2005), which formally established the correlations and lead-times below for the Australian housing market. Where elsewhere in this report we say "the data is consistent with…", this is the framework we are leaning on.

The strongest forward-looking indicator we know of is wage growth. Wages have led residential property prices in Australia by 3 to 4 months with a correlation of 0.94 — the kind of statistical relationship that doesn't show up by accident. Household spending follows almost in real time (correlation 0.91). Interest rates and credit growth, by contrast, lag prices — by 12 months and 3.5 months respectively. They confirm; they do not predict.

What the indicator board is saying right now (as at Q1 2026)

QLD wage growth has held at approximately 4.0% year-on-year through the trailing twelve months — historically consistent with continued price firmness on a 3-4 month forward basis. The cash rate (4.35%) and any potential easing cycle would confirm price moves already in train rather than precede them; if rates ease in late 2026, the signal would be retrospective, not predictive. Housing-supply pressure remains the largest structural risk over a 2-year horizon — but QLD approvals are running below trend, which is why the supply pipeline does not yet look meaningful through to 2027. Of these, the indicator we watch most carefully is wage growth: a meaningful slowdown would precede price softness with the highest reliability.

Income elasticity (1.71×) and asymmetric adjustment (prices take ~6 quarters to fall vs ~4 to rise) are also drawn from Abelson, Joyeux & Knight (2005). The implication of asymmetric adjustment is that sharp downward price corrections are structurally rare in this market — cooling tends to play out across 18-24 months.

Robina · 4226The Fields Quarterly · Q1 2026

Postcode 4226 · 12.3 km²

Robina.

The largest of the three core southern Gold Coast suburbs by transaction volume — and the easiest to read in the data.

Robina's housing stock is the most diverse of the three core suburbs: master-planned releases by the original Robin Group (now Robina Land Corp), older Bond Estate streets dating to the early 1980s, the newer Robina Quays releases, and a continuous belt of townhouses and units around Robina Town Centre. Owner-occupier share at sale is approximately 64% — the highest of the three.

Three things define Robina as a property market. First, buyer-pool depth. Bond University, Robina hospital, the Town Centre, the train station, and the school catchments (Robina State High, Hillcrest Christian, Somerset College) draw multiple distinct buyer cohorts simultaneously. When one cohort pauses, another fills the gap. Second, master-planned variance is low. Covenants on build envelope, fence height and colour palette mean Robina houses cluster more tightly around the suburb median than in either of the other core suburbs — which makes valuation comparisons easier and the indexed price more reliable. Third, the suburb is split between houses (~61%) and units / townhouses (~39%) in a way the other two are not. Through 2024 and into 2025 the two halves moved together; through Q3-Q4 2025 they began to separate.

Burleigh Waters.

A canal suburb that does not announce itself — ridged east, creek-bound south, motorway-bound north. Geography is a feature.

Burleigh Waters sits inland of Burleigh Heads, ridged on its eastern edge, bounded south by Tallebudgera Creek and north by the motorway. The geography is a feature: it tightens supply and shapes the price distribution. Two-thirds of the house stock sits canal-front or one street back from a canal.

The canal-front cluster trades on a 30-50% premium over comparable non-canal stock, which is why the suburb median (currently $1,840,000 on the rolling 12-month basis) hides a meaningfully bimodal distribution. Reporting only the median in this suburb misrepresents both halves of the market.

The rolling 12-month median continued to climb, reaching $1,840,000 (+8.2% year-on-year). Median days-on-market came in at 30, only slightly above the 28-day historical median — so the few sales that completed completed at the usual pace. The suburb's Conviction Index is 96.7. The gap between Burleigh Waters' record-high rolling median and its below-baseline conviction reading is the cleanest single example of the standoff anywhere in the Q1 data.

The flood question

"Does Burleigh Waters flood?" is the most-searched question for this suburb and the most data-rich question we can answer. Two distinct flood definitions are in use: the City of Gold Coast City Plan flood overlay (planning-conservative; captures most canal-front lots), and the ICA Insurance Probability Zones (insurer-actuarial; often narrower than council mapping).

Many properties under the council overlay sit outside any ICA zone — council classifies them as flood-affected for planning, insurers do not price them as flood risk. Both maps are evidence; we report both. Recorded flood events on this suburb are real (flash flooding 2017 and 2022). Insurance premiums on canal-front properties have risen materially since Cyclone Alfred (2025).

MethodologyThe Fields Quarterly · Q1 2026

How every chart in this report is made.

A first-class chapter, not an appendix. Trust scales with disclosure; we publish what we measure and how we measure it.

The Fields Conviction Index

A composite of three components, each z-scored against its own rolling 5-year history, then weighted and rebased so that Q1 2020 = 100. The components are: indexed median price (50% weight), latest-quarter sales volume (25%), and inverse days-on-market (25%) where DOM data is available. Where DOM data is not available (pre-Q2 2023), the index is computed on the two-component basis (67% price, 33% volume). The four-component FCI specified in the methodology document — adding sale-to-list ratio and stock-on-market — will be live from Issue 02 once historical backfill is complete.

Indexed prices

Hedonic regression at suburb level. Each transaction generates an attribute-adjusted index point; the index is the time-series of those points smoothed to quarterly. Methodology cross-validated against the Cotality Hedonic Home Value Index at the SA2 level.

Comparable-sales valuations

Per property: 3-8 comparables matched on distance ≤500m, recency ≤6 months, beds/baths, floor area ±15%, land size ±20%, condition, position, view. Each comp adjusted; weighted mean used; 90% confidence interval is 1.645 × weighted standard deviation. Properties listing ≥$2,500,000 flagged as directional_only — comp scarcity widens the CI; shown as a range only.

Days-on-market

First-list date to unconditional contract. Excludes properties withdrawn within 14 days. Excludes family transfers under $200,000 and probate sales without arms-length pricing.

Sales volume

Houses only, deduplicated across three sources (Domain, REA, on-property), taking max per quarter to avoid double-counting. The in-progress current quarter is excluded from this report.

Sample sizes

Any quarter with fewer than 30 transactions for a suburb is flagged. Charts cite the underlying N. Confidence intervals widen with sample size; we report N every time.

Where we differ from Cotality, Domain, PropTrack and SQM

Each source answers a slightly different question. Cotality models attribute-adjusted hedonic prices on settled sales; we use the same approach at suburb level and reconcile against their published series. Domain reports stratified medians on settled sales; we use these as a postcode-level sanity check. PropTrack and SQM track asking prices on currently listed stock; we treat asking-price indices as a negotiability signal rather than as price-trend evidence. The four-source reconciliation chart is in scope for Issue 02.

What this report does not answer.

  • Whether the volume drop is structural or seasonal. Q1 2026 volume is below the five-year average across the southern Gold Coast. We will not know whether this is a cyclical pause or the start of structural change until we see Q3. The data does not currently distinguish.

GlossaryThe Fields Quarterly · Q1 2026

The terms we use, defined.

A one-glance reference for the technical terms in this report. The plain-English meaning is given first; the formal name is in italics.

The Fields Conviction Index (FCI)
One number, published every quarter, that captures the underlying strength of the southern Gold Coast property market. 100 is a typical pre-COVID baseline; above 115 is tight; below 85 favours buyers. Formal: a transaction-weighted weighted composite of three components, each z-scored against five years of history.
Composite
Robina, Burleigh Waters and Varsity Lakes combined into one number, weighted by how many transactions each suburb produced. Formal: transaction-weighted aggregate.
Indexed price
The price level after adjusting for the mix of houses sold each quarter, so a quarter heavy with prestige sales does not fake a price boom. Formal: hedonic regression-based price index, the same statistical method CoreLogic / Cotality use.
Rolling 12-month median
The middle sale price across the most recent twelve months, recomputed monthly. We trust it more than a single-quarter median because the sample size is larger. Formal: trailing-twelve-month median, recomputed at month-end.
Z-score
A measure of how unusual a reading is against its own history. A z-score of +1 means "one standard deviation above the five-year average" — uncommon but not rare. +2 means strongly above; −2 means strongly below. Formal: standardised score, (current value − mean) ÷ standard deviation.
Sale-to-list ratio
The final sale price as a share of the property's first listed price. 100% means the home sold at ask; 95% means it sold 5% below; 102% means it sold above. Formal: median (sale ÷ first list) across closed transactions.
Days-on-market
How long a home took to sell, measured from first listing to unconditional contract. We report the median across each quarter so a few outliers do not distort the figure. Formal: list-to-unconditional median, private-treaty + auction combined.
Sample size (N)
How many transactions a number is based on. Below 30, we flag the figure as small-sample and do not make strong claims off it. Formal: transaction count for the relevant period and segment.
