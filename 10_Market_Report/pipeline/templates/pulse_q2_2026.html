Prices held.
Volume didn't.

Across Robina, Burleigh Waters and Varsity Lakes, median house prices held or rose through Q2 2026 on the reliable trailing-12-month measure — yet the number of homes changing hands ran sharply below year-earlier levels, down 44% in Burleigh Waters and closer to half in Robina and Varsity Lakes, and those that sold took longer to do it. With the RBA cash rate at 4.35% after three rises this year, the squeeze is landing on activity, not on price. The Conviction Index captures what a single price line cannot: conviction eased even as headline prices did not. This is a market cooling by volume, not by value.

Three suburbs, three readings

The composite hides real divergence. Varsity Lakes held above its conviction baseline; Robina fell the furthest. Same forces, different exposure.

Robina's broad mid-market is where the volume squeeze shows most. The trailing median is still rising, but its pace shrinks each quarter; the sharp single-quarter dip runs on just 30 sales. More listings, a deeper buyer pool and longer selling times have moved conditions from a seller's market toward balance.

The single-quarter median fell from a thin, peak-distorted Q4 2025 reading — but every supply-side signal points the other way: turnover eased from an elevated 2025 rather than collapsing, listings are falling, absorption is tightening, average selling times dropped sharply, and the trailing median is accelerating (+5.6%). Cooling by volume, not by value.

The only suburb still above its conviction baseline — the entry price point kept attracting bids, and the trailing median is still rising. But activity is decelerating hard: turnover halved year-on-year and listings roughly doubled in a month. Historically activity moves first and price follows, so the firm price is the lagging signal here.

Reading the divergence. One market, three exposures. Robina's depth cuts both ways — the widest buyer pool also means the most listings and the softest single-quarter print. Burleigh Waters' premium stock holds value while turnover normalises. Varsity Lakes, the entry point, still draws bids but is where activity is easing fastest. The composite averages them to 94.4; the suburbs tell the fuller story.

The evidence, in three sales

The data describes the market. These transactions show it — quality homes still clearing, but buyers with a little more room to negotiate.

Robina · top-end depth

138 Camberwell Circuit

5 bed · 3 bath · 4 car · 920 m² · lakefront, 22 m water frontage
Sold $2,410,000  · auction · 17 April 2026

Taken to auction rather than listed with a price, it sold on the day — well above Robina's ~$1,500,000 median, and more than double the $960,000 paid in 2020. Where a home sits outside the ordinary comparable set, a public deadline can still concentrate competition. Depth remains at the distinctive top end, even as broad turnover thins.

Burleigh Waters · close to ask

22 Lanai Drive

4 bed · 2 bath · 2 car · single-level family home
Sold $1,640,000  · guided "offers considered $1,695,000" · 23 July 2026

A four-bedroom home guided at $1,695,000 that settled at $1,640,000 — about 3% under guide. Close to ask, but with room to negotiate: the profile of Burleigh Waters' quarter, where homes are still clearing while buyers no longer pay the top of the range.

Varsity Lakes · price holds, urgency eases

16 South Bay Drive

3 bed · 2 bath · 2 car · lake outlook
Sold $1,250,000  · guided "offers over $1,290,000" · 9 July 2026

Guided over $1,290,000, sold at $1,250,000 — again about 3% below guide. Priced to a lake-facing position, it moved without the urgency of a year ago, matching Varsity Lakes' easing activity and still-firm pricing.

The throughline. Across all three, quality homes settled roughly 3% under a firm guide. That is not capitulation — it is modest negotiating room returning to buyers while vendors hold their line. The same story the Conviction Index tells, read in three transactions rather than three charts.
