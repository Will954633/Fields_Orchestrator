# Best-in-Class Property & Finance Market Reports — Research Synthesis

**Purpose:** Set the global standard for the Fields flagship Gold Coast market update. This document audits the world's top property and finance market reports, identifies what they do exceptionally well, where they fail, and where a small data-led independent on the Gold Coast can credibly beat them.

**Compiled:** May 2026
**Coverage:** 18 reports across global property authorities, Australian benchmarks, US/UK comparables, and finance/data-journalism standards.

---

## PART 1 — REPORT-BY-REPORT AUDIT

### 1. Knight Frank — *The Wealth Report* (20th edition, 2026)

- **URL:** https://www.knightfrank.com/research/reports/wealthreport
- **Cadence:** Annual, published early March
- **Length:** ~70-100pp glossy PDF, three primary sections (Wealth, Property, Luxury) with sub-themes (superyachts, jets, art, wine, whisky)
- **Distribution:** Gated PDF (name/email/phone), distributed across 52 country offices
- **Best-in-class for:**
  1. **Proprietary indices that compound credibility over years** — the PIRI 100 (Prime International Residential Index) tracks 100 luxury markets and has run continuously, so the brand owns the data series.
  2. **Survey-derived primary data** — the annual Family Office Survey produces quotes and figures nobody else has, generating PR coverage.
  3. **Long-arc retrospectives** — the 20th anniversary edition does "20 years on" pieces revisiting 2007 predictions, which builds intellectual humility into the brand and creates a moat (you can't fake 20 years of archives).
- **Best chart:** PIRI 100 sortable league table — 100 cities ranked by 12-month % change, colour-coded by region. Why it works: every reader can find their city, and the format invites screenshotting and onward sharing.
- **Weaknesses to exploit:**
  1. **Vendor-friendly framing** — luxury bias glosses over middle-market reality; little engagement with the affordability crisis.
  2. **Lifestyle filler dilutes data** — superyacht and jet sections feel like advertorial, eroding analytical authority.
  3. **Annual cadence misses cycles** — by the time it lands, the data is already 3-9 months stale.
- **Lead-capture model:** Hard-gated PDF download. The form is the product — Knight Frank's funnel feeds private bank and prime advisory leads.

### 2. Knight Frank — *Australian Residential Review* / *Prime Residential Review*

- **URL:** https://www.knightfrank.com.au/residential/research
- **Cadence:** Quarterly
- **Length:** ~12-16pp per report; mainstream + prime split
- **Best-in-class for:** Splitting mainstream from "top 5% by value" prime — gives sellers and HNW buyers their own report.
- **Weaknesses:** Generic capital-city aggregation. Burleigh Waters, Robina, Varsity Lakes are invisible.

### 3. Knight Frank — *Prime Global Cities Index* (Q1 2026)

- **URL:** https://www.knightfrank.com/research/article/2026/4/piri-100-ultimate-prime-residential-property-index
- **Cadence:** Quarterly
- **Best chart:** Annual change scatter for 45 cities, with regional colour coding and clear annotations on outliers (Dubai, Hong Kong).
- **Weakness:** Top-down only — no neighbourhood granularity, no methodology depth on what counts as "prime."

### 4. Savills — *World Cities Prime Residential Index*

- **URL:** https://pdf.savills.com/World+Research+-+World+Cities+2026.pdf (Feb 2026 edition)
- **Cadence:** Twice yearly (Feb / Aug, "H1" and "H2")
- **Length:** ~20pp PDF, 30 cities tracked across capital values, rents, yields
- **Best-in-class for:**
  1. **Three-axis ranking** — capital values, rents, yields shown together, exposing places where rent has decoupled from price.
  2. **Forecast pairing** — alongside historical, e.g. Seoul/Tokyo forecast +7.9% in 2026.
  3. **Crisp design** — pull-quote callouts; each city gets two sentences plus a sparkline.
- **Weaknesses:** Same 30 cities every cycle; little explanation when a city's behaviour breaks the model.
- **Distribution:** Free PDF, ungated. Strategy is brand-building and inbound enquiries.

### 5. JLL — *Global Real Estate Perspective*

- **URL:** https://www.jll.com/en-us/insights/market-perspectives
- **Cadence:** Monthly
- **Best-in-class for:** Sector-by-sector structure (Living, Office, Logistics, Retail, Hotels, Capital Markets) — readers can jump straight to their sector.
- **Weakness:** Dense corporate prose; few memorable charts.

### 6. CBRE — *Global Living* / market outlooks

- **URL:** https://www.cbre.com/insights
- **Cadence:** Annual *Global Living*; rolling outlooks
- **Best-in-class for:** Investment-grade rigour, geographically broad. Strong on commercial — weaker on residential where Knight Frank/Savills dominate.

### 7. Cushman & Wakefield — *MarketBeat*

- **URL:** https://www.cushmanwakefield.com/en/united-states/insights/us-marketbeats
- **Cadence:** Quarterly per market and sector
- **Best-in-class for:** **Hyper-local granularity** — Silicon Valley, Las Vegas, Chicago, NYC each get individual MarketBeats. Submarket-level data on vacancy, absorption, rents.
- **Weakness:** Commercial-only; identical templates across markets feel mechanical.
- **Lesson for Fields:** The submarket template is exactly what a Robina / Burleigh / Varsity Lakes report should look like.

### 8. CoreLogic (Cotality) — *Hedonic Home Value Index*

- **URL:** https://www.cotality.com / https://www.corelogic.com.au/our-data/corelogic-indices
- **Cadence:** Daily index, monthly headline release ("first of the month")
- **Methodology:** Hedonic regression — values imputed from bedrooms, bathrooms, land area, location attributes; controls for compositional bias that distorts median price.
- **Best-in-class for:** **Methodology transparency** — the hedonic technique is documented, peer-reviewed, and the headline number is what every Australian media outlet leads with on the 1st of the month.
- **Weakness:** Capital-city-aggregate dominates the headline; suburb-level only available behind paid subscription.

### 9. CoreLogic — *Pain & Gain Report*

- **URL:** https://www.cotality.com/news-research/reports/pain-gain (formerly corelogic.com.au)
- **Cadence:** Quarterly
- **Length:** ~15-20pp
- **Structure:** % profit-making vs loss-making sales; median nominal gain/loss; gross profit dollars; hold-period analysis (median 8.8 years; short-hold 2-4yr segment); house vs unit; capital vs region; LGA-level outliers.
- **Best chart:** Rolling 3-month line chart of profit-making vs loss-making rates, with capital-city dots ranked alongside.
- **Best-in-class for:** **A single proprietary metric the media can't ignore** — "% of sales that made a profit" is a number journalists understand instantly. March 2025: 94.9% profitable, $31.7B in gross gains.
- **Weakness:** Uses *nominal* gain — ignores inflation and holding costs (rates, repairs, mortgage interest), which an honest data outfit could expose.

### 10. CoreLogic — *Best of the Best* (annual year-ender)

- **URL:** https://www.corelogic.com.au/best-of-the-best
- **Cadence:** Annual, published mid-December
- **Structure:** Top 5 sales by address; top 10 most expensive / most affordable suburbs (houses + units); top 10 largest 12-mo growth / decline; top 10 rent growth; top 10 yields. National + each capital + rest-of-state.
- **Best-in-class for:** **The list-driven format owns December media** — every TV bulletin and newspaper runs "the top 10 suburbs of [year]" stories, and it runs on CoreLogic data because the report writes itself for journalists.
- **Lesson for Fields:** A Gold Coast version of this — Top 5 sales / fastest-selling streets / biggest discount / best-yielding pocket — is publishable now.

### 11. Domain — *House Price Report* / *Forecast Report*

- **URL:** https://www.domain.com.au/research
- **Cadence:** Quarterly HPR; biannual Forecast
- **Best-in-class for:** **Forecast confidence** — they put a number on next year (e.g. combined capitals +6% in 2026 to $1,339,267 median). Specific predictions get cited everywhere.
- **Weakness:** Predictions age badly. Median measures are sensitive to compositional shift. House/unit split too coarse.

### 12. PropTrack (REA Group) — quarterly insights

- **URL:** https://www.proptrack.com.au
- **Best-in-class for:** Asking-price + listing-volume early indicators (their data leads CoreLogic by ~2 weeks because it's at-listing, not at-settlement).
- **Weakness:** Asking prices ≠ transaction prices — risk of over-stating market moves.

### 13. SQM Research — weekly sales / asking prices / vacancy

- **URL:** https://sqmresearch.com.au/
- **Cadence:** Weekly
- **Best-in-class for:**
  1. **Weekly cadence** — only Australian source with this rhythm.
  2. **Postcode-level CSV downloads** for free, the entire history.
  3. **Vacancy rate** is the most-cited Australian rental tightness measure.
- **Lesson:** Free CSV + postcode granularity builds analyst loyalty. Fields should publish raw suburb data.

### 14. Herron Todd White — *Month in Review*

- **URL:** https://htw.com.au/month-in-review/
- **Cadence:** Monthly
- **Length:** ~80-150pp, free PDF
- **Structure:** CEO macro letter → National Office → National Residential → Agribusiness, each with the **HTW Property Clock** (positioning each market on a 12-position clock from "rising" through "peak" / "declining" / "bottom"). Then a city-by-city, valuer-on-the-ground commentary.
- **Best-in-class for:**
  1. **The Property Clock visual** — instantly readable, branded, screen-shottable. Becomes shorthand: "HTW has Gold Coast at 11 o'clock."
  2. **Local-valuer voice** — each section written by the actual valuer in that city, so it reads as practitioner intel, not desk-bound spin.
  3. **Free, monthly, 13-year archive** — they have the consistency moat.
- **Weaknesses to exploit:**
  1. **Brisbane-level coverage only** — Gold Coast subsumed under SE Qld; no Burleigh / Robina granularity.
  2. **Long-form prose, weak data tables** — a reader has to read paragraphs to extract a number.
  3. **No interactive or downloadable data** — PDF only.
- **This is the report Fields should benchmark hardest against.** Same cadence; better granularity; modern delivery.

### 15. RBA — *Statement on Monetary Policy* (housing chapters)

- **URL:** https://www.rba.gov.au/publications/smp/
- **Cadence:** Quarterly
- **Best-in-class for:**
  1. **Source line under every chart** ("Sources: ABS; CoreLogic; RBA") — the gold standard for credibility.
  2. **Dual-axis charts comparing dwelling investment with building approvals**, with shaded backgrounds marking easing cycles. Multi-panel small multiples (Graph 2.10 etc.).
  3. **Liaison program insights** — informal interviews with builders and lenders sit alongside published data, giving texture without becoming opinion.
  4. **Uncertainty stated explicitly** — language like "monthly growth may have stabilised somewhat" sets tone of analytical caution.
- **Weakness:** Prose is dense and policy-flavoured; not designed for retail readers.
- **Lesson:** Always cite source under chart; always quantify uncertainty; never overclaim.

### 16. Redfin — *Data Center* + *Redfin News*

- **URL:** https://www.redfin.com/news/data-center/
- **Cadence:** Weekly (rolling 1, 4, 12-week windows, released Wednesdays); Monthly (third Friday)
- **Format:** Interactive Tableau-powered dashboards; bulk CSV downloads; printable market PDFs.
- **Best-in-class for:**
  1. **Free, ungated, citable** — researchers and journalists trust it because the cost of using it is just attribution.
  2. **Proprietary metrics nobody else has** — tour activity, offer-acceptance ratios, share-with-price-drops.
  3. **Seven-region zoom** — National → Metro → State → County → City → ZIP → Neighborhood, all in the same tool.
- **Weakness:** Aesthetic is utilitarian; not narrative-led. Charts don't have voice.
- **Lesson for Fields:** Open data + downloadable dashboard at suburb level is a moat. Trust beats gating at the awareness stage.

### 17. Zillow — *Research* + ZHVI / ZORI

- **URL:** https://www.zillow.com/research/
- **Cadence:** Monthly indices, rolling research articles
- **ZHVI methodology:** Neural-network Zestimate, 35th-65th percentile band, smoothed over time. Updated 2023.
- **Best-in-class for:**
  1. **Methodology transparency** — full documentation, version-history of revisions (2013 → 2019 → 2023), code/data downloadable for replication.
  2. **Two indices in tandem** — ZHVI (values) + ZORI (rents) lets researchers analyse yield compression.
  3. **Open data ethic** — "we publish the data so our work can be replicated independently."
- **Weakness:** Black-box AI Zestimate is contested by appraisers; valuation-level accuracy at individual property is suspect.

### 18. Realtor.com — Economic Research

- **URL:** https://www.realtor.com/research/
- **Cadence:** Monthly housing report; weekly inventory tracker
- **Best-in-class for:** Listing-side leading indicators — active listings, new listings, days on market, price-cut share.

### 19. John Burns Research & Consulting

- **URL:** https://jbrec.com/
- **Cadence:** Weekly *Building Market Intelligence* newsletter to 40,000 subscribers; monthly *New Home Insights Podcast*
- **Best-in-class for:** **Newsletter-as-product** — 40k engaged subscribers means Burns is paid by enterprise clients to produce the content the public sees only the surface of.
- **Lesson for Fields:** Newsletter is the asset; reports are bait.

### 20. Rightmove HPI

- **URL:** https://www.rightmove.co.uk/news/house-price-index/
- **Cadence:** Monthly; sample of ~200,000 newly-listed homes (95% of UK new stock)
- **Best-in-class for:** Three-tier segmentation — first-time buyer (≤2 bed) / second-stepper (3-4 bed ex 4-bed-detached) / top-of-ladder (5+ bed and 4-bed detached). Buyers find themselves in the data.
- **Weakness:** Asking-price index — doesn't reflect sale prices. Vendor bias.

### 21. Halifax + Nationwide HPI (UK)

- **URL:** https://www.halifax.co.uk/media-centre/house-price-index.html / https://www.nationwide.co.uk/house-price-index
- **Best-in-class for:** Mortgage-approval data, decades of continuity. Halifax index runs from 1983.
- **Weakness:** Excludes cash transactions and BTL; both miss high-end and investor activity.

### 22. Hamptons (UK) — *Market Insight* (quarterly)

- **URL:** https://www.hamptons.co.uk/research/reports
- **Cadence:** Quarterly
- **Best-in-class for:** Theme-led not data-led — "The Generation Housing Game", "Price Paralysis", "One Foot on the Ladder". Each report has a single thesis, supported by their own group's transactional data.
- **Lesson:** Pick one big idea per quarter and own it. Don't recap every metric every time.

### 23. Financial Times — data journalism / property coverage

- **URL:** https://www.ft.com/visual-and-data-journalism / *Visual Vocabulary*: https://github.com/Financial-Times/chart-doctor
- **Best-in-class for:**
  1. **Visual Vocabulary framework** — a public taxonomy classifying chart types by purpose (Deviation, Correlation, Ranking, Distribution, Change-over-time, Part-to-whole, Magnitude, Spatial, Flow). Free guide to picking the right chart.
  2. **John Burn-Murdoch principles** — "Don't make charts for chart people, make stories for all people." Charts read in a Z-pattern: title → axes → data, so titles must carry the insight.
  3. **FastCharts internal tool** — pre-constrained chart types so nobody can ship a bad chart; quality scales with team size.
  4. **Salmon-pink consistency** — instantly recognisable colour palette + serif headline + sans-serif annotation.

### 24. The Economist — chart style guide

- **URL:** style guide at https://design-system.economist.com (and informal mirrors)
- **Best-in-class for:**
  1. **One chart, one message** — primary data point in Economist Red (#E3120B), everything else in greyscale.
  2. **Headline as conclusion**, not as label — title states the insight, deck adds the context.
  3. **Specialised chart types** — slope charts, dumbbells, heatmaps, beeswarms — used purposefully not decoratively.

### 25. Bloomberg / CityLab housing coverage

- **URL:** https://www.bloomberg.com/citylab/housing
- **Best-in-class for:** Long-form data narrative — pieces like "Where Housing Is Outpacing Wages" combine satellite imagery, ZIP-code maps, and interactive scrollytelling.

### 26. Visual Capitalist — housing infographics

- **URL:** https://www.visualcapitalist.com/
- **Best-in-class for:** Single-image charts engineered for social sharing. "20 Years of Home Price Changes in Every U.S. City" — one image, decade of data, viral by design.
- **Lesson for Fields:** One sharable cover chart per report. Built for screenshots, not just PDF reading.

### 27. Federal Reserve / RBA chart packs

- **URL:** RBA — https://www.rba.gov.au/chart-pack/
- **Best-in-class for:** Disciplined, source-cited, no-narrative charts. Every analyst's reference set.
- **Lesson:** Publish a Fields chart-pack — raw, downloadable, well-sourced — alongside the editorial report. Becomes a media reference set.

## PART 2 — THE 10 UNIVERSAL TRAITS OF TOP-TIER REPORTS

1. **A proprietary index with a name** — PIRI 100, ZHVI, HTW Property Clock, Redfin RHPI, Pain & Gain. The brand owns a number journalists can't get elsewhere. Fields needs a named index (e.g. *Fields Gold Coast Conviction Index*).
2. **Methodology transparency** — Zillow publishes code; CoreLogic explains hedonic regression; RBA cites every source. Trust scales with disclosure.
3. **Consistency over years** — Halifax (1983), Wealth Report (2007), HTW Month in Review (2010s+). The archive *is* the moat. Start now and never miss an issue.
4. **One signature chart that becomes shorthand** — HTW Property Clock, Knight Frank PIRI sortable league, Redfin tour-activity heatmap. The chart is the brand.
5. **A clear cadence the audience can plan around** — first of the month (CoreLogic), Wednesday (Redfin weekly), quarterly with Hamptons, monthly with HTW. Predictability earns subscription.
6. **Headlines that carry the conclusion** — FT/Economist principle. "Burleigh Waters median up 4.2% as listings tighten 18%" beats "Burleigh Waters Update Q1 2026."
7. **Source line under every chart** — RBA standard. "Sources: Domain.com.au scraped daily; Fields valuation model v3; ABS Census 2021." Builds defensibility.
8. **A free entry tier and a gated depth tier** — Redfin gives away data; HTW gives away PDFs; Burns gives the newsletter, charges for the data. Fields can give the suburb summary free, gate the seller-grade per-property report.
9. **A practitioner voice** — HTW writes by valuer; Burns by analyst; Hamptons by the agent on the ground. Fields' equivalent: Will writing as the buyer the seller wishes they had.
10. **Forward-looking framing within bounds** — Domain forecasts a number; RBA states ranges; HTW positions the clock. None say "now is a great time to buy." All offer conditional language.

## PART 3 — THE 10 UNIVERSAL WEAKNESSES (EXPLOITABLE)

1. **Length without insight** — Wealth Report runs 100+ pages; readers skim ten. Most pages are recycled prose around two real findings.
2. **Recency-biased narrative** — last quarter framed as a turning point regardless of context. CoreLogic and Domain both routinely declare "the cycle has turned" multiple times in one cycle.
3. **Vendor-friendly framing** — agencies write reports that don't make their seller clients look bad. No agency report ever leads with "stock has been overpriced for six months."
4. **Capital-city aggregation hides truth** — the "Australian housing market" up 8.5% YoY masks that Sydney is flat, Brisbane is +14%, regional Vic is -2%. Suburb-level reports are rare because national outlets don't have local data.
5. **Asking prices conflated with transaction prices** — Rightmove, PropTrack, Domain listing data overstate moves. CoreLogic's settlement-based hedonic is more accurate but lags.
6. **Nominal returns reported, real returns hidden** — Pain & Gain reports 94.9% "profitable" but ignores inflation and holding costs. Real return after CPI + rates + maintenance often shows 30%+ of sales lose money.
7. **Predictions without methodology or accountability** — Domain forecasts a number, never publishes its track record. RBA at least states uncertainty bands.
8. **Forbidden words and clichés as filler** — "robust", "resilient", "stunning", "rare opportunity" — you can detect a vendor report at 50 paces by language alone.
9. **Charts without annotations** — most reports show lines without labels on inflection points. Reader has to infer the story. FT/Economist standard is to annotate every meaningful turn.
10. **Heavy gating destroys reach** — Knight Frank's hard-gate captures leads but kills citation. Reports with no gate (Redfin, RBA) become reference standards; gated reports get used once and archived.

**Bonus weakness — non-comparable metrics across reports.** A reader trying to triangulate Domain median vs CoreLogic hedonic vs PropTrack vs SQM asking price gets four different numbers. Whoever publishes a *reconciliation chart* showing the four sources for one suburb owns the trust.

## PART 4 — 10 OPPORTUNITIES FOR A DATA-LED INDEPENDENT ON THE GOLD COAST

1. **Suburb-level granularity nobody else publishes.** Robina, Burleigh Waters, Varsity Lakes deserve a 10-page section each, not a sentence inside "South-East Queensland." Knight Frank, CoreLogic, HTW *cannot* go this deep — their model is national. Fields can.

2. **Reconcile the four asking/sales/hedonic measures in one chart.** Side-by-side line of Domain median, CoreLogic hedonic, PropTrack asking, SQM asking price for Burleigh Waters. Show the gap. Explain why. Become the trusted referee.

3. **Real returns, not nominal.** Build a *Real Pain & Gain* table — strip CPI, council rates, average maintenance, mortgage interest. Reveal that a 6-year hold returning 22% nominal is actually 4% real. This is publishable, repeatable, devastating to the industry narrative, and impossible for CoreLogic to publish without offending their bank clients.

4. **Honest valuation ranges, never single figures in headlines.** Already a rule in Fields' editorial code. Display comparable-sales ranges with confidence intervals. Use the comp range as the headline metric.

5. **Sold + withdrawn + still-on-market in one funnel chart.** Most reports show only what sold. Fields can show how many listings *failed* to sell — the discount story. "Of 412 Burleigh Waters listings in Q1, 71% sold, 18% withdrawn, 11% still listed at quarter-end."

6. **A signature visual the Gold Coast adopts as shorthand.** Equivalent of HTW Property Clock. Suggested: *Fields Conviction Map* — every suburb plotted on price-momentum × listings-tightness, four quadrants (Heating / Frothy / Tightening / Cooling). Updated monthly. Press, agents, lenders all reference position.

7. **Free CSV + chart pack alongside the editorial report.** Publish raw suburb data publicly with attribution-only licence. Become the source other outlets cite. Builds defensibility and SEO authority.

8. **Methodology page published alongside every release.** Cosmos DB row counts, scrape dates, hedonic / comp-range method, confidence intervals stated. Match RBA / Zillow standard. No competitor on the Gold Coast does this.

9. **Practitioner voice — the buyer's-eye view.** HTW has valuers; Burns has analysts; Hamptons has agents. Fields has Will, who walks the suburbs, runs the model, photographs the listings, and refuses to take seller commission. That voice is unique on the Gold Coast and aligns with the buyer-first mission.

10. **Forecast accountability.** Publish forecasts and republish them later with hit/miss tracked. Domain, Knight Frank, banks all forecast — none publish accuracy. A "How wrong were we last year?" page builds 10x more trust than another forecast.

**Stretch opportunities (extensions of the above):**
- **Monthly cadence — first Friday of the month, no exceptions.** HTW does it monthly; Knight Frank annually; CoreLogic monthly but capital-only. Fields monthly at suburb level + free + data download = uncontested.
- **Weekly mini-update on listings + asking price + days-on-market** — sub-1000-word email, like Burns' 40k-subscriber playbook. The newsletter is the subscriber asset; the report is the bait.
- **Annual *Fields Gold Coast Year in Review*** — Q4 list-driven format ("top 5 sales", "biggest discount", "fastest-selling street"). Owns December media because it writes itself for journalists.

## PART 5 — DESIGN & VOICE PRINCIPLES TO ADOPT

From FT, Economist, RBA, Knight Frank visual standards:

- **One chart, one message** (Economist). Primary point in brand colour, supporting elements in greyscale.
- **Title is the conclusion**, deck adds context (FT). "Burleigh Waters median up 4.2%" beats "Burleigh Waters Sales Q1 2026."
- **Source line under every chart** with date and method (RBA standard).
- **Annotate inflection points** — never let the reader guess what changed (Burn-Murdoch).
- **Z-pattern reading** — title, axes, data. Title and labels carry the story; data confirms.
- **Restrained palette** — Fields brand colour for primary data, charcoal/grey for context, white space generous (Economist).
- **Sparklines in tables**, not just bare numbers (Savills).
- **Slope charts for two-period comparisons**, dumbbell charts for spread, beeswarms for distribution (FT Visual Vocabulary).
- **Forbidden words list enforced** — "stunning", "nestled", "boasting", "robust", "rare opportunity" never appear (Fields editorial code).
- **Conditional forecasts only** — "if X, data suggests Y" — never "prices will fall."

## TOP-LINE SUMMARY (3 SENTENCES)

The world's best property reports — Knight Frank PIRI, CoreLogic Hedonic + Pain & Gain, HTW Month in Review, FT/Economist data journalism — succeed by combining a named proprietary index, transparent methodology, a signature chart, and a multi-year archive that compounds into a reference moat; they fail by aggregating away local detail, conflating asking and transaction prices, reporting nominal returns, and writing vendor-friendly forecasts they never grade. The clean opportunity for Fields on the Gold Coast is suburb-level granularity (Robina, Burleigh Waters, Varsity Lakes each deserve their own ten-page section, which national publishers cannot produce), four-source price reconciliation, real (after-cost) returns, and a signature *Fields Conviction Map* updated monthly that the local market adopts as shorthand. Publish free with downloadable data and methodology — match Redfin and RBA on openness — and the report becomes the citation standard for Gold Coast property within twelve issues.
