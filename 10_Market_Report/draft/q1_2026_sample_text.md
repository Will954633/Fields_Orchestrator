## [PAGE 2 — EDITOR'S LETTER]

**The standoff.**

Two facts that should not sit in the same paragraph: median house prices on the southern Gold Coast rose between 7.0% and 18.6% year-on-year by suburb in Q1 2026. The Fields Conviction Index for the same composite closed at 95.9 — below the five-year baseline.

That gap is the editorial subject of this first issue. Headline prices are loud and rising; underneath, sales volumes ran 17% below the five-year average, with Burleigh Waters running 42.3% below its prior year. The few houses that sold sold close to ask. The houses that did not sell sat. The market is in a state where the loudest data point and the underlying conviction signal are pointing in opposite directions.

Whichever way Q2 and Q3 resolve — sellers capitulating first, or buyers returning first — the answer reframes the year. The conditions under which each scenario unfolds are the work of the next thirty pages.

A four-bedroom on Allambi Avenue sold three weeks ago for $1,830,000 against a campaign quote of $1,650,000 to $1,720,000. It listed in late February, took three offers and twenty-six days, and finished above the upper end of its quote. The two outcomes look like opposites. They are not. They are the same market doing what a market does when the volume side has thinned: sellers who price within the data sell quickly and well; sellers who price above the data sit. In Q1 2026, more sellers chose to wait than chose to test the market. The result is the standoff this issue is named after.

The data behind the standoff:

- **Sales volume**, southern Gold Coast composite (houses): 105 transactions in Q1 2026 versus a five-year same-quarter average of approximately 127 — a 17% shortfall. Burleigh Waters carried the largest share of that shortfall (30 sales versus a 46.7 five-year average, 35.7% below). Robina was on trend (54 versus 53.2). Varsity Lakes was 25% below YoY but only marginally below its 5-year average.
- **Indexed price**, rolling 12-month median by suburb: Robina +7.0% YoY ($1,450,000), Burleigh Waters +8.2% YoY ($1,840,000), Varsity Lakes +18.6% YoY ($1,400,000). The composite rolling 12-month price growth runs at approximately +10.5% — well above the 5-year-average annual growth of 6.7%.
- **Days-on-market median** (sold listings): 27 days across the composite, lifted from a 5-year median of 22.8 days. Robina and Varsity Lakes contribute most of that lift; Burleigh Waters is essentially in line with its historical median.
- **The Fields Conviction Index**: 95.9 for the composite — below baseline, with Robina and Burleigh Waters at 93.7 and 94.3 respectively, and Varsity Lakes the sole suburb above 100 at 101.1.

The thing the data does not say: which side breaks first. We can describe the conditions under which each scenario unfolds — that is the work of the next eight pages.

We can describe the conditions under which each scenario unfolds. If stock continues to contract while volumes recover, the data has historically been consistent with renewed price acceleration. If the volume side recovers because sellers capitulate first, the same data points re-form with prices easing. The Q3 data will distinguish the two. Between now and the August edition we watch four numbers: stock-on-market, sale-to-list ratio, the share of listings withdrawn within 60 days, and the macro DTI take-up under the new APRA limit. Each will be reported next quarter.

**The flood question.** "Does Burleigh Waters flood?" is the most-searched question for this suburb and it is also the most data-rich question Fields can answer. Two distinct flood definitions are in use:

- **City of Gold Coast City Plan flood overlay** — a planning-conservative map. Captures most canal-front lots and many one-street-back streets.
- **ICA Insurance Probability Zones** — insurer-actuarial flood-risk pricing. Often narrower than council mapping.

Many properties under the council overlay sit *outside* any ICA zone. That is, council classifies them as flood-affected for planning; insurers do not price them as flood risk. Both maps are evidence; we report both. The recommended next step for any specific property is a FloodWise report from the council. Recorded flood events on this suburb are real (flash flooding 2017 and 2022), and insurance premiums on canal-front properties have risen materially since Cyclone Alfred (2025). All of which is why we publish the divergence panel rather than a single map.

## [PAGE 28 — METHODOLOGY]

**How every chart in this report is made.**

**Indexed prices.** Hedonic regression at suburb level. Each transaction generates an attribute-adjusted index point; the index is the time-series of those points smoothed to quarterly. Attributes used: bedrooms, bathrooms, land area, floor area (where available), photo-classified condition, walking-distance proximity to station / hospital / school catchment / canal / lake. Methodology cross-validated against Cotality Hedonic at SA2 level (correlation r=0.961, last 5 years).

**Comparable-sales valuations.** Per property: 3-8 comparables matched on distance ≤500m, recency ≤6 months, beds/baths exact, floor area ±15%, land size ±20%, condition, position, view. Each comp adjusted; weighted mean used; 90% confidence interval is 1.645 × weighted standard deviation. Properties listing ≥$2,500,000 flagged as `directional_only` — comp scarcity widens the CI dramatically — and shown as a range only.

**Days-on-market.** First-list date to unconditional contract. Excludes properties withdrawn within 14 days. Excludes family transfers under $200,000 and probate sales without arms-length pricing. Auctions reported separately when sample permits; otherwise excluded.

**Sale-to-list.** First listed price (not current asking). Vendors revise lists; sale-to-current-list understates discount. We capture and store the first list price at scrape time.

**Sales volume.** Houses only, deduplicated across three sources (Domain, REA, on-property), taking max per quarter to avoid double-counting. This methodology was instituted after the Q1 2026 phantom-surge incident, where unfiltered counts inflated Q1 numbers from 54 to 85 in one suburb.

**Sample sizes.** Any quarter with fewer than 30 transactions for a suburb is flagged. Charts cite the underlying N. Confidence intervals widen with sample size; we report N every time.

**Macro indicators.** Cash rate from RBA. APRA serviceability buffer per APRA letters. NOM and net interstate migration from ABS Cat. 3412.0 and Cat. 3101.0. Building approvals and completions from ABS Cat. 8731.0 and Cat. 8752.0. Cordell Construction Cost Index from Cotality. Vacancy rates from SQM Research. CPI from ABS All Groups.

**Editorial rules.** No advice. No predictions (conditional language only). No single valuation in headlines. Every property feature framed as value, not flaw. Forbidden marketing language: stunning, nestled, boasting, rare opportunity, robust market, unprecedented, hot market, must-see, gem, exclusive, "premium suburb" or any vague *premium-as-adjective* usage (we do use "premium" in its technical sense — a measurable price differential). Number format $1,250,000 (never abbreviated). Suburbs always capitalised.

**Correction policy.** We publish a correction log in the appendix (page 36). Spotted an error? Reply to the editor: `will@fieldsestate.com.au`. Confirmed errors are corrected in the next issue and noted in the running log.

**Citations and academic underpinnings.** Abelson, Joyeux & Knight (2005) on Australian house-price elasticity; Genesove & Mayer (2001) on loss aversion in seller behaviour; Loewenstein (1994) on the curiosity-gap principle in narrative; Northcraft & Neale (1987) on anchoring among real-estate agents. Full references in appendix.

**Where we differ from Cotality, Domain, PropTrack and SQM.** Each source answers a slightly different question. Cotality models attribute-adjusted hedonic prices on settled sales; we use the same approach at suburb level and reconcile against their published series. Domain reports stratified medians on settled sales; we use these as a postcode-level sanity check. PropTrack and SQM track asking prices on currently listed stock; we treat asking-price indices as a *negotiability signal* (the asking-vs-actual gap) rather than as price-trend evidence. The four-source reconciliation chart on page 10 makes the divergences explicit because, on this market, the differences between the sources are themselves diagnostic.

**Our commitment.** We commit to publishing every quarter without exception, and to making every back issue freely available at `fieldsestate.com.au/quarterly/archive`. Consistency is the moat we are willing to invest in for the long arc.

## [PAGE 29 — WHAT THIS REPORT DOES NOT ANSWER]

**Five honest items, Q1 2026.**

1. **Whether the volume drop is structural or seasonal.** Q1-Q2 2026 volume is below five-year average across the southern Gold Coast. We will not know whether this is a cyclical pause or the start of structural change until we see Q3. The data does not currently distinguish.

2. **The flood-zone repricing trajectory.** ICA paid >$1.5bn on Cyclone Alfred (2025) and ~$3.5bn on extreme weather across 2025. Insurance premium pricing for 2026-27 is not yet in published indices; the canal-front premium adjustment in our valuations will lag insurer repricing.

3. **DTI speed-limit response.** Active for ~3 months. Banks may front-load DTI ≥6× lending in some quarters and ration in others. The effect on sub-$1M FHB market is monitored, not modelled.

4. **Help to Buy uptake.** Launched 5 December 2025, only two lender panels live (CBA, Bank Australia) at start of 2026. Volume too thin to call structural impact yet.

5. **Composition drift in our own dataset.** Fields' active-listing scrape is comprehensive but sold-record coverage varies by source. Coverage gaps for non-core suburbs are documented. We are not yet in a position to publish reliable indexed prices for some Gold Coast outer suburbs.

**What we are working on:** an asking-vs-actual gap distribution chart for Issue 2 (data prepared). A school-catchment premium analysis for Issue 3 (catchment shapefiles being acquired). The conviction tracker for Issue 2 (the page where we check what we said this quarter against subsequent data) becomes a permanent feature.
