# Psychology Application Playbook — From Research to Report Mechanics

**Document:** 02 of 6 (Strategy series)
**Source research:** `research/homeowner_psychology.md`
**Purpose:** Translate every psychology finding into a specific report element, design choice, copy rule, or page mechanic. If a finding can't be mapped to something concrete, it's not in this playbook.

---

## How to read this document

For each psychological lever, three columns:
1. **The finding** — the research claim, distilled.
2. **The report element** — the chart, callout, page, paragraph, or design rule that activates it.
3. **The reviewer test** — a yes/no check the editor or designer can apply when reviewing a draft.

This document is the rubric the report is reviewed against. Every page must score positively against at least three rows.

## Section A: The four archetypes — operational guidance

The report does not write to a single archetype. It writes to all four simultaneously by **layering**: every section has surface text (works for the curious owner), an evidence layer (satisfies the active buyer/seller), and a methodology footnote (rewards the informed observer).

### A.1 The Active Seller
| Finding | Report element | Reviewer test |
|---|---|---|
| Underselling fear is dominant | Each suburb section shows **price-distribution charts**, not just medians, so a seller can see where their tier sits | Does the suburb page show the 25th/50th/75th/95th percentile — not just the median? |
| Stress-equivalent-to-wedding | A *calm, methodical* tone throughout; never urgent | Read aloud — does it sound rushed, or considered? |
| 30% of Australians say agents played dirty | An explicit, single-paragraph "we are not an agent" disclosure on page 2 | Is the funding model declared in the first three pages? |
| Wants to confirm a half-belief | Charts show **outliers** (top-of-range sales) — sellers will see the ceiling and feel validated to ask higher than the median | Are top-quartile sale prices visible per suburb? |

### A.2 The Active Buyer
| Finding | Report element | Reviewer test |
|---|---|---|
| FOMO + FOMM dual trap | A **slowdown protocol** — "What to verify before bidding" callout per suburb section, evidenced not advisory | Is there a specific evidence-checklist per suburb? |
| 77% auction regret vs 37% private treaty | Cite the figure once, in the context of "the southern Gold Coast is a private-treaty market" — converts the buyer's natural pace into permission | Is the auction-vs-private treaty pace difference mentioned? |
| Wants better information than the agent | Comparable-sale walkthrough, in detail, anonymised — shows the kind of analysis a buyer cannot get from agents | Is there at least one full comparable-set walkthrough per quarter? |

### A.3 The Curious Owner
| Finding | Report element | Reviewer test |
|---|---|---|
| Quiet anxiety dressed as casual curiosity | A **"how we think about your tier"** sidebar — they self-identify, then read more carefully | Does the suburb section let an owner locate their home in the data? |
| Validation of past purchase | The 5- and 10-year capital-growth chart, with the suburb winning on at least one metric | Does each suburb have at least one "look how we've performed" angle? |
| Not ready to be sold to | The CTA is **subscribe**, not enquire — the seller offer is reserved for the nurture sequence | Is there a single "tell us about your home" form anywhere in the report? If yes, remove it |

### A.4 The Informed Observer
| Finding | Report element | Reviewer test |
|---|---|---|
| Wants to cite without looking foolish | A separate, prominent **methodology page** with named data sources, sample sizes, and exclusions | Could a journalist quote the report's chart and footnote it accurately? |
| Provides the social proof other archetypes need | A "How to cite this" line on the methodology page, plus a permanent canonical URL | Is there a permanent URL with the data underneath? |
| Hates errors | A correction policy on the methodology page | Is there a stated correction policy? |

## Section B: Cognitive biases — operational rules

### B.1 Loss aversion (Genesove & Mayer 2001)
- **Rule:** Never present a single number that triggers a loss frame. Always show context.
- **Mechanic:** The headline of each suburb section is a *path* — "Burleigh Waters house prices, 2015 → 2026" — not a number. The number sits on the path.
- **Forbidden:** A boxed-out single figure for any suburb's median. Even with a year label, the loss frame fires.
- **Permitted:** Median embedded in a sparkline with the prior 8 quarters visible.

### B.2 Endowment effect
- **Rule:** Respect the emotional truth before delivering the data truth.
- **Mechanic:** Each comparable-sales walkthrough opens with a one-line acknowledgement: *"The owners of this home priced from their kitchen renovation. The data prices the kitchen renovation differently. Here's why."* Empathic frame → data, in that order.
- **Test:** Read each comparable walkthrough — does it sound like it respects the owner, or like it's lecturing them?

### B.3 Anchoring (Northcraft & Neale 67% anchoring index)
- **Rule:** Lead with **distributions** and **ranges**, not point estimates.
- **Mechanic:** Headlines and chart leads use range syntax: "$1.62m–$2.10m for four-bedroom houses (47 sales, last 12 months)." The median is reported but never as the lead.
- **Forbidden:** Putting any single number above 36pt in size. The biggest number on the page is always inside a chart, in context.
- **Permitted:** A 24pt range. A 36pt suburb name. A 36pt section header.

### B.4 Status-quo bias
- **Rule:** Don't dislodge the reader's frame. Provide tools to revisit on their own terms.
- **Mechanic:** Each suburb section ends with "Three numbers we'd watch over the next 90 days" — explicit *signal*, not action.
- **Test:** Does the closing paragraph of each section avoid imperatives ("you should…", "consider…")?

### B.5 Sunk-cost on renovations
- **Rule:** Use Fields' photo-analysis data to *quantify* renovation value where the sample supports it.
- **Mechanic:** A small "Feature value teardown" sidebar in one suburb per quarter — pool, view, frontage, school catchment — with the sample size next to each estimate.
- **Caveat:** Always label as directional, not appraisal-grade. Sample sizes must be visible.

### B.6 FOMO + FOMM
- **Rule:** Slow the reader down through structure, not advice.
- **Mechanic:** Section subheads are *questions*, not declarations. "What does the price-volume divergence tell us?" forces the reader to think before reading. Five questions per section, max.
- **Forbidden:** "Don't miss out", "act now", "before it's too late".

### B.7 Confirmation bias
- **Rule:** Every suburb section must include at least one finding that the typical local reader *will not have expected*.
- **Mechanic:** A "What surprised us" callout box per suburb. A small editor-style mark — handwritten, almost — flagging the unexpected.
- **Test:** Could a Robina local read this and say "I didn't know that"? If not, rewrite.

### B.8 Authority bias (Cialdini)
- **Rule:** Establish authority *quietly* through methodology, sourcing, and named author. Never claim it.
- **Mechanic:** The cover has Will's name, not "Fields Real Estate." The byline is on the second page. Will's role description is one line: *"I built the database. I wrote the methodology. I'm responsible for the errors."*
- **Forbidden:** "Award-winning", "leading", "trusted by", or any unverifiable authority claim.

### B.9 Narrative transportation (Green & Brock 2000)
- **Rule:** Open every section with a specific, anonymised case before going to data.
- **Mechanic:** Each suburb section starts with one paragraph anchored to a real, recent transaction: *"A four-bedroom on Allambi Avenue sold three weeks ago for $1,840,000 against a campaign quote of $1.6m–$1.7m. Here is what the data says about that gap."* Then chart.
- **Test:** Does every suburb section open with a name-and-place specific?

## Section C: Trust architecture — page-level requirements

### C.1 Methodology disclosure
- **Where:** A standalone methodology chapter — minimum 3 pages, maximum 6 — placed *before* the appendix, not after. (Methodology is content; appendix is reference.)
- **Content:**
  - Data sources (Domain.com.au scrape, RP Data, council records, ABS, RBA, SQM Research, Fields' own validated cadastre, etc.)
  - Sampling rules (e.g. "houses only, deduplicated across three sources, taking max per quarter")
  - Indexed-price formula
  - Comparable-adjustment methodology
  - DOM definition (date listed → date sold; not date contract signed)
  - Exclusions ("we exclude properties listed and withdrawn within 14 days; we exclude family transfers under $200k")
  - Sample-size flags ("any quarter with fewer than 30 transactions for a suburb is flagged as small-sample")
- **Cite key academic underpinnings:** Abelson 2005 (income elasticity), Genesove & Mayer 2001 (loss aversion in seller behaviour), Loewenstein 1994 (curiosity gap) — only where the report uses them.

### C.2 Conditional language audit
- **Rule:** Every forward-looking sentence uses conditional language. Three permitted hedge levels:
  - **Strong:** "The data shows…"
  - **Medium:** "The data is consistent with…"
  - **Weak:** "The data does not rule out…"
- **Forbidden:** "Will", "going to", "set to", "is expected to", "predicted to" — unless quoting someone else, in which case it must be a direct quote with attribution.
- **Reviewer test:** Search the draft for "will", "going to", "set to". Each instance must be defensible.

### C.3 Named author
- **Rule:** Will Simpson is named on the cover, on the methodology page, on the email that distributes the report, and on the audio version.
- **Mechanic:**
  - Cover: small typographic byline beneath the title. Not a marketing photo.
  - Methodology page: 4×5cm photo, three-paragraph bio, one-line accountability statement.
  - Audio version: Will reads the introduction and the closing reflection in his own voice.
- **Why it matters:** KU 2024 study showed even "written by staff writer" was interpreted as AI-written and reduced trust. Anonymity is a tax.

### C.4 The "what we don't know" page
- **Where:** Late in the report, just before the methodology page.
- **Content:** A page titled "What this edition doesn't answer." Three to five honest items per quarter.
  - Sample-size limitations.
  - Data-source disagreements.
  - Where the photo-analysis ML may have erred.
  - Where the indexed-price calculation rests on assumptions (composition shifts).
  - One question we asked the data and couldn't answer.
- **Reviewer test:** Is at least one item in this page genuinely uncomfortable to publish?

### C.5 Contrarian findings
- **Rule:** At least one finding per quarter must contradict the local consensus.
- **Examples:**
  - "Despite the consensus that pools add value, the data on Burleigh Waters pool homes shows…"
  - "Days-on-market for unit listings in Robina has shortened 23% over six quarters even as house DOM has lengthened — the unit market is no longer following the house market."
  - "Schools as catchment premium: the data shows the premium for Hillcrest catchment is X% — substantially smaller than the local belief of Y%."
- **Reviewer test:** Read the report. Where is the section a local agent would push back on? If there isn't one, write one.

### C.6 "We got it wrong" — the long-term trust compound
- **Where:** Issue 2 onwards. Issue 1 has nothing to revise.
- **Mechanic:** A single page titled "What we said last quarter — and how it played out." Each prior conditional-language signal is checked against subsequent data. Note where the signal was confirmed, where it was disconfirmed, where it remains unresolved.
- **Why it works:** Cooley/Edelman 2025 — companies that openly admit getting things wrong build *more* trust than those that don't. This is the page that cements long-term subscriber relationships.

### C.7 Photographic and physical evidence
- **Rule:** Will-on-the-ground photography in every suburb section.
- **Specification:**
  - At least one *establishing shot* per suburb (street, landmark, characteristic building).
  - At least one *human-scale shot* — a sign, a notice, a hand holding a council overlay, a window listing card.
  - At least one *seasonal shot* per quarter to demonstrate currency.
  - All photos shot on Will's camera. No stock imagery.
- **Caption:** Date, street name, and one observation in Will's voice. Example: *"Allambi Avenue, 14 April 2026 — six listings on this stretch, three with offers above asking."*

### C.8 Citation density
- **Rule:** Minimum 1 citation per 3 pages of body content.
- **Format:** Footnote-style, numbered, collected at the end of the methodology page.
- **Allowed sources:** ABS, RBA, council, peer-reviewed academic, named industry surveys (REIA, REIQ, OpenAgent, NAR), CoreLogic, SQM. Each citation is hyperlinked in the digital version.
- **Forbidden:** Vague citations ("studies show"), unattributed statistics, "according to industry experts."

## Section D: Objection pre-empt — page placements

| Objection | Where it appears in the report | Form it takes |
|---|---|---|
| "How do you know if you've never sold a house?" | Methodology page + Will's bio | "The data is the credential, not the licence." Plus methodology depth. |
| "Why trust this over CoreLogic / Domain?" | Methodology page sidebar: "Where we differ from CoreLogic, Domain, RP Data" | Honest comparison. We're not better; we're different. CoreLogic has scale; we have local depth. |
| "Interesting, but how does it help me right now?" | Each section closes with "What to watch over the next 90 days" — three signals, no advice | Information, not advice. |
| "What's the catch?" | Page 2 "About this report" panel | Funding model declared: free for buyers, paid by sellers who want custom analysis. |
| "Are they going to sell my data?" | Email-capture page + privacy line in the footer | "Your email is used only to send the next edition. One-click unsubscribe. We do not sell your data." |
| "What's their angle?" | Same as "what's the catch" — handled by funding-model disclosure | Clear, plain language. |
| "Won't a free report be shallow?" | Length, density of footnotes, methodology page | Substance proves itself. No need to claim depth — it has to be visible. |
| "This is just last quarter's news" | Date stamp on every chart + commitment to refresh cadence on cover | "Data closed 30 April 2026. Next edition: 31 July 2026." |

## Section E: Conversion mechanics — page-by-page

### E.1 Information gap (Loewenstein 1994)
- **Where:** Cover headline + first paragraph + opening of each suburb section.
- **Sizing rule:** "Just enough that the reader feels a question they didn't have before opening the report." Not "Just enough that they feel stupid."
- **Anti-patterns:**
  - Too small: "Robina prices rose this quarter." (No gap.)
  - Too big: "The end of southern Gold Coast property as we know it." (Inflated, alienating.)
  - Right: "Sales in Burleigh Waters have halved. Prices haven't moved. Why?"

### E.2 Social proof — used carefully
- **Currently available:** None. Don't fake it.
- **As we accumulate:**
  - Issue 2: "Read by [N] subscribers across the Gold Coast" only if N > 500.
  - Issue 3+: Citations of the report by other outlets, with permission, with the citation exact.
  - Always: avoid testimonials that read as marketing.

### E.3 Scarcity (ethical only)
- **Permitted:**
  - "Data closed [date]; next edition [date]."
  - "This edition includes the new Burleigh Waters flood-overlay data for the first time."
- **Forbidden:**
  - "Limited time offer."
  - "Only X copies left."
  - "Last chance."

### E.4 Identity reinforcement
- **Rule:** The reader leaves the report having implicitly upgraded their self-image: "I'm the kind of person who reads the original data, not the marketing."
- **Mechanic:**
  - The opening lines establish that this report is for serious readers ("If you've ever wondered what's actually happening to property prices on the southern Gold Coast — not what an agent told you, not what a headline implied, but what 2,153 sales over the last 12 months can be made to say — this is for you.").
  - A small "for the curious" or "for the patient" framing on the cover, never "for the savvy" or any other vain framing.

### E.5 Low-friction next step
- **Primary CTA:** Subscribe to receive the next edition. Email-only capture, single field, no other questions.
- **Placement:** Page 2 (immediately after "About this report"), and once at the back. Two placements maximum. No sticky footer.
- **Secondary CTA (only on the back page):**
  - "Want this analysis applied to your home? Request a Position Report." (Seller funnel.)
  - "See live updates between editions." (Web layer.)
- **Prohibited:** Phone capture, postcode capture, intent capture, any form longer than one field on the primary CTA.

### E.6 Narrative scaffolding
- **Rule:** Every section follows the three-act pattern: Specific case → Data behind it → Pattern across the suburb / market.
- **Counter-pattern (forbidden):** Bullet-pointed key takeaways at the top, then unstructured data dump.

### E.7 Sutherland nudges
- **Rule:** Include one "irrational" small detail per edition that signals craft.
- **Examples for issue 1:**
  - A hand-drawn map of one street in each suburb, sketched by a local illustrator.
  - A "field notes" margin column in handwritten font, in Will's voice.
  - A subtle textured paper if printed.
  - A signed editor's note inside the cover (Will's signature, scanned).
- **Why:** The reader cannot articulate why they trust the report more after seeing these — but they do.

## Section F: Voice rules — quick reference

(Consolidates `feedback_no_advice_data_only`, `feedback_editorial_voice`, `feedback_factual_accuracy`, `feedback_no_valuation_in_headlines`, `feedback_editorial_value_framework`.)

| Rule | Pass | Fail |
|---|---|---|
| No advice | "If you are considering selling, the data you would want to weigh includes…" | "If you're thinking of selling, now is a good time…" |
| No predictions | "If listing volumes stay below the 5-year median, the conditions that have historically preceded growth would be in place." | "Prices are set to rise next quarter." |
| No single valuation in headlines | "$1.62m–$2.10m for four-bedroom houses (47 sales)." | "Median four-bedroom: $1,830,000." |
| Value framing | "Lower entry price, walking distance to beach." | "Small block size." |
| Forbidden words | (none of: stunning, nestled, boasting, rare opportunity, robust market, unprecedented, hot market, must-see, gem) | "Stunning beachside opportunity." |
| Number format | "$1,250,000" | "$1.25m" |
| Suburb capitalisation | "Burleigh Waters" | "burleigh waters" |
| Hedging strength | Match the data: strong / medium / weak. | Mismatched — claims certainty when data weak. |
| "Mum understands" test | Each paragraph readable without industry jargon. | "Hedonic-adjusted indexed median…" without translation. |

## Section G: Gold Coast context — chapter-level requirements

### G.1 Southern migration as the master narrative
- **Where:** Either the macro chapter or its own section.
- **Mechanic:** Quantify it. Settlement-postcode origin where data allows. % of buyers from interstate. Equity differential.
- **Caveat:** Conditional language only — "the data is consistent with sustained interstate-buyer pressure on prices in the southern corridor."

### G.2 Burleigh Waters flood section
- **Required content:**
  - Council overlay (City Plan).
  - ICA insurance probability zones.
  - The divergence between the two — quantified at the suburb level.
  - Data on actual flood events (2017, 2022).
  - Recommended next step: FloodWise report (third-party, not Fields).
- **Forbidden:**
  - "Underwater."
  - "Never flooded."
  - "Safe to ignore overlay."
  - "Council is wrong / insurers are wrong" — both are evidence; we report both.
- **Tone:** Calm, data-led, acknowledging the question is the #1 search query for the suburb without sensationalising it.

### G.3 The agent reputation problem
- **Where:** Page 2 "About this report" panel.
- **Mechanic:** One paragraph. "We don't list properties. We don't earn commission on sales. The data is the product." Fields' structural position is acknowledged once and not laboured.

### G.4 Retiree economics layer
- **Where:** Wherever life-stage segmentation is supported by data.
- **Mechanic:** Where the data allows, segment by typology — four-bed family vs three-bed downsizer vs apartment — instead of by suburb-aggregate. Make the segmentation explicit.

### G.5 School catchments
- **Where:** Robina + Varsity Lakes sections.
- **Mechanic:** Catchment-vs-non-catchment median analysis where Fields can publish it. Cite catchment overlay source. Sample sizes visible.
- **Why this is shareable:** Most national reports cannot do this.

### G.6 Three suburbs, three angles
| Suburb | Strongest narrative angle | Required chart |
|---|---|---|
| Robina (4226) | Master-planned, school-driven, units-vs-houses divergence | Unit-vs-house indexed-price chart, 2015-present |
| Burleigh Waters (4220) | Premium, tightly held, flood overlay overhang | Flood-overlay vs ICA-zone Venn diagram + price impact per overlay status |
| Varsity Lakes (4227) | Younger demographic, lake-fronting premium, growth | Lake-fronting vs non-lake-fronting price gap, 5-year |

## Section H: How a draft is reviewed

When a section is reviewed before publication, run it against this checklist:

1. **Information gap test (E.1):** Is the section opening a question the reader didn't know they had?
2. **Narrative transport test (B.9):** Does the section open with a specific, named, anonymised case?
3. **Anchoring test (B.3):** Is the biggest number on the page a *range*, not a point?
4. **Confirmation bias test (B.7):** Is there at least one finding that surprises the typical reader?
5. **Authority test (B.8):** Is authority demonstrated through methodology, not claimed?
6. **Voice test (F):** Are forbidden words absent? Are forecasts absent? Is advice absent?
7. **Vulnerability test (C.4):** Did the report acknowledge what it doesn't know in this section?
8. **Sample-size test:** Is every figure attached to a sample size?
9. **Source-of-truth test:** Does every figure trace back to `precomputed_market_charts` or `precomputed_indexed_prices`?
10. **Mum-understands test:** Could a non-specialist follow this paragraph?
11. **BBQ test:** Could the reader summarise this section in one sentence at a barbecue?
12. **Share test:** Would a homeowner forward this section to their partner?

If a section fails three or more, rewrite. If it fails the information gap test or the BBQ test specifically, rewrite from the lead.
