## 1. The single sentence

> The Fields Quarterly is the most trusted, most rigorous, and most beautifully made record of what the southern Gold Coast property market actually did, and why — written for the reader, not the agent.

If a single sentence has to do the work, that's it. Every section, chart, paragraph, and CTA must be defensible against that sentence.

## 2. Why this report exists

Fields is pre-revenue, sole-operator, and has until **Q3 2026** to demonstrate market interest or actual listings. The bottleneck isn't traffic — Facebook brings the traffic. The bottleneck is **conversion-grade trust at scale**. A two-minute reel can stop a scroll; a property page can hold attention for two minutes; but neither of those formats can carry the weight of "I would let this person represent the largest financial decision of my life."

A flagship quarterly report is the heaviest credibility instrument Fields can produce. It is the only format in which:

1. A reader gives Fields **40+ minutes** of uninterrupted attention.
2. **Methodology** can be shown, not asserted.
3. The same artefact can serve **buyers, sellers, and curious owners** without dilution.
4. Will Simpson can be **unmistakably named** as the author and analyst — fighting the "they don't even sell houses" objection head-on.
5. **Email capture** is justified by the value of what's being given away.
6. The reader becomes a **subscriber** — opening a relationship that Fields can extend across the next four quarters.
7. The same content **cascades into** YouTube, articles, Facebook, Instagram, and an in-person leave-behind without rewriting.

The report is the **trust artefact**. Everything else is the trust amplifier.

## 3. What this report is not

This is the corrective list. Each item is a temptation we must explicitly avoid.

- **Not a CoreLogic-style monthly index.** Index numbers without narrative are commodity. Anyone can subscribe to that.
- **Not a Domain quarterly summary.** Vendor-friendly, recency-biased, polite to a fault. Reads as press release.
- **Not a Knight Frank Wealth Report knock-off.** Knight Frank optimises for high-net-worth identity reinforcement; we are not selling identity, we are building decisions.
- **Not a Ray White / McGrath market wrap.** Their incentive is to pump confidence; readers know it; the medium is dead before it starts.
- **Not a Realtor's letterbox flyer.** The letterbox flyer says "we're amazing"; the Fields Quarterly says "here is what is true, traceable, and falsifiable."
- **Not a real-estate-marketing pamphlet wearing a serif font.** Substance has to be present, not implied by typography.
- **Not advice.** No "you should sell"; no "now is a good time"; no "buyers are well-placed." The data and the methodology speak; the reader decides.
- **Not a forecast.** Conditional language only. Indicators are reported; outcomes are not predicted.
- **Not a single-figure valuation document.** Headlines never carry one number; comparable ranges and methodology carry the credibility instead.
- **Not a static PDF.** The PDF is the artefact of record; the live-data layer extends and updates it.

## 4. The reader

Three reader archetypes share the report — but the report writes to **one composite reader** at a time.

### 4.1 The Curious Owner (volume: highest)
A homeowner in Robina, Burleigh Waters, or Varsity Lakes who has owned for 3-15 years. Watches the market the way other people watch the cricket. Has no immediate transaction intent — but is the most likely person to forward the report to a relative who *does*. Three things matter to them: **validation** ("did I make a smart purchase?"), **identity** ("I live in a place worth understanding"), and **gossip currency** ("did you see what the report said about Robina?").

### 4.2 The Active Seller (volume: lowest, value: highest)
0-12 months out from listing. Already terrified. They've been told contradictory numbers by three agents. They have read every "5 things you must know" listicle and absorbed nothing. The agent who already valued their home pitched too high; another pitched too low; they don't know who is honest. What they need is not more confidence — it is a **method**. They are looking for someone who will treat their decision like an analytical problem instead of a sales opportunity.

### 4.3 The Active Buyer (volume: medium)
Has 200K-1M to spend. Has watched four properties go to auction and overpay. Has lost sleep about overpaying for the next one. Has been mocked by the agent for asking about flood overlays. Wants someone to stand on their side of the table without explicitly being on their side. They are looking for **a frame** — a way to reason about what each property is actually worth.

### 4.4 The fourth, hidden archetype: The Influencer
Property investors, mortgage brokers, financial planners, accountants, and the occasional journalist. They do not transact through the report — but if it impresses them, they distribute it. **Optimise the methodology page for them.** A one-line citation in a journalist's article or an investor's blog post is worth thousands of dollars of paid distribution.

## 5. The two non-negotiable jobs of the report

Every section answers to one of these two jobs. If a section answers neither, cut it.

### Job 1: Make the reader feel they understand the market for the first time
This is the **insight job**. It's the moment in section 3 where the reader stops reading at a chart, sits back, and says "I had no idea — but now I get it." It's where Fields earns the right to speak.

Test: at the end of the report, can the reader explain to a friend, in one sentence, the one thing the southern Gold Coast did this quarter that they didn't expect? If yes — job done.

### Job 2: Make the reader feel that Fields is the place to come back to
This is the **trust job**. It's the moment the reader looks at the methodology, the citations, the named sources, the disclosed sample sizes, the photo of Will at his desk, and the quiet "what we don't know" footnote, and realises that the people behind this are nothing like the agents they have been dealing with.

Test: would the reader subscribe to next quarter's report, give Fields their email address for a live dashboard, and book a 15-minute call to learn about their own home's number? If yes — job done.

If both jobs are done, the report converts. Conversion isn't an extra section. It's the consequence of the two jobs being done.

## 6. The competitive frame — what wins

We are not competing on price (we charge nothing). We are not competing on data volume (CoreLogic has more). We are not competing on brand (Domain is everywhere). We are competing on **the quality of the conversation between the data and the reader**.

That quality has six axes. The Fields Quarterly should win on each.

| Axis | Most existing reports | Fields Quarterly |
|---|---|---|
| **Authorial voice** | Anonymous, corporate | Named (Will), human, conditional, willing to say "I don't know" |
| **Methodology disclosure** | Hidden or boilerplate | First-class — a chapter of its own |
| **Vulnerability** | Suppressed (vendor-friendly) | Foregrounded — at least one number per quarter that complicates the narrative |
| **Local depth** | National roll-up with a paragraph per region | Three suburbs, walked street by street |
| **Visual standard** | Branded, generic, low data-ink | Reuters-grade typographic charts; sparklines, small multiples |
| **Format respect** | PDF or web, never both | Print-grade PDF + interactive web layer + audio summary, all from one source |

A reader who has read both the Domain quarterly and the Fields Quarterly should think: *the Fields one is the one I would print*.

## 7. Cadence and series logic

The report is **quarterly**. Four issues per year. Each issue is **30-50 pages**. Each issue is anchored by **one tension** (a price-volume divergence, a milestone crossing, a suburb divergence, a speed paradox) and uses the data to dramatise that tension.

The series logic matters. By the fourth issue:
- The reader has seen the same charts updated with new bars — pattern recognition kicks in.
- "What changed" can be a section because there's prior issue to compare to.
- The reader who downloaded Q1 has been email-nurtured for nine months and is open to a deeper relationship.
- We have a credibility track record: "Q1 2026 said wages had begun to lead — Q3 2026 has the receipts."
- The CEO Agents (Engineering / Growth / Product) have a public artefact to point to as proof of the analytical operation.

In between quarterly issues, **Market Pulse** (monthly) and **Special Editions** (e.g. flood, supply pipeline) maintain the rhythm.

## 8. The "WOW moment" doctrine

Every issue must have **one** moment in the first 60 seconds of attention that produces the involuntary reaction "I have not seen this before, I need to know more." Without it, the report is one of many; with it, the report is *the one*.

Reliable WOW patterns:
- **A chart no one else publishes** (e.g. a sale-to-list ratio overlaid with photo-quality score; a 10-year suburb-vs-suburb capital growth derby; the actual asking-vs-actual gap quantified for the first time on the Gold Coast).
- **A number that contradicts the consensus** (e.g. "29 sales last quarter, the lowest in eight years — but DOM fell from 48 to 28 days").
- **A confession** (e.g. "Last quarter we said X. Here is what actually happened. Here is what we got wrong.").
- **A named person** (Will, by name, with a photo, saying something only an operator who has walked these streets could say).
- **A piece of original research** (e.g. "We analysed 922 sold properties and found Ray White Robina handles 17% of all transactions in the suburb — a level of concentration that is unique in southern Queensland").

Pick one per issue. Make sure it is the first thing on the cover, the first chart inside, and the headline of the email that distributes the report.

## 9. Conversion logic

The report converts in **four stages**, none of which look like "click here to book a call."

### Stage 1: Email-gated download (single field, no friction)
Reader gives an email to receive the PDF. The form is one input. The follow-up email contains the PDF as an attachment AND a link to the live web layer. **Do not** ask for phone number, postcode, or intent. Friction kills capture.

### Stage 2: Live web layer
Each chart in the PDF has a partner chart on `fieldsestate.com.au/quarterly/Q2-2026/{section}`. The web layer:
- Updates between quarters (so it has reason to be visited).
- Includes one **personalisation hook**: "Enter your address to see your suburb's chart."
- Includes one **next-step CTA per page** — but the CTA is *softer* than current Fields CTAs (a saved-search alert, a notification when the next quarter drops, "see the data layer"). No "call us" until Q4.

### Stage 3: Email nurture (12 sends across the quarter)
Reader gets one email per week between issues. Mix:
- "Methodology footnote we wanted to add to Q2": one technical lesson per week.
- "What's moving in Robina this week": one-paragraph data update.
- "The number we're watching": forward-looking signal post.
- One "answer to a question we got": signals listening, builds loop.

The nurture stream is what graduates a curious owner into an active seller over the 9-12 months they are in pre-listing.

### Stage 4: The seller-or-buyer offer
By the third or fourth quarterly issue, the reader has consumed 60+ pieces of Fields content. They are warm. The CTA at this point is no longer "subscribe." It is:
- **For the seller-leaning reader:** "Get your home's Position Report — the same methodology applied to your address, for free, no listing commitment." (This is the per-property report — already designed by external designer.)
- **For the buyer-leaning reader:** "Join 5 Property Friday — five hand-picked listings every Friday, with our judgement on each. Free." (Buyer Assist product per Daniel feedback.)

The quarterly report does not sell either of these. It earns the right for them to be offered without resistance.

## 11. The success criteria

The report works if (12 months from launch):

1. **Email subscribers**: 1,500+ verified, actively-opening subscribers across the three core suburb lists.
2. **Per-issue downloads**: 4,000+ unique downloads of each issue.
3. **Editorial citations**: at least one citation per quarter in a non-Fields outlet (local news, broker blog, podcast).
4. **Inbound enquiries**: 30+ pre-listing or buyer-product enquiries trace their attribution back to the quarterly within the first 12 months.
5. **Search visibility**: the report ranks for "Burleigh Waters market report", "Robina property report", "Varsity Lakes data" — original organic traffic.
6. **Internal use**: every Fields Facebook post, YouTube video, and per-property report from Q3 2026 onward references the most recent issue. The quarterly becomes the editorial spine of the brand.
7. **Industry use**: at least one mortgage broker, financial planner, accountant, or buyer's agent on the Gold Coast distributes the report to their own client base voluntarily.

If those seven things happen, the report has done what it was designed to do — and the runway question for Q4 2026 has been answered before it was asked.

## 12. Open questions for Will

These are decisions only Will can make.

1. **Should the first issue be Q2 2026 (June) or Q3 2026 (September)?** Q2 ships sooner but compresses the design + data work; Q3 buys time but loses a quarter of compounding.
2. **Photo of Will, name of Will, voice of Will — how visible?** Strong recommendation: maximally. Daniel's "no author credibility signal" feedback is direct evidence. But this is a personal call.
3. **Who designs the cover and typography?** Either reuse the per-property report designer (already engaged), or commission a separate small-house design studio for editorial-grade typography. The per-property designer is closer to brand-marketing; an editorial designer (think Monocle, Kinfolk, FT Weekend) is closer to journalism. Recommend the editorial route, but it costs more.
4. **Should the report be physically printed and posted to high-net-worth households in the three core suburbs?** A modest run (200 copies of issue 1) is a startlingly cheap and high-conversion lead-generation tactic. Letterbox-drop the prime streets in each suburb. The unit economics are strong if a single seller converts.
5. **What is the headline of issue 1?** This is the most important single decision. It defines the brand for the next year. The recommended frame: *"The southern Gold Coast in three movements."* But this is to be debated.
