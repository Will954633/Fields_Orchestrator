## Important honesty note — this is NOT yet the case study Will described
Will's framing was "why you should not spend big on renovations." **I can't make that specific claim
from available data** — I don't know what was actually spent on the renovation, and without that
figure there's no way to say whether the ~$884K uplift represents a good or poor return on the work.
A ~91.6% gain in 19 months is far larger than typical Gold Coast market movement over that window
would explain on its own, so the renovation clearly did SOMETHING significant — but "something
significant" isn't the same as "not worth it."

**Recommended framing instead (and it fits the no-advice editorial rule better anyway):** present the
verified before/after fact — unrenovated sale price, extensive renovation, current ask — as a single
compelling data point, and let the reader draw their own conclusion about renovation ROI, the same way
the site's actual best-performing ad ("Who buys a home for $1,550,000 and sells it eighteen months
later for $3,465,000?") does. That ad doesn't assert whether the flip was "worth it" either — it just
presents the number and lets the gap do the work. Asserting "don't over-renovate" as a conclusion
would itself be advice, which the editorial rules already prohibit regardless of what the reno cost.

**If Will has the actual renovation cost** (from local knowledge, the agent, or the owner), that
changes the story materially and is worth adding — a real cost-vs-uplift comparison would be a much
stronger, more specific case study than the bare before/after. Flagging this rather than guessing a
renovation-cost figure.
